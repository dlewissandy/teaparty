# Knock-Knock Joke History: Research Findings

Research conducted: 2026-03-14
Sources: Web searches + direct page fetches. Honesty notes included per finding.

---

## 1. Earliest Dateable Examples / First Appearance in Print or on Radio

### Pre-modern antecedents (literary, not the joke format)

- **1597-1598 — Ben Jonson, *The Case is Altered***: A character named Juniper knocks on a door, Rachel asks "Who's there?", and Juniper delivers a punchline with a double meaning. A Library of Congress folklife blog (Stephen Winick, April 2024) argues this has a stronger structural claim to originating the knock-knock format than Shakespeare because it features interactive two-person dialogue with an actual punchline. However, there is no documented line of descent from this to the 1930s joke cycle.
  - Source: https://blogs.loc.gov/folklife/2024/04/knock-knock-whos-there-metafolklore-jokes-and-shakespeare/ (OPEN)

- **1606 — Shakespeare, *Macbeth*, Act 2, Scene 3**: The drunken porter mutters "Knock, knock! Who's there, i' the name of Beelzebub?" This is widely cited in popular articles but is a monologue, not a joke exchange. The LOC folklife post calls the Shakespeare attribution "metafolklore" — a popular myth about cultural origins. Not a direct ancestor of the 1930s format.
  - Source: https://blogs.loc.gov/folklife/2024/04/knock-knock-whos-there-metafolklore-jokes-and-shakespeare/ (OPEN)

### Precursor joke format: "Do You Know" / call-and-response riddles (early 1900s)

Before "knock-knock" jokes, a structurally similar format called "Do You Know" jokes was circulating in British and American oral tradition. Example: "Do you know Arthur?" — "Arthur who?" — "Arthurmometer!" The NPR History Dept. piece (2015) mentions a May 26, 1922, column in the Oakland *Tribune* by Merely McEvoy discussing this type of joke cycle returning in waves.
- Source: https://www.npr.org/sections/npr-history-dept/2015/03/03/389865887/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes (OPEN)
- Source (WGBH mirror): https://www.wgbh.org/news/2015-03-03/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes (OPEN)

### Henry Bett, *The Games of Children* (1929)

Multiple sources cite this book as containing the first printed description of the knock-knock *structure*, embedded in a children's game called "Buff," where one child bangs a stick and says "Knock, knock!" and another replies "Who's there?" / "Buff." / "What says Buff?" etc. This is documented as a children's game, not yet a pun-joke format.
- Sources: Mental Floss https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/55962/8-moments-knock-knock-joke-history (OPEN); Grokipedia https://grokipedia.com/page/Knock-knock_joke (OPEN)
- CAVEAT: No source I could access independently verified the Bett book text directly. This claim is asserted by secondary sources without quotation. UNCERTAIN as to exact wording.

### 1934 — First documented knock-knock joke in a newspaper column

Mental Floss and several secondary sources state that a newspaper columnist published this joke in 1934, calling it "the knock-knock joke's first published appearance in popular culture":

> Knock knock. Who's there? Rufus. Rufus who? Rufus the most important part of your house.

- Source: https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/55962/8-moments-knock-knock-joke-history (OPEN)
- CAVEAT: The specific newspaper name and date are NOT given in any source I could access. The claim is widely repeated across secondary sources but the primary newspaper source is not identified. UNCERTAIN / UNVERIFIED at the primary level.

### Radio: WKBO Harrisburg, Pennsylvania (June 1936)

The NPR piece and its PBS/WGBH/KCUR mirrors report that WKBO radio in Harrisburg, PA, broadcast knock-knock jokes throughout the day in June 1936, capitalizing on Col. Frank Knox's selection as Alf Landon's running mate. The Harrisburg *Telegraph* (June 17, 1936) is cited as crediting Knox's candidacy with sparking the radio-driven surge.
- Source: https://www.wgbh.org/news/2015-03-03/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes (OPEN, NPR mirror)
- Note: The Harrisburg *Telegraph* itself would be in a historical newspaper archive. It is not freely accessible online.

### July 25, 1936 — Radio column, *News Herald* (Franklin, PA)

Jean Mackenzie, a radio-listening columnist, wrote: "You can't turn the radio on anymore without getting one of the Knock-Knock gags." Cited in the NPR/WGBH piece.
- Source: https://www.wgbh.org/news/2015-03-03/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes (OPEN)
- The *News Herald* original is in historical archives, not freely accessible.

## 2. The 1930s US Craze: Newspaper Coverage, Radio, Parlor-Game Spread

### Peak year: 1936

By the consensus of all sources, 1936 was the year the craze exploded. One secondary source (via search result summary) cites a figure from the Newspapers.com database claiming 6,110 American newspapers ran knock-knock jokes in 1936 — more than all prior years combined. I could not verify this figure directly; it appeared only in a search result summary, not in a page I fetched. UNCERTAIN.

### Associated Press article, August 3, 1936

The *Titusville Herald* (Titusville, PA) published an AP wire story titled **"'Knock Knock' Latest Nutsy Game for Parlor Amusement"** on August 3, 1936. The article reported that "What's This?" had given way to "Knock, Knock!" as America's favorite parlor game, comparing its sudden rise to that of the novelty song "The Music Goes 'Round and Around." This is the most specific, named newspaper citation across all sources.
- Cited in: https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/55962/8-moments-knock-knock-joke-history (OPEN)
- Primary source (Titusville Herald): Historical archive, not freely accessible online.

### Other 1936 newspaper coverage cited

All from the NPR/WGBH piece; primary sources are in historical archives:
- **Harrisburg *Telegraph***, June 17, 1936: linked the craze to Frank Knox's VP candidacy
- **Delaware County Times**: Edgmont Cash & Carry grocery ad using knock-knock jokes, August 1936
- **Kerrville Times** (Texas), August 1936: described the phenomenon as "a game" with mysterious origins
- **Altoona Tribune**, **Reading Times**, **Delaware County Daily Times**: mentioned as carrying coverage

### Frank Knox political angle (1936 presidential election)

WKBO Harrisburg and several papers used knock-knock jokes riffing on "Knox" / "knock": e.g., "Knock knock — Who's there? — Frank Knox." The Republican vice-presidential candidate's name was a natural fit. This political hook is credited by multiple sources as an accelerant of the radio craze in mid-1936.
- Source: https://www.wgbh.org/news/2015-03-03/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes (OPEN)

### Knock-knock clubs in the Midwest

Wikipedia's article on knock-knock jokes (blocked with 403 on direct fetch) is summarized in search results as stating that "knock-knock clubs formed in the Midwestern United States." No specific city, club name, or primary source is given in any source I could access. UNVERIFIED at the primary level.

### Fred Allen radio broadcast, December 30, 1936

Humorist and radio host Fred Allen produced a year-end wrap-up on December 30, 1936, featuring a mock interview with a fictional character named "Ramrod Dank," described as "the first man to coin a knock knock." This was clearly a satirical bit, not a genuine attribution — Allen was lampooning the craze by inventing a fictional inventor.
- Cited in: https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/55962/8-moments-knock-knock-joke-history (OPEN)
- A wrestling/entertainment blog also references this segment: http://wrestlingvsentertainment.blogspot.com/2013/05/act-06-scene-05-year-2013-ramrod-dank.html (OPEN, secondary)

### Vincent Lopez and the "Knock Knock Song"

Swing bandleader **Vincent Lopez** recorded **"Knock, Knock, Who's There?"** in 1936 as a novelty audience-participation song. It was co-written by Lopez, Bill Davies, Johnny Morris, and Jimmy Tyson, and released as a 78 RPM shellac record. Fletcher Henderson's orchestra also recorded a version. The song was an audience call-and-response format.
- Discogs record: https://www.discogs.com/release/15876631-Vincent-Lopez-And-His-Orchestra-Knock-Knock-Whos-There-Sing-Sing-Sing-With-A-Swing (OPEN)
- Internet Archive (audio): https://archive.org/details/78_knock-knock-whos-there_vincent-lopez-and-his-orchestra-johnny-morris-tyson-davi_gbia0075458a (OPEN)
- In his later autobiography *Lopez Speaking* (1960s), Lopez reportedly reflected on the craze. UNCERTAIN: search results mention this book but I could not fetch the text.

### Bob Dunn comic book, 1936

Cartoonist **Bob Dunn** published *Knock Knock: Featuring Enoch Knox* in 1936 through Whitman/Western Publishing (later also Dell; "Knock Knock Who's There" #801 appears in Heritage Auctions records). The book contained over 50 single-panel gag strips, each structured as a knock-knock joke, and reportedly sold millions of copies.

Many popular accounts credited Dunn with *inventing* the knock-knock joke. A CBR (Comic Book Resources) "Comic Legends" piece explicitly investigated this claim and rated it **False**, concluding that knock-knock jokes existed in print by 1934, two years before Dunn's book, and that Dunn was "just at the right place at the right time."
- CBR investigation: https://www.cbr.com/knock-knock-joke-comic-bob-dunn/ (OPEN)
- Heritage Auctions listing (confirms 1936 Dell publication): https://comics.ha.com/itm/magazines/humor/knock-knock-who-s-there-801-dell-1936-condition-fn/a/121610-11799.s (OPEN)

### Critical/academic response (1936)

**D.A. Laird**, director of the Rivercrest Psychological Laboratory at Colgate University, published criticism near the end of 1936 comparing the knock-knock joke obsession to *Witzelsucht* — a neurological term for compulsive joke-telling. The joke format was being taken seriously as a social phenomenon worth psychological analysis.
- Cited in: https://www.wgbh.org/news/2015-03-03/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes (OPEN)

## 4. Credit for Invention or Popularization

### No confirmed inventor

No source I could access identifies a single person who invented the knock-knock joke format. The consensus across reputable sources (NPR, Mental Floss, CBR, LOC Folklife) is that the format emerged organically from earlier "Do You Know" riddle traditions and the children's "Buff" game.

### People commonly associated with the craze

| Person | Role | Claim |
|---|---|---|
| Bob Dunn | Cartoonist | Published bestselling 1936 joke book; sometimes credited with inventing the format. CBR research rates this claim FALSE. |
| Vincent Lopez | Bandleader | Wrote and recorded the "Knock, Knock, Who's There?" novelty song (1936). |
| Frank Knox | Republican VP candidate, 1936 | His name accidentally fueled the WKBO/Harrisburg radio angle. Not a jokester himself. |
| Fred Allen | Radio comedian | Satirized the craze in December 1936 by inventing fictional inventor "Ramrod Dank." |
| "Merely McEvoy" | Columnist | Documented the precursor "Do You Know" joke format in the Oakland Tribune, 1922. |

### What can be said with confidence

1. The format was circulating in the US by at least 1934 (first documented newspaper example), possibly earlier in oral tradition.
2. It exploded into a national fad in 1936, driven by radio (especially the Knox/WKBO political angle), swing music (Lopez, Fletcher Henderson), and commercial publishing (Bob Dunn's book).
3. No individual is credibly identified as the inventor. Bob Dunn is the most commonly named popularizer, but that attribution is contested.
4. Fred Allen's satirical "Ramrod Dank" bit (December 30, 1936) shows that contemporaries found the question of origin absurd enough to mock.
