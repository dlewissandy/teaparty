## SECTION 1: PSYCHOLINGUISTICS OF HOMOPHONES AND PHONETIC AMBIGUITY

---

**SOURCE 1**

**Citation:** Giora, R. (1997). Understanding figurative and literal language: The graded salience hypothesis. *Cognitive Linguistics*, 7, 183–206.

**URL:** https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Understanding-figurative-and-literal-language:-The-Giora/a6fc9f4c8d7cb1ba69d6765f094b039ee76e39e9 (abstract/index only)

**Paywalled:** Yes (original journal article behind paywall; ResearchGate and Academia.edu have PDFs of uncertain legitimacy)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — original statement of the Graded Salience Hypothesis.

**Relevance:** The foundational theoretical paper for understanding how the brain processes words with multiple meanings, including homophones. The Graded Salience Hypothesis holds that more salient meanings — those that are more frequent, familiar, conventional, or prototypical — are accessed faster and reach activation threshold before less salient ones, regardless of contextual support. This directly predicts the mechanism of the knock-knock pun: the listener's mental lexicon activates the salient meaning of the name-word first (e.g., "Olive" as a food item), and the less salient meaning ("I'll have") is retrieved only after the punchline forces reanalysis. The hypothesis also explains why puns conventionally spell out or signal the less salient reading — the salient meaning activates automatically and requires no support.

**Relevance:** Extends the 1997 article into a full theoretical framework. Includes treatment of homonyms, reaction-time experiments, and how salience interacts with figurative language and wordplay. The book explicitly notes that "puns tend to spell out the less salient meaning of ambiguous words or expressions, trusting the lexical processor to activate the more salient meaning on its own accord."

**SOURCE 3**

**Citation:** Zwitserlood, P. (1989). The locus of the effects of sentential-semantic context in spoken-word processing. *Cognition*, 32, 25–64.

**URL:** Not freely available; indexed at Semantic Scholar: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Accessing-Spoken-Words:-The-Importance-of-Word-Marslen-Wilson-Zwitserlood/c7ad5b8aca9e01109250fdb40f7907c58c7ff6aa

**Paywalled:** Yes (ScienceDirect paywall for original)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — foundational experimental psycholinguistics.

**Relevance:** Established via cross-modal semantic priming (using Dutch materials) that multiple lexical candidates are co-activated during spoken word recognition until sufficient phonetic information disambiguates them. The stem "kapit-" primed semantic associates of both "kapitein" (captain) and "kapitaal" (capital) simultaneously. This is the empirical foundation for understanding homophone co-activation: in a knock-knock joke heard aloud, the name-word activates multiple lexical entries in parallel until the punchline resolves the ambiguity. Directly relevant to the knock-knock mechanism, where the name sounds exactly like a phrase and both interpretations are briefly co-active.

**Citation:** Rose, S. B., Spalek, K., & Abdel Rahman, R. (2015). Pun exposure induces co-activation of phonologically related word meanings in language production: Evidence from picture–word interference. *PLoS ONE*, 10(7), e0130853. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0130853

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4482729/ (freely available via PubMed Central)

**Paywalled:** No

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — experimental psycholinguistics, picture-word interference paradigm.

**Relevance:** Demonstrated experimentally that exposure to puns induces an "ambiguity processing mode" that enhances co-activation of phonologically related alternate word meanings during speech production. Participants primed with puns showed facilitation for distractor words semantically related to the non-depicted homophone meaning of target pictures — an effect absent in the control group primed with non-pun jokes. Directly relevant to pun and knock-knock processing: hearing the setup of a knock-knock joke may prime the listener into exactly this co-activation mode.

## SECTION 2: PUN PROCESSING — NEURAL AND COGNITIVE RESEARCH

---

**SOURCE 6**

**Citation:** Mayerhofer, B., & Schacht, A. (2015). From incoherence to mirth: Neuro-cognitive processing of garden-path jokes. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 6, 550. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2015.00550

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4429229/ (freely available via PubMed Central); also https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2015.00550/full

**Paywalled:** No

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — ERP and behavioral study.

**Relevance:** Tested 48 garden-path jokes across three conditions (coherent, joke, incoherent endings) measuring reading times, ERPs, and pupil diameter. Key findings: joke endings produced enhanced N400 (250–500 ms) relative to coherent text, reflecting semantic integration difficulty; reduced N400 for jokes versus incoherent text suggests early activation of an alternative hidden interpretation. Late positivities (700–1000 ms) correlated with humor appreciation. Pupil dilation correlated specifically with humor ratings. Proposes a three-stage model: (1) violation detection, (2) semantic revision, (3) mirth. Although this study addresses semantic/pragmatic ambiguity rather than phonetic ambiguity specifically, the framework directly parallels the knock-knock joke mechanism, where the punchline forces reanalysis of the name-word as a phrase.

**SOURCE 8**

**Citation:** Zhao, J., et al. (2022). The resolution stage, not the incongruity detection stage, is related to the subjective feeling of humor: An ERP study using Japanese nazokake puns. *Brain and Cognition*. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bandc.2022.105790

**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S000689932200004X

**Paywalled:** Yes (ScienceDirect; 403 returned on fetch)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — ERP study on pun-specific processing.

**Relevance:** Used Japanese riddle-puns (nazokake) to test whether humor experience is generated at incongruity detection (N400) or at resolution (P600). Found that P600 amplitude — not N400 — correlated with subjective funniness ratings. Funny puns enlarged P600 compared to unfunny puns; N400 did not differ by funniness. The resolution stage is therefore where the subjective humor experience is generated. Highly relevant: for knock-knock jokes, this places the humor experience specifically at the moment of phonetic reanalysis (the punchline), not at the initial surprise of hearing the unexpected name.

**SOURCE 9**

**Citation:** Feng, Y., et al. (2023). Frame-shifting instead of incongruity is necessary for pun comprehension: Evidence from an ERP study on Chinese homophone puns. *Language, Cognition and Neuroscience*, 38(8). https://doi.org/10.1080/23273798.2023.2192509

**Paywalled:** Partial (Taylor & Francis; some institutional open access)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — ERP study specifically on homophone puns.

**Relevance:** Directly addresses homophone pun comprehension in Chinese. This is the most directly relevant neurolinguistic study to the knock-knock mechanism: the joke name ("Olive") activates one frame (proper noun, food), and the punchline triggers a frame-shift to the phonetically identical phrase ("I'll have"). The study confirms that this shift, not mere surprise, is what constitutes pun comprehension.

## SECTION 3: RELEVANCE THEORY AND JOKES

---

**SOURCE 17**

**Citation:** Sperber, D., & Wilson, D. (1986/1995). *Relevance: Communication and Cognition* (2nd ed.). Blackwell/Harvard University Press.

**URL:** https://philpapers.org/rec/SPER (index); PDF excerpt at https://danielwharris.com/teaching/spring16/readings/SperberWilsonRelevance.pdf

**Paywalled:** No for PDF excerpt (partial); book itself commercially available

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — foundational monograph.

**Relevance:** The origin of Relevance Theory. The core principle — that utterances are interpreted by selecting the first interpretation that provides an optimal balance of cognitive effects and processing effort — is the framework within which humor has been analyzed. The "echoic interpretation" concept (an utterance that echoes another to express an attitude toward it) has been applied to irony and humor. Puns have been analyzed as cases where the communicator exploits the hearer's search for relevance to produce a double cognitive effect. The knock-knock structure — where the setup name is later echoed in a new propositional form — fits naturally within echoic interpretation. Note: the 1986 edition does not extensively treat puns; this application was developed by subsequent scholars (principally Yus).

**SOURCE 18**

**Citation:** Yus, F. (2003). Humor and the search for relevance. *Journal of Pragmatics*, 35(9), 1295–1331. https://doi.org/10.1016/S0378-2166(02)00179-0

**Paywalled:** Yes (ScienceDirect; 403 returned on direct fetch)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — journal article explicitly applying Relevance Theory to humor.

**Relevance:** One of the central papers applying Relevance Theory to humor comprehension. **Citation:** Attardo, S., & Raskin, V. (1991). Script theory revisited: Joke similarity and joke representation model. *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research*, 4(3–4), 293–347.

The GTVH is the standard formal framework for classifying knock-knock joke mechanisms.

**SOURCE 27**

**Citation:** Palmann, A., & Miller, T. (2025). What's in a pun? Assessing the relationship between phonological distance and perceived funniness of punning jokes. *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research*, 38(4), 643–658. https://doi.org/10.1515/humor-2024-0060

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12487772/ (freely available via PubMed Central); also https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/humor-2024-0060/html

**Paywalled:** No (PubMed Central open access)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — quantitative empirical study.

**Relevance:** Quantitatively tested whether phonological similarity between a pun word and its target predicts perceived funniness, using 2,718 annotated puns with crowdsourced funniness ratings and four phonological distance metrics. Found significant negative correlations for two metrics (ALINE distance: ρ = −0.165, p = 0.0003; Levenshtein distance: ρ = −0.089, p = 0.021): the more phonologically similar the pun and its target, the funnier the joke is rated. Homographic puns (identical spelling) rated significantly funnier (mean = 0.60) than heterographic puns (mean = 0.52). Important negative finding for knock-knock research specifically: the study did not examine knock-knock jokes; all stimuli were self-contained written jokes, not the call-and-response format. Despite this, the finding directly supports the assumption underlying knock-knock joke construction — using names that are maximally phonologically similar to the intended phrase is not just convention, it is what makes the joke funnier.

**SOURCE A — Incongruity-Resolution Theory (Foundational)**

**Citation:** Suls, Jerry M. "A Two-Stage Model for the Appreciation of Jokes and Cartoons: An Information-Processing Analysis." In J. H. Goldstein and P. E. McGhee (eds.), *The Psychology of Humor: Theoretical Perspectives and Empirical Issues.* New York: Academic Press, 1972, pp. 81–100. DOI: 10.1016/B978-0-12-288950-9.50010-9

**URL:** https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Chapter-4-%E2%80%93-A-Two-Stage-Model-for-the-Appreciation-Suls/d1bafaae8d26224d61ae892c8ae600061db7399e (Semantic Scholar index; chapter paywalled)

**Paywalled:** Yes

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — foundational book chapter.

**Relevance:** The most influential early formalization of incongruity-resolution humor theory. Stage 1: setup creates an expectation; punchline violates it (incongruity). Stage 2: listener searches for a retrospective rule or frame that makes the punchline sensible (resolution). Humor requires *both* stages — incongruity alone produces puzzlement. Precursor to the GTVH's Script Opposition + Logical Mechanism dyad. Maps directly onto the knock-knock structure: the name creates a proper-noun expectation; the punchline forces phonetic reanalysis (the resolution stage). Confirmed via Semantic Scholar abstract and secondary descriptions in Ritchie (1999) and oalib.com.

**SOURCE B — Computational Model of Pun Funniness**

**Citation:** Kao, Justine, Roger Levy, and Noah D. Goodman. "A Computational Model of Linguistic Humor in Puns." *Cognitive Science* 40, no. 5 (2016): 1270–1300. DOI: 10.1111/cogs.12269

**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5042108/ (freely available via PubMed Central); preprint at https://cocolab.stanford.edu/papers/KaoEtAl2015-CognitiveScience.pdf

**Paywalled:** No (PMC open access)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — peer-reviewed; quantitative predictions correlated with human funniness ratings.

**Relevance:** Proposes two information-theoretic measures predicting pun funniness: (1) **Ambiguity** — entropy over interpretation distribution (high = both meanings equally likely); (2) **Distinctiveness** — semantic distance between the two meanings (KL divergence). Both measures correlate significantly with crowdsourced funniness ratings. Implication for knock-knock jokes: the best names have high ambiguity (name-reading and phrase-reading both plausible in context) and high distinctiveness ("Olive" as food vs. "I'll have" as grammatical phrase). Content confirmed via PMC full text and Wiley Online Library abstract.

**SOURCE C — Why Languages Tolerate Homophones**

**Citation:** Trott, Sean, and Benjamin K. Bergen. "Why Do Human Languages Have Homophones?" *Cognition* 205 (2020): Article 104449. DOI: 10.1016/j.cognition.2020.104449

**URL:** https://pages.ucsd.edu/~bkbergen/papers/trott_bergen_2020.pdf (freely available; author-hosted PDF)

**Paywalled:** No

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — computational corpus study, five languages.

**Relevance:** Analyzed homophony rates in English, German, Dutch, French, and Japanese (~3% Dutch to ~15% Japanese) and demonstrated that human contextual disambiguation capacity creates tolerance for homophones — communicative pressure does not select against them. Background for understanding why the English lexicon is rich enough in homophones to sustain a joke genre built on the mechanism. Content confirmed via author-hosted PDF.

**SOURCE D — Homophone Pronunciation Differences in Spontaneous Speech**

**Citation:** Gahl, Susanne. "Time and Thyme Are not Homophones: The Effect of Lemma Frequency on Word Durations in Spontaneous Speech." *Language* 84, no. 3 (2008): 474–496. DOI: 10.1353/lan.0.0035

**URL:** https://linguistics.berkeley.edu/~gahl/HomophonesLanguage.pdf (freely available; author-hosted PDF); also https://escholarship.org/uc/item/12g3x6pr

**Paywalled:** No (author-hosted)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — corpus study; ~90,000 homophone tokens from Switchboard corpus.

**Relevance:** Demonstrated that English homophones are *not* acoustically identical in spontaneous speech: higher-frequency words (e.g., "time") are significantly shorter in duration than lower-frequency homophones (e.g., "thyme"), even after controlling for speaking rate, predictability, and syntactic category. Implication for knock-knock jokes: the name-word and the target phrase are probably not perfectly acoustically identical in performance — the joke works despite slight phonetic differences because listeners process phonetic similarity probabilistically. This connects to Palmann & Miller 2025 (Source 27 above): the threshold for phonological similarity that triggers pun recognition is proximity, not strict identity. Content confirmed via author-hosted PDF and Cambridge Core abstract.

**SOURCE E — Spoken Word Recognition and Lexical Frequency Competition**

**Citation:** Dahan, Delphine, James S. Magnuson, and Michael K. Tanenhaus. "Time Course of Frequency Effects in Spoken-Word Recognition: Evidence from Eye Movements." *Cognitive Psychology* 42, no. 4 (2001): 317–367. DOI: 10.1006/cogp.2000.0744

**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11368527/ (PubMed abstract; full text paywalled at Elsevier)

**Paywalled:** Yes (Elsevier)

**Primary/Secondary:** Primary — eye-tracking experiment; visual world paradigm.

**Relevance:** Demonstrated that high-frequency lexical candidates are fixated earlier than low-frequency candidates during spoken word recognition (visual world paradigm). Lexical frequency modulates the time course of phonological competition. Background support for why the "name" reading of a knock-knock word wins the initial recognition race — it is a common English word — before the phrase reading activates at the punchline. Content confirmed via PubMed abstract and Cognitive Psychology journal listing.

**Citation:** Attardo, Salvatore, Christian F. Hempelmann, and Sara Di Maio. "Script Oppositions and Logical Mechanisms: Modeling Incongruities and Their Resolutions." *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research* 15, no. 1 (2002): 3–46.

**URL:** https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Script-oppositions-and-logical-mechanisms:-Modeling-Attardo-Hempelmann/3111fa20eb73361a09cf5c79acc16640a5dae20f9 (Semantic Scholar index; journal paywalled via De Gruyter)
