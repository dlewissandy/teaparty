## 1. RACI Framework — Origin and Earliest Citable Source

### Verdict: PRACTITIONER FRAMEWORK — No founding academic paper exists.

RACI is a management consulting/practitioner tool. It has **no single founding peer-reviewed academic paper** and cannot be traced to a single inventor.

**Historical origin:**
- Roots in the **Linear Responsibility Chart (LRC)**, used in systems engineering and project management beginning in the 1950s–60s. NASA used a "Responsibility Assignment Matrix" (RAM) by the 1960s; the acronym "RAM" appeared in Gale's *New Acronyms, Initialisms, and Abbreviations* (1977) attributed to NASA.
- The LRC concept is associated with project management scholars including **David I. Cleland** (University of Pittsburgh), who published extensively on it (e.g., Cleland, D.I. (Ed.) *Project Management: Strategic Design and Implementation*, McGraw-Hill; multiple editions from 1988 onward).
- RACI as specifically named (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) emerged in management consulting practice in the **1970s–1980s**, attributed variously to James R. Robinson, Ernst & Young, and DuPont's "Responsibility Assignment Matrix." No single peer-reviewed paper introduces the RACI acronym.
- **PMBOK codified it:** PMI. (2017). *A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK Guide)* (6th ed.). Project Management Institute. ⚠️ **Practitioner reference, not peer-reviewed.**

**Connection to Galbraith (1973):**
- Galbraith's *Designing Complex Organizations* (1973, Addison-Wesley) is about **information processing and lateral coordination mechanisms** as responses to task uncertainty. RACI frameworks are *consistent with* Galbraith's logic (structural coordination to reduce uncertainty) and are applied *within* his Star Model / McKinsey 7S framework, but he **did not originate RACI**. Citing Galbraith to ground RACI theoretically is legitimate; citing him as RACI's source is not.
- **Best academic citation for RACI's theoretical grounding:** Galbraith, J.R. (1973). *Designing Complex Organizations*. Addison-Wesley. ✅ Academic press monograph, widely cited in organization theory.

**Best strategy for an org psych reviewer:**
- Frame RACI as a *practitioner instantiation* of role-responsibility assignment theory, anchored academically to:
  1. **Role theory** (Katz & Kahn, 1978; Rizzo et al., 1970)
  2. **Decision rights theory** (Jensen & Meckling, 1992)
  3. **Galbraith (1973)** on structural coordination
- Cite RACI itself with: PMI (2017) as practitioner source, flagged explicitly as such.

## 2. Role Ambiguity Academic Anchor

### Rizzo, House & Lirtzman (1970) — VERIFIED ✅

**Full citation:**
> Rizzo, J.R., House, R.J., & Lirtzman, S.I. (1970). Role conflict and ambiguity in complex organizations. *Administrative Science Quarterly*, *15*(2), 150–163.

**Details:**
- Journal: *Administrative Science Quarterly* (ASQ) ✅ Top-tier peer-reviewed management journal
- Volume: 15
- Issue: 2 (June 1970)
- Pages: 150–163

**Key findings:**
- Developed and validated questionnaire measures of role conflict and ambiguity as **factorially distinct constructs**
- Role ambiguity and role conflict correlated with: lower satisfaction, higher anxiety, and greater propensity to leave
- Established that **ambiguous role expectations are associated with negative organizational and individual outcomes** — the standard academic anchor for role ambiguity → performance degradation
- These scales became foundational and have been used in hundreds of subsequent studies

**Note on the specific claim:** Rizzo et al. establish the role ambiguity → dissatisfaction/anxiety link, not coordination failure per se. For the "coordination breakdown" framing, pairing with Galbraith (1973) and/or Van de Ven et al. (1976) on coordination modes would strengthen the claim.

## 3. Decision Rights / Ambiguous Ownership Research

### Jensen & Meckling (1992) — VERIFIED ✅

**Full citation (primary):**
> Jensen, M.C., & Meckling, W.H. (1992). Specific and general knowledge and organizational structure. In L. Werin & H. Wijkander (Eds.), *Contract Economics* (pp. 251–274). Blackwell.

**Full citation (reprinted, more accessible):**
> Jensen, M.C., & Meckling, W.H. (1995). Specific and general knowledge and organizational structure. *Journal of Applied Corporate Finance*, *8*(2), 4–18.

**Key finding:** Organizations must either (a) transfer specific knowledge to those with decision rights, or (b) transfer decision rights to those who have the knowledge. Failure to align knowledge and decision authority creates inefficiency and coordination breakdown. This is the canonical economics anchor for "decision ownership ambiguity as a coordination failure driver."

**Note for org psych reviewers:** The 1992 book chapter (Blackwell) is the primary citation. The 1995 JACF reprint is more accessible but JACF is a finance journal. The book chapter is defensible for an org psych paper.

## 4. Dunning-Kruger (1999) — Primary Paper and Replication Literature

### Primary Paper — VERIFIED ✅

**Full citation:**
> Kruger, J., & Dunning, D. (1999). Unskilled and unaware of it: How difficulties in recognizing one's own incompetence lead to inflated self-assessments. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, *77*(6), 1121–1134.

**Details:**
- Journal: *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* (JPSP) ✅ Top-tier social psychology journal
- Volume: 77
- Issue: 6
- Pages: 1121–1134
- Published: December 1999

**Key findings (what the paper actually claims):**
1. Poor performers in logical reasoning, grammar, and humor overestimate their performance significantly
2. Better performers underestimate their relative standing (though not their absolute performance)
3. Explanation: metacognitive skill deficits — those who lack domain skill also lack the metacognitive capacity to recognize their deficiency

**What the paper does NOT claim:**
- It does **not** address "silence as a failure mode" — non-escalation when confidence is high but accuracy is low
- It does **not** propose "confidence decomposition by dimension"
- It does **not** study team coordination or organizational escalation
- The "silence not overclaiming" claim and "confidence decomposition" are **applied inferences/extensions** that go beyond the paper ⚠️ A reviewer may flag citing Kruger & Dunning (1999) for these claims.

---

### Replication / Contestation Papers

**Gignac & Zajenkowski (2020) — VERIFIED ✅**

> Gignac, G.E., & Zajenkowski, M. (2020). The Dunning-Kruger effect is (mostly) a statistical artefact: Valid approaches to testing the hypothesis with individual differences data. *Intelligence*, *80*, 101449.

- Journal: *Intelligence* ✅ Peer-reviewed psychology journal (Elsevier)
- Volume: 80
- Article number: 101449
- Published: May 2020

**What they claim:** The classic DK visualization (quartile line charts showing overestimation by poor performers) is largely an artifact of: (1) regression to the mean, (2) scale boundary restriction, and (3) mathematical properties of plotting self-assessments against objective scores. When appropriate statistical corrections are applied, much less evidence for the DK effect remains. They do not claim it disappears entirely but that the magnitude is far smaller than conventionally presented.

---

**Nuhfer et al. (2016 & 2017) — VERIFIED ✅ (Two separate papers)**

> Nuhfer, E., Cogan, C., Fleischer, S., Gaze, E., & Wirth, K. (2016). Random number simulations reveal how random noise affects the measurements and graphical portrayals of self-assessed competency. *Numeracy*, *9*(1), Article 4.

> Nuhfer, E., Fleischer, S., Cogan, C., Wirth, K., & Gaze, E. (2017). How random noise and a graphical convention subverted behavioral scientists' explanations of self-assessment data: Numeracy underlies better alternatives. *Numeracy*, *10*(1), Article 4.

- Journal: *Numeracy* (open access journal of the National Numeracy Network)
- ⚠️ Lower-prestige venue compared to ASQ/JPSP. Use with caution for org psych audiences; pair with Gignac & Zajenkowski (2020) as the more defensible primary citation.
- Main argument: DK effect graphs replicate with random number data, suggesting the pattern is a mathematical artifact of how data is graphed, not a genuine psychological phenomenon.

---

**Dunkel, Nedelec & van der Linden (2023) — Partial Defense/Replication**

> Dunkel, C.S., Nedelec, J.L., & van der Linden, D. (2023). Reevaluating the Dunning-Kruger effect: A response to and replication of Gignac and Zajenkowski (2020). *Intelligence*, *96*, 101717.

- Journal: *Intelligence* ✅
- Volume: 96
- Article number: 101717
- Published: 2023 (available online 2022; DOI suffix: 10.1016/j.intell.2022.101717)

**What they claim:** When Gignac & Zajenkowski's sample limitations are corrected, the DK effect is statistically significant—but the **magnitude is minimal**, raising questions about practical/theoretical meaningfulness. Partially rehabilitates the original effect while conceding the contestation.

---

### Alternative Support for "Silence Not Overclaiming"

The DK paper does not support this claim. Better options:

**Fischhoff, Slovic & Lichtenstein (1977):**
> Fischhoff, B., Slovic, P., & Lichtenstein, S. (1977). Knowing with certainty: The appropriateness of extreme confidence. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, *3*(4), 552–564.

- Shows systematic overconfidence in calibration (people ~65–70% confident when correct ~50% of the time)
- Useful for "calibration vs. confidence" distinction but does not specifically address "non-escalation as failure mode"

**⚠️ Conclusion:** No dedicated empirical paper was located directly tying high-confidence/low-accuracy calibration to non-escalation in organizational settings. This appears to be a reasonable inference from the calibration literature. **Flag in the paper as an inference/application, not a direct finding.**

## 5. AI-Human Team Dynamics Citations

### Algorithm Aversion — VERIFIED (with author correction) ✅

> Dietvorst, B.J., Simmons, J.P., & Massey, C. (2015). Algorithm aversion: People erroneously avoid algorithms after seeing them err. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, *144*(1), 114–126.

**⚠️ CORRECTION:** The second author is **Joseph P. Simmons**, not "Sparks."
**Venue note:** *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General* is an APA psychology journal — not a management journal, but highly respected and commonly cited across disciplines. Acceptable to org psych reviewers.

**Key finding:** After seeing an algorithm make a single error, participants preferred human judgment even when the algorithm was demonstrably better on average.

### Algorithm Appreciation — VERIFIED (with author correction) ✅

> Logg, J.M., Minson, J.A., & Moore, D.A. (2019). Algorithm appreciation: People prefer algorithmic to human judgment. *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, *151*, 90–103.

**⚠️ CORRECTION:** The proposed citation "Dietvorst & Logg" is incorrect — the authors are **Logg, Minson & Moore**. This is a *separate* paper from Dietvorst et al. (2015).
**Venue:** *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes* ✅ Strong org psych venue.

**Key finding:** In domains with established algorithmic superiority, people often *prefer* algorithmic judgment — the flip side of algorithm aversion.

### Meta-Analysis on Human-AI Performance — VERIFIED ✅

> Vaccaro, M., Almaatouq, A., & Malone, T. (2024). When combinations of humans and AI are useful: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Nature Human Behaviour*, *8*(12), 2293–2303. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-024-02024-1

**Venue:** *Nature Human Behaviour* — interdisciplinary, high-prestige, accepted across management and social science.

## 6. Gloria Mark — Interruption Research

### Mark, Gonzalez & Harris (2005) — VERIFIED ✅

**Full citation:**
> Mark, G., Gonzalez, V.M., & Harris, J. (2005). No task left behind? Examining the nature of fragmented work. In *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI 2005)*, April 2–7, 2005, Portland, OR. ACM Press, pp. 321–330.

**Venue:** CHI 2005 (ACM SIGCHI) ⚠️ HCI venue, not org psych
**Key finding:** Observed 24 information workers; work is highly fragmented with frequent switching. Does not explicitly quantify re-entry time.

---

### Mark, Gudith & Klocke (2008) — VERIFIED ✅ (with critical flag on "23 minutes")

**Full citation:**
> Mark, G., Gudith, D., & Klocke, U. (2008). The cost of interrupted work: More speed and stress. In *Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI 2008)*, April 5–10, 2008, Florence, Italy. ACM Press, pp. 107–110.

**Venue:** CHI 2008 (ACM SIGCHI) ⚠️ HCI venue, not org psych
**Key finding:** Interrupted workers completed tasks faster but with higher stress, frustration, and time pressure; no quality difference.

### ⚠️ CRITICAL FLAG: The "23 minutes" figure is not in any published paper

**The "23 minutes 15 seconds to resume a task after interruption" figure does NOT appear in any published peer-reviewed paper by Gloria Mark.** Investigative analysis of the 2008 paper confirms it **never mentions the number 23**. The figure appears only in:
- Media interviews with Mark (cited by Wall Street Journal and others)
- Popular press articles derived from those interviews

This figure is **not citable as a peer-reviewed finding** and should not be used in an academic paper without flagging its non-peer-reviewed provenance.

---

### ✅ Org Psych Alternatives — Far Better for an Org Psych Audience

**Leroy (2009) — Attention Residue:**
> Leroy, S. (2009). Why is it so hard to do my work? The challenge of attention residue when switching between work tasks. *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, *109*(2), 168–181.

- Venue: OBHDP ✅ Top-tier org psych journal
- Key finding: When switching from Task A to Task B, cognitive attention remains on Task A ("attention residue"), degrading performance on Task B. Mechanism is well-specified and empirically demonstrated.

**Leroy, Schmidt & Madjar (2020) — Comprehensive Interruption Review:**
> Leroy, S., Schmidt, A.M., & Madjar, N. (2020). Interruptions and task transitions: Understanding their characteristics, processes, and consequences. *Academy of Management Annals*, *14*(2), 661–694.

- Venue: *Academy of Management Annals* ✅ Top-tier management review journal
- Key finding: Integrative theoretical framework on interruption type, transition mechanisms, and performance outcomes. The org psych canonical review for this area.

---

### "Optimal Check-In Frequency ~ Task Risk, Not Agent Uncertainty" — Flag as Inference

This claim is **not empirically supported by Mark's work** and does not appear to be a direct finding in the interruption literature. It resembles monitoring theory from principal-agent models (Eisenhardt, 1989). Mark's work documents costs of interruption but does not prescribe optimal interruption frequency as a function of task risk. ⚠️ **Flag as a theoretical inference; do not attribute it to Mark.**
