# Human Dynamics: What Agent Alignment Can Learn From Organizational Psychology

The engineering framing of AI alignment tends to dominate the conversation: specification completeness, constraint satisfaction, output verification. These are real concerns. But they miss something more fundamental. The deepest lessons for building systems where agents and humans work together effectively come not from computer science but from organizational psychology — specifically, from decades of research into what makes human teams actually function well under uncertainty, ambiguity, and distributed decision-making. The entire intent engineering system described in this POC is, at its core, an attempt to engineer shared mental models between agents and humans. Understanding what that means — and what research tells us about how and why it succeeds or fails — should shape every design decision in the architecture.

## Shared Mental Models as the Foundation of Team Alignment

The concept of the shared mental model (SMM) in organizational psychology refers to something more specific and more demanding than shared information. Two engineers can read the same product requirements document and come away with genuinely different mental models of what the project means — different understandings of which constraints are hard versus soft, which trade-offs the organization is willing to make, what success looks like when it isn't completely specified, and who has authority to decide what. The information is identical. The mental models are not.

This distinction is not semantic. Research on high-performing teams consistently shows that what separates them from lower-performing teams with equivalent resources and information is the quality of their shared mental models of goals, constraints, roles, and decision authority. Team members who share a mental model can anticipate each other's actions, fill gaps in communication, and make locally good decisions that are globally coherent — without constant coordination overhead. Teams without shared mental models require explicit communication for every decision because no one can reliably predict how others will interpret an ambiguous situation.

INTENT.md is best understood as an artifact of SMM-building, not as a task specification. The practical implication is significant: the quality of an INTENT.md should not be judged by whether it contains the right sections or covers the expected ground. It should be judged by whether it produces a shared mental model in the agent that reads it. An INTENT.md that is formally complete but leaves genuine ambiguity about decision authority, trade-off preferences, or the definition of good enough has failed at its actual purpose — regardless of how many boxes it checks. An INTENT.md that is concise but creates genuine alignment between human intent and agent understanding has succeeded. The test is the mental model, not the document.

## Trust as Asymmetric and Incremental

The organizational psychology literature on trust in teams converges on a pattern that is directly relevant to the autonomy progression in this system. Trust is not granted — it is built through what researchers call competence credentialing: a process of demonstrating reliable judgment in small, low-stakes situations before being extended trust in high-stakes ones. A new team member who makes good calls on minor decisions earns latitude on larger ones. The progression is incremental, and it is earned through demonstrated alignment between the person's judgment and the team's standards — not through seniority, credentials, or stated intentions.

The loss function for trust is asymmetric in a way that matters for system design. Trust is built slowly and lost quickly. One serious misalignment — a decision made that clearly contradicted understood constraints, or a confident recommendation that turned out to be badly wrong — can undo a long sequence of demonstrated successes. The gain function is concave; the loss function is convex. This asymmetry means that expanding autonomy should be conservative and calibrated to actual alignment signals, not to passage of time or session count.

The cold-start (low autonomy) to warm-start (higher autonomy) progression in this system is correct in its structure. But the expansion of autonomy needs to be explicitly tied to demonstrated alignment signals rather than to implicit session accumulation. This is where the memory system's confidence calibration becomes the operational mechanism — and why it needs to be visible to the agent itself. An agent should be able to reason explicitly: "I'm operating in higher-autonomy mode for this domain because prior sessions have demonstrated consistent alignment with this user's decision patterns and preferences." That explicit awareness serves two purposes: it helps the agent calibrate appropriately when operating near the edges of established alignment, and it makes the trust model legible to the human, who can validate or correct it.

## Communication Load as a Tax on Flow

Research on interruption and flow in knowledge work is unambiguous: frequent interruption imposes a disproportionate cost on productive output relative to the informational value of any individual check-in. The cost is not additive — it is multiplicative. Each check-in breaks a state of deep engagement that requires significant time to re-enter. Teams that over-check-in don't just lose the time of the check-ins themselves; they lose the deep work capacity of everyone involved.

But the failure mode in the opposite direction is equally real and more expensive at delivery time. Too few check-ins allows silent drift: the agent continues executing against a working model that has diverged from current human intent, and the mismatch surfaces only at delivery — at the highest possible cost, when work must be redone and trust must be repaired.

The optimal check-in cadence is calibrated to task risk, not to agent uncertainty as a general condition. The signal to check in is not "I am not sure what to do" but rather "the cost of being wrong on this decision exceeds the cost of interrupting." This is why the spec's "bring solutions not questions" principle is the right frame for check-in quality. A bare question — "Should I do X or Y?" — transfers the cognitive burden to the human without adding value. A check-in that includes the agent's current working model, the decision it's facing, its proposed resolution, and the reasoning behind it allows the human to confirm or redirect with minimal cognitive load. The communication is not a request for direction; it is a proposal seeking ratification or correction. The difference in interruption cost is substantial.

## Psychological Safety for Uncertainty and Escalation

One of the most replicated findings in organizational psychology — made famous by Amy Edmondson's research on medical teams and later extended to organizational settings broadly — is that psychological safety is a prerequisite for effective team performance. Specifically: if raising concerns is likely to be penalized, people stop raising them. The result is not that concerns disappear; it is that they go underground, accumulate, and surface catastrophically.

For agent systems, the escalation culture embedded in the architecture matters in the same way. Escalation must be framed and experienced as good judgment, not as failure or inadequacy. An agent that flags a situation with "I'm escalating this because the cost of being wrong is high and I don't have sufficient confidence to proceed unilaterally" is demonstrating competence — the same competence a senior engineer demonstrates when they call in a colleague before making a consequential infrastructure decision rather than after. The architecture should reinforce this framing explicitly, not just permit it.

The complement of "bring solutions not questions" is "acknowledge genuine uncertainty explicitly." These two principles are in tension if misread, but they are not in conflict. "Bring solutions not questions" governs how to communicate when escalating — always include the working model and proposed path. "Acknowledge genuine uncertainty explicitly" governs when to escalate — when actual confidence is low, that fact must be surfaced, not hidden. An agent that projects false confidence to avoid the appearance of asking questions is more dangerous than one that escalates appropriately. False confidence is the failure mode that causes silent drift to become invisible until it is expensive. Genuine uncertainty, acknowledged and escalated with a proposed resolution, is a system functioning as designed.

## The Dunning-Kruger Trap

The Dunning-Kruger effect — the finding that people with limited competence in a domain systematically overestimate their competence — has a structural explanation that applies directly to agent systems. The mechanism is not stupidity; it is pattern-matching on superficially similar prior experience. Someone who has seen a few examples of X develops a pattern for X. When they encounter X-prime — something that looks like X but differs in important ways — the pattern fires, confidence is high, and the differences go unnoticed. Genuine expertise involves not just having better patterns but having calibrated awareness of when a new situation is outside prior experience.

For agents, the most dangerous execution state is high confidence combined with low actual alignment. This state is dangerous precisely because it produces no signal that would trigger escalation. The agent executes confidently against a wrong model, delivers an outcome that contradicts human intent, and the system has no mechanism to catch the drift before delivery.

The solution is explicit confidence reporting that is decomposed by dimension. Not "I am doing X" but "I am doing X, with high confidence in the technical approach, moderate confidence that this trade-off aligns with your preferences, and low confidence in the timeline estimate." This decomposition serves multiple purposes. It gives the human a basis for targeted intervention — not "is this right?" but "the trade-off framing is slightly off; adjust in this direction." It creates a record that the memory system can use to calibrate future confidence estimates. And crucially, surprises — outcomes that contradict confident predictions — become explicit events that should trigger immediate calibration updates rather than being absorbed silently. The connection between the escalation model and the memory system runs through confidence: confidence is what the agent reports, surprises are what updates it, and memory is what carries the calibration forward.

## RACI and Decision Ownership

The RACI framework — Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed — was developed in organizational management as a tool for reducing coordination failures in complex projects. Its core insight is that ambiguity about decision ownership is the primary source of coordination breakdown. When it is unclear who is responsible for making a decision versus who needs to be consulted versus who merely needs to be kept informed, decisions get made by the wrong person, get made without necessary input, or don't get made at all because everyone assumes someone else is handling it.

The decision boundaries defined in INTENT.md are an implicit RACI. Making them explicit — mapping categories of decisions to their ownership and consultation requirements — reduces ambiguity for agents operating with significant autonomy, and especially for agents operating in parallel within multi-team architectures. In the uber lead, liaison, subteam structure, RACI breakdowns are the primary source of coordination failures. A liaison that is unclear on whether a decision is within its authority or requires uber lead consultation will either over-escalate (imposing coordination overhead) or under-escalate (allowing divergence). The same failure mode appears in parallel subteams: if each subteam has a different implicit understanding of decision ownership, their outputs will be locally coherent but globally inconsistent. The fix is not more communication but more explicit decision boundary mapping in the shared intent artifacts that coordinate the teams.

## How Effective Human Teams Handle Ambiguity

The final pattern from organizational psychology that shapes this architecture is the research on how high-performing teams handle ambiguity — because they handle it differently from low-performing teams, not by eliminating it but by managing it at the right level.

High-performing teams clarify at the lowest-cost moment, which is almost always before starting rather than after investing significant effort. This is not the same as resolving all ambiguity upfront — that is often impossible and always expensive. It is the practice of distinguishing, quickly, between ambiguity that is local and low-stakes ("I'll figure it out as I go") versus ambiguity that is shared and high-stakes, affecting multiple work streams or requiring a decision that touches human preferences the agent cannot reliably infer. The former is handled autonomously; the latter is surfaced for resolution before work begins.

High-performing teams also develop working agreements that cover edge cases, not just the happy path. A working agreement that only specifies what to do when everything goes as expected is not load-bearing — it only functions under the conditions that require it least. Working agreements that earn their keep address the situations where normal assumptions break down: what to do when a dependency is late, when a constraint turns out to be harder than expected, when a decision that seemed clear in planning is ambiguous in execution.

Finally, high-performing teams maintain a shared understanding of "good enough" that allows individual members to make local quality judgments without constant calibration. This is one of the most underrated elements of effective collaboration. If every output requires external validation to know whether it meets the standard, the system cannot scale. INTENT.md must convey not just what to build but what standard of completion is sufficient — not as a floor to minimize effort, but as a genuine shared understanding of fit for purpose. An agent with a calibrated sense of good enough can make thousands of small decisions correctly without check-ins. An agent without that calibration will either over-engineer or under-deliver, and will require constant intervention either way.

The architecture this POC is building is, in the end, a social system with software components. The social dynamics that make or break human teams — shared mental models, asymmetric trust, calibrated communication, psychological safety for escalation, explicit decision ownership, and shared quality standards — are not metaphors for what is happening in agent-human collaboration. They are the thing itself, operating through different substrate. The engineering decisions in this system should be evaluated against that understanding.
