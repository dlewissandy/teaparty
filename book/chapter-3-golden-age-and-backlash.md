# Chapter 3: The Peak and the Groan — Golden Age and Backlash

Every joke form has a moment when it tips from fresh to stale, and the knock-knock joke reached that moment faster than almost anything else in the history of American humor. By the late 1930s — possibly as early as the fall of 1936 — adults were already rolling their eyes. The newspaper columnists who had celebrated the craze were writing sarcastic retrospectives. The parlor game sets were on the clearance shelves. The format was, as far as sophisticated adult culture was concerned, finished.

It wasn't finished. It was just leaving.

The story of what happened to the knock-knock joke between roughly 1937 and the 1970s is not, strictly speaking, a story of survival — because the joke never actually threatened to die. It's a story of migration. The joke form moved out of adult parlor culture and into the one social ecosystem that would sustain it indefinitely: childhood. And once it was there, it turned out that childhood was exactly where it belonged.

---

## The Backlash, Such As It Was

The secondary sources documenting the 1936 craze also document its backlash, though the primary newspaper evidence is behind paywalls and cannot be quoted directly. What can be established from verifiable accounts is that by the time the craze reached its peak, the format was already generating its own counter-reaction.

This is not unusual. Cultural crazes tend to trigger backlash in proportion to their intensity, and the knock-knock craze was intense. The format was, by design, impossible to avoid if you were in the company of anyone who knew the jokes — and by late 1936, everyone knew the jokes. The social mechanics of the knock-knock joke, which required a willing audience to play along, could also function as a kind of conversational hostage-taking: you couldn't easily refuse to say "who's there?" without seeming like a poor sport, and once you said it, you were in the joke whether you wanted to be or not.

Adult frustration with this dynamic seems entirely plausible, even if primary backlash pieces cannot be directly quoted. What adults objected to, in all likelihood, was not the jokes themselves but the structure — the way the format commandeered your participation without asking. A comedian can tell you a joke and you can receive it passively. The knock-knock joke doesn't let you be passive. It needs you.

That structural quality — the compulsory participation — is exactly what made children love it.

## Why Children Kept It

Paul McGhee, a developmental psychologist who produced foundational work on children's humor development (*Humor: Its Origin and Development*, 1979; behind a paywall and not directly accessed), proposed four stages of humor development that map onto Piagetian cognitive stages. His Stage 4 — the comprehension of double meanings — is the stage at which knock-knock jokes become genuinely enjoyable. This stage typically emerges around ages six or seven. Before that, children may attempt knock-knock jokes — and anyone who has spent time around four-year-olds knows they do, with great enthusiasm and no real grasp of what they're doing. A 2022 literature review on children's humor development (published in the *World Journal of Advanced Research and Reviews*, freely accessible) states directly: "from 4–5 years old, children may start to tell short jokes with nonsense words and may even attempt a knock-knock joke, though the punch line is often incongruous."

This is a phenomenon familiar to any parent: the four-year-old who gets hold of the knock-knock format and begins telling jokes that go "Knock knock / Who's there? / Banana / Banana who? / Uh… BANANA!" The form is there; the punchline mechanism is not yet understood. What the child has mastered is the ritual frame — the five-line exchange that signals joke-mode — without yet grasping why the punchline has to be a phonetic substitution rather than anything else that pops into their head.

By ages six or seven, per McGhee's developmental framework and a 2021 peer-reviewed study in the *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis* (Jackson et al., PMC open access), children have acquired the metalinguistic awareness to understand that words can have multiple meanings, that sounds can map onto different interpretations, and that the joke is the moment of switching between them. This is the comprehension window. And it is precisely when knock-knock jokes become not just funny but *tools* — tools for demonstrating cognitive sophistication to peers, for claiming social status through wit, for exercising a newly discovered power over language.

## The Social Life of Joke-Telling

What children do with knock-knock jokes is not simply repeat them. They perform them, collect them, trade them, and modify them. Mary Knapp and Herbert Knapp, whose 1976 book *One Potato, Two Potato: The Secret Education of American Children* (Internet Archive; not fully accessed during research) documented children's oral culture across forty-three U.S. states and territories, argued that children's folklore functions as a "secret education" — a parallel curriculum, transmitted entirely peer-to-peer, that teaches children how to manage social stress, claim status, and navigate relationships outside adult supervision.

Iona and Peter Opie spent eight years knocking on school doors across Britain — literally, in some cases — collecting whatever children were saying to each other when adults weren't listening. Their 1959 book *The Lore and Language of Schoolchildren* drew on roughly five thousand informants at seventy schools, and what they found was an oral culture of extraordinary stability: jokes, rhymes, and game formulas that had survived for generations, passed child-to-child without adult mediation, resistant to revision, alive in schoolyards across the country. Their core finding: children's oral culture is a self-sustaining transmission system, children as the agents of both preservation and change. The Opies did not specifically document knock-knock jokes — their survey predates the widespread British adoption of the form — but the transmission dynamics they described apply directly. Jokes pass from older children to younger, from school to school, and accumulate variant forms through the normal processes of folk transmission.

A 1982 piece in *Children's Folklore Review* (freely accessible via IU ScholarWorks) frames this precisely: "folklore is used in the conduct of peer group social life as the traditionally shared means for displaying one's competence to others." For children, the knock-knock joke is not primarily a humor delivery mechanism. It is a social performance: evidence that you know the form, can execute the exchange correctly, and possess the store of jokes required to sustain the game. Telling a knock-knock joke well, especially to an adult who is visibly ambushed by being required to participate, is a form of power.

## The Interrupting Cow and the Art of the Variant

Once a joke format enters the children's transmission system, it does not simply circulate intact. It generates variants — structural experiments that test and expand the form's possibilities.

The Interrupting Cow is among the most documented of these. Recorded by a student at the University of Southern California's Digital Folklore Archives, with full transmission context (the collector's informant was an eighteen-year-old from Barrington, Illinois, who reported learning it on a school bus in elementary school and passing it on during recess and lunch), the joke works like this:

> Knock knock.
> Who's there?
> Interrupting cow.
> Interrupting cow wh—
> **MOO!**

The punchline arrives before the straight man can finish their line. The joke subverts the knock-knock format's most fundamental rule — that the speaker waits for "who?" before delivering the name, and waits again for "[name] who?" before delivering the punchline — by making the interruption itself the punchline. The humor is structural rather than phonetic; it's a joke about the form rather than a joke within it.

This kind of variant is significant because it requires sophisticated understanding of the original format to appreciate. You cannot find the Interrupting Cow funny unless you know what it is interrupting. In this sense, the children's transmission system was not merely preserving the knock-knock joke — it was developing a meta-commentary on it. The joke form was becoming something children thought *about*, not merely performed.

## The Adult-Child Divide as Cultural Engine

The division between adult exhaustion and childhood enthusiasm is not merely a curiosity — it is the mechanism that explains the knock-knock joke's survival. Most cultural crazes that peak and backlash simply disappear. The adult judgment that something is passé tends to stick; the market for it dries up, and it is remembered as a dated artifact of a specific moment. The hula hoop. The Macarena. Fidget spinners.

The knock-knock joke escaped this fate because it found an alternative social ecosystem that was immune to adult fashion cycles. Children do not care whether their jokes are passé. They care whether the jokes work — whether they produce the response they are supposed to produce, whether the form can be executed correctly, whether the punchline lands. Adults' embarrassment about the jokes is, from a child's perspective, not a reason to stop telling them; it is, if anything, a reason to tell more of them.

By the 1940s, the knock-knock joke was being systematically collected in children's joke books — an industry that has never really stopped producing them. (Specific titles with verified publication dates from the 1940s and 1950s were not located during research; the existence of the market is documented in secondary accounts but the individual volumes were not.) The joke books matter as infrastructure: they converted an oral tradition into a print canon, gave children a repository they could browse and memorize, and provided a standardized corpus that was simultaneously accessible to all children and expandable by any of them.

---

## What the Golden Age Actually Was

The phrase "Golden Age" for the knock-knock joke is a little misleading. The 1930s were the craze; the golden age was what came after — the long, quiet period in which the joke settled into its permanent place in American culture not as a fashionable entertainment for adults but as a foundational genre for children. The "golden" part is not the brightness of cultural prominence but the durability of cultural installation.

By 1950, the knock-knock joke had achieved something that very few joke forms manage: it had become infrastructure. You didn't have to seek it out. You would encounter it, reliably, at any age between four and twelve — passed along by a sibling, a schoolmate, a summer camp counselor, a joke book. The joke form was now in the standard kit of American childhood, as permanent as jump-rope rhymes and counting games and the rules for tag.

Adults who had declared it dead in 1938 were, by the time their own children were five years old, being subjected to it again from the other side of the exchange. The wheel turned. The jokes were different. The form was exactly the same.

And as the joke settled into childhood and the children carried it everywhere they went — to school, to summer camp, to television, to other countries, eventually to the internet — its popular culture footprint grew in ways that no single craze could have produced. A joke form that lives in childhood is a joke form that gets rediscovered by every generation. What happened next is the story of how knock-knock jokes colonized every medium that tried to host them.
