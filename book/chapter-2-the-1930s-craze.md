# Chapter 2: "Knock Knock, Who's There? America" — The 1930s Craze

In the summer of 1936, Americans were obsessed with knock-knock jokes. Not mildly fond of them, not intermittently amused — obsessed, in the peculiar way that the 1930s United States could become collectively obsessed with something small and repetitive and inexhaustible. Radio stations were running knock-knock joke contests. Newspaper columnists were debating whether the craze was delightful or maddening or both. Party guests were subjecting each other to them compulsively. A cartoonist named Bob Dunn was publishing a knock-knock-themed comic strip. There was a song.

How does this happen? How does a particular joke format — not a joke, not a comedian, not a film, but a *format*, an empty shell that anyone can fill with any pun — become a national phenomenon? And how does it happen so suddenly, in what appears from the historical record to have been a matter of months?

The honest answer, given the evidence available, is that we don't fully know. The primary newspaper sources from the 1936 craze — columns in the Harrisburg Telegraph, the News Herald, the AP wire — are documented in secondary accounts but are locked behind paywalls. What can be established with confidence is that the craze was real, that it was documented, and that at least one person played a significant role in amplifying it. The rest requires some careful reasoning from what's verifiable.

---

## Before the Craze: The "Do You Know" Ancestor

The knock-knock joke did not erupt from nothing. As the previous chapter described, a family of wordplay formats was already circulating in American popular culture by the early twentieth century — and the one most directly ancestral to the 1930s craze was the "Do You Know" game.

The structure: "Do you know Arthur?" / "Arthur who?" / "Arthur-itis!" The mechanism is already there — the phonetic pun, the misleading name, the snap into a second reading. What the "Do You Know" game lacked was the ritual frame that would make the knock-knock joke so instantly recognizable as a form. Around 1900, per secondary accounts, this format was making its way through vaudeville circuits and parlor culture. It was the evolutionary draft of what was coming.

What made the "Do You Know" game structurally insufficient was its trigger. The question "Do you know Arthur?" is an open gambit — the straight man might reasonably answer yes or no, or ask which Arthur. The knock-knock format resolves this ambiguity by making the frame mandatory. "Knock knock" produces "Who's there?" as a reflex. There is no sensible alternative response. The ritual takes care of what the earlier format left open, which meant the punchline mechanism could focus entirely on the phonetic substitution instead of also managing the conversational setup.

The first knock-knock joke using the modern frame — "Knock knock / Who's there? / [name] / [name] who? / [punchline]" — cannot be precisely dated. Secondary sources suggest somewhere in the early 1930s; NPR's 2015 piece on knock-knock history mentions 1934 as a plausible date for an early newspaper appearance. The primary source for that specific claim was not located during research, and the exact first joke must be treated as uncertain. What is clear is that by 1936, the format was everywhere.

## Bob Dunn and the Machinery of the Craze

Picture a newspaper cartoonist in early 1936, sitting at a drawing board somewhere in New York, watching what a joke format is doing to his readers' mail. Bob Dunn was, according to the investigation by CBR (Comic Book Resources) and the documentation at What Were Comics, the figure most responsible for turning the knock-knock joke from a circulating curiosity into a national institution — and his method was, in retrospect, ingenious.

Dunn launched a comic strip titled *Knock Knock* in 1936, built around the format. The strip's premise is less important than its function: it gave the knock-knock joke an institutional address. But the more significant move was the contests. Dunn organized knock-knock joke competitions that invited readers to submit their own jokes — the best of which would be published, either in the strip or in related features. This created a feedback loop that is worth examining closely, because it is the mechanism that turned a joke format into a craze.

Step one: a reader encounters the knock-knock format in the strip. Step two: the reader realizes, because the format is a template, that they can generate a joke of their own. Step three: the reader submits their joke and sees it in print. Step four: other readers see the published joke, understand that they too can participate, and begin submitting. The loop is self-amplifying: each published joke recruits the next batch of contributors. Dunn's insight — whether deliberate or intuitive — was that the knock-knock joke's participatory structure, which required an audience to co-perform the setup, also made it a participatory creative act. The audience wasn't just receiving the jokes; they were generating them.

This is different from how most comedy worked in 1936. A radio comedian wrote jokes; a broadcast audience laughed at them. A newspaper humorist composed columns; readers consumed them. The relationship was producer-to-audience, one-directional. Dunn's contest model flipped it: the audience produced jokes, Dunn curated and published them, and the audience's products were the content. He was, in the vocabulary that would be invented decades later, running a user-generated content platform in 1936.

Think about what that looked like from the reader's side. You pick up the newspaper on a Wednesday morning in June 1936 and there, in the comics section, is a strip full of knock-knock jokes — some of which were submitted by people who live in your city, or in a city you've been to, or whose names suggest they might be roughly like you. The jokes are not particularly good. But they were submitted, selected, and published. The mechanism has been demonstrated: ordinary people can generate content worthy of appearing in print, and the mechanism for doing it is simple enough that anyone can try. The knock-knock joke's genius was always that it was a template, not a performance; Dunn's insight was that a template could be turned into an open call.

The contest format also gave the knock-knock joke something it had previously lacked: editorial curation. Not all phonetic puns are equally clean. A name that only vaguely resembles its target phrase requires more imaginative work from the listener; the frame-shift feels labored, and the humor is diminished accordingly. Dunn's publication choices — which submitted jokes ran, which didn't — created a de facto quality standard. The jokes that appeared in print were the ones that worked best: closest in phonetic distance, most natural in delivery. Readers didn't just receive a template; they received validated examples of the template done well. This is a more useful lesson in joke construction than any instruction manual could provide. You learn what "Olive" does and intuitively understand what it means for a name to succeed in that slot.

The CBR investigation documents Dunn's role with some care, and the picture that emerges is of a pragmatist who recognized what the format could do and built machinery around it. He is not documented as having invented the knock-knock joke — secondary accounts suggest the format was already circulating before his strip. What he appears to have done is recognize it, amplify it, and give it an institutional home: a mechanism of collection and redistribution that the format lacked when it was only being passed mouth-to-mouth.

What made Dunn an interesting figure in retrospect is not that he invented anything. He didn't. The phonetic pun was ancient; the call-and-response structure was ancient; the name-as-misdirection had been circulating in American parlor culture for decades. What Dunn contributed was institutional. He is remembered by comics historians rather than by humor scholars or cultural historians, which is itself a small injustice — his operation was a genuinely unusual thing: a mechanism for turning audience participation into published content, run out of a newspaper comic strip, before any of the vocabulary existed to describe what he was doing. The craze he helped amplify would be studied for decades as a mystery. He was, arguably, the person who most clearly understood its mechanics at the time.

## Vincent Lopez and the Song

If Bob Dunn provided the print infrastructure for the craze, Vincent Lopez provided its soundtrack. Lopez was a bandleader and radio personality — a significant figure in early American radio entertainment whose orchestra had been broadcasting regularly since the 1920s — who in 1936 released what secondary sources call the "Knock-Knock Song." The song is documented in both NPR's 2015 piece and Mental Floss's contemporaneous coverage; the full recording and its exact chart performance were not traced during research.

What is notable about Lopez's involvement is what it reveals about the craze's speed and breadth. For a bandleader of Lopez's prominence to release a knock-knock-themed song in 1936 means the format had already achieved sufficient cultural saturation to be worth capitalizing on by a major entertainment figure. The song is a commercial artifact of a craze already in progress, not a cause of the craze — but it's also evidence that by the middle of 1936, the knock-knock joke had achieved the kind of critical mass that made it a legitimate commercial property.

This is how joke crazes work: they are self-amplifying once they cross a threshold. The format is simple enough that anyone can participate; participation increases visibility; increased visibility draws more participants. The knock-knock joke's particular genius, from the perspective of cultural transmission, is that it is infinitely generative — you don't need to remember a joke, you need to remember a template, and the template will generate as many jokes as you can find phonetically exploitable names. "Olive" becomes "I love you." "Howard" becomes "Howard you like to hear another knock-knock joke?" "Lettuce" becomes "Lettuce in, it's cold out here." The constraints are light; the outputs are endless. Every person who heard the format on the radio could, in principle, go home and invent a new one before dinner.

## What the Newspapers Saw

The direct newspaper record of the 1936 craze is, for researchers without institutional access, frustrating. Secondary accounts describe columns in the Harrisburg Telegraph (June 1936), the News Herald from Franklin, Pennsylvania (July 1936), and AP wire reports (August 1936). These papers are documented as carrying coverage of the craze — contests, jokes, commentary — but their specific contents cannot be described here because they are behind paywalls and were not directly accessed during research.

What secondary sources convey is that newspaper coverage was substantial enough to fuel the craze's spread across the country. The AP wire piece in particular matters: the Associated Press distributed to hundreds of papers simultaneously, meaning that a wire report about knock-knock jokes would have appeared in local newspapers from Maine to California on the same day. The AP wire was, in the 1930s, approximately what trending social media is today — a mechanism for synchronizing national awareness of a cultural moment. When the AP decided the knock-knock craze was worth a wire story, it effectively nationalized a phenomenon that might otherwise have stayed regional.

One earlier document is also worth noting: an Oakland Tribune column from 1922, attributed to a writer named McEvoy, that reportedly contains early wordplay in the knock-knock vein. This column is documented in secondary sources (including NPR 2015) but is also paywalled. Its existence suggests that elements of the joke format may have been circulating in newspapers for over a decade before the 1936 craze — but without reading it directly, characterizing its content would be speculation. What can be said is that the 1930s craze did not land without precedent; there was a newspaper infrastructure for wordplay, and for phonetic joke-contests in particular, that had been building for years.

## The Commercial Ecosystem

The 1936 craze was not only a media phenomenon — it was also a commercial one. Mental Floss and NPR both report that "Knock Knock" party game sets were sold commercially during the craze. This is the kind of artifact that is easy to underestimate: it means someone decided the craze was stable enough to invest in physical manufacturing, packaging, and retail distribution. Party games have long lead times. If knock-knock party game sets were in stores by summer 1936, they were being designed and manufactured months earlier, meaning the craze had been detectable by commercial actors since at least early 1936.

The party game sets suggest something else too: the craze wasn't purely spontaneous or purely driven by individual media personalities. It had an ecosystem — cartoonists, radio personalities, commercial manufacturers, newspaper columnists — all of whom had slightly different interests in the format but were collectively creating the conditions for it to saturate the culture. This is what makes the 1936 moment hard to attribute to any single cause or person. Bob Dunn was real. Vincent Lopez was real. The newspaper contests were real. But the craze was larger than any of them, and its specific contours — why 1936, why that summer, why so suddenly — cannot be fully answered from the available evidence.

## Why 1936: Context and Structure

There is a temptation to explain the knock-knock craze by pointing to something specific about 1930s America — the Great Depression, the rise of radio as a shared national medium, the need for cheap participatory entertainment during economic contraction. These contextual factors are real and not irrelevant.

Radio was transforming American culture in the 1930s in ways that are hard to fully appreciate now. It created a genuinely national audience for the first time — tens of millions of people listening to the same programs, same news, same comedy on the same evenings. The shared reference culture that had previously been fragmented by geography became, through radio, genuinely national. An entertainment phenomenon that went out over the airwaves in New York could be discussed in Kansas the next morning. Bob Dunn's contests and Vincent Lopez's song had the benefit of this infrastructure; the knock-knock format could travel at radio speed.

The Depression had depressed spending on entertainment while increasing appetite for it. Parlor games and word games were cheap — they required no ticket, no equipment, no special materials. A joke format that anyone could play in a living room had a structural advantage in this economy. And the knock-knock joke, crucially, was *free*. You didn't buy a knock-knock joke. You received one and passed it on. The cost of entry was nothing.

But context explains the conditions for a craze, not this particular craze. What made the knock-knock format catch was structural: it was infinitely generative, participatory, and low-barrier. You didn't need to remember a joke — you needed to know a template. You didn't need a comedian to deliver it — you could deliver it yourself, to anyone, anywhere. The template invited participation rather than observation. In a medium like radio, that kind of participatory text — something listeners could reproduce at home, share with their families, contribute their own versions to — had an advantage over a comedian's set-piece joke that required passive reception. The Depression made the conditions right; the format's structure made it work.

## The Mechanics of a 1930s Knock-Knock Joke

What did the jokes actually sound like? The classic examples of the period, reported in secondary sources, are in the "Olive / I love you" family — phonetic substitutions where a common name sounds enough like a phrase to generate a punchline. It is worth walking through the mechanism precisely, because understanding how the joke works makes it easier to understand why it spread.

Take:

> **Knock knock.**
> Who's there?
> **Olive.**
> Olive who?
> **Olive you!**

The name "Olive" is phonetically near-identical to the phrase "I love." The setup builds a coherent scenario: someone is at the door, they have a name. The frame is so familiar — a knock, a name announced — that the listener builds a perfectly ordinary mental picture. Then the punchline collapses the name into the phrase, and the mental picture has to be rebuilt. The humor (such as it is) is in the split-second reorganization of meaning: *Olive is a name, Olive is also "I love," both of these are true at once, and the punchline is the moment you hold them simultaneously.*

This mechanism required a few things to work. The name had to be phonetically close enough to the phrase that the substitution was clean — strained puns required more cognitive effort and felt labored. The phrase had to be recognizable without setup — no explanation, no context. And the frame had to be so automatic that the listener's mind was occupied with following the ritual rather than anticipating the trick. The ritual is, in a sense, a distraction: while you're saying "who's there?" and "[name] who?", you're not thinking about what the punchline might be. The five-line structure does the work of disarming you.

The "Olive" joke is a particularly well-constructed example of the form because the phonetic distance is minimal: the two syllables of "Olive" map almost exactly onto the two syllables of "I love," with only a slight shift in the initial consonant cluster. The listener's brain has already built a coherent picture — a person named Olive, standing at a door — before the substitution arrives and reorganizes everything. The humor is not in the pun itself; it is in the reorganization, the brief moment of holding two readings of the same sounds simultaneously.

Other examples from the same period illustrate the range of quality the template permits. "Lettuce in, it's cold out here" works because "Lettuce" / "let us" is clean and the implied scenario (a cold person outside, requesting admission) plays into the same door-situation the ritual establishes. "Howard I know if you don't open the door?" is technically functional but requires the listener to work slightly harder — "Howard" / "how would" involves more phonetic stretching. The best jokes of the period were the ones that minimized the cognitive distance between name and phrase while maximizing the surprise of the substitution.

This is why the format is infinitely generative but not infinitely good. The template is easy to instantiate: find a name that sounds like something else, build the ritual around it, deliver the substitution. But the quality of the punchline depends entirely on the phonetic quality of the pun, and phonetically adequate names are not limitless. The craze burned through the best material fast. By the time it peaked, the jokes were already self-parodying: the Banana joke, in which there is no punchline but only an eternal loop of "Knock knock / Who's there? / Banana / Banana who?" until the teller finally says "Orange / Orange who? / Orange you glad I didn't say banana?" The joke works precisely because by 1936, everyone already knew what a knock-knock joke was supposed to do.

Consider what the Banana joke is actually doing. It is not a phonetic pun — "Orange" / "aren't you" is an adequate homophone, but that substitution is not the real punchline. The real punchline is the setup: the compulsory delay that went on three exchanges too long, the teller exploiting the ritual obligation the format imposes, the listener trapped in a loop they consented to when they said "who's there?" the first time. The format's social contract — stay in the exchange, respond on cue, wait for the punchline — has been weaponized against the listener. The humor is structural rather than semantic; it requires not just knowing the knock-knock format but having been stuck in it long enough to feel the obligation.

This is a sophisticated joke. It is a knock-knock joke about knock-knock jokes. Its emergence by the late 1930s signals that the form had become culturally saturated enough to generate its own meta-commentary — which is usually the last stage before a joke format stops being fashionable and starts being something else entirely.

## The End of the Beginning

By the end of 1936, the craze was at or near its peak. Backlash was already stirring — the jokes were everywhere, the format was exhausted, adults were growing weary of being conscripted into the performance. But something had already happened that the backlash could not reverse: the knock-knock joke had entered the canon. It was in the joke books now. It was in the children's repertoire. The adults might grow tired of it, but the children were just getting started.

The story of what happened next — why the joke survived its own overexposure, and where it went when the adults abandoned it — belongs to the next chapter. What belongs here is the recognition that the 1936 craze was not merely a fad. It was an installation event. The knock-knock joke was, in 1936, installed into American cultural infrastructure so thoroughly that no subsequent backlash, however sincere, could dislodge it. Bob Dunn's feedback loop ran long enough to become self-sustaining. The jokes didn't need the craze anymore. They had the children.
