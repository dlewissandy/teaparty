# SPEC.md — *Knock Knock: A History* Chapter Specification

**Status:** Phase 2 working document. Writers read this alongside `book/research-dossier.md` and `INTENT.md`. Do not restate requirements from those documents — this spec fills in what is specific to each chapter.

## Decisions Made

**Chapter count: 6**
The arc from precursors to internet requires at least five distinct periods. The humor-theory material (Decision 2) is rich enough to earn a standalone final chapter — but the global-spread and internet chapters share so much evidence about the joke's resilience that they work better merged. Six chapters covers the full arc without padding.

**Chapter 7 decision: Include as Chapter 6 — "The Joke That Wouldn't Die"**
The applicable scholarship is genuinely substantial: SSTH/Raskin directly explains the phonetic mechanism; GTVH/Attardo maps the fixed frame; Graded Salience and frame-shifting ERP work explains why the punchline moment produces the cognitive effect; Weide 2014 is the only dedicated taxonomy. The Sacks conversation-analysis framing of the opening exchange is conceptually compelling. None of this scholarship was written *about* knock-knock jokes, but it all maps onto them precisely — and that fact is itself interesting. The chapter earns its place if written as "what does existing scholarship tell us about why this specific joke form works so durably" rather than "here is humor theory." The Norrick IJHR open lead is flagged but not relied on.

**1930s primary source gap: Acknowledged throughout**
Chapters 2 and 3 must cite the secondary accounts that are verifiable (NPR History Dept 2015, Mental Floss 2015, CBR/Bob Dunn investigation, LOC Winick 2024) and acknowledge explicitly that primary newspaper sources (Harrisburg Telegraph June 1936, News Herald July 1936, Oakland Tribune 1922 McEvoy column) are documented but paywalled. Writers must not assert primary-sourced facts from those papers. Where secondary accounts disagree or are thin, say so.

## Chapter 1 — "Before Anyone Was Home": Origins and Precursors

**Through-line:** The knock-knock joke didn't arrive from nowhere — it crystallized out of structures that had been circulating in children's games, theatrical comedy, and riddling traditions for centuries. This chapter asks: what had to exist before the knock-knock joke could exist?

**Narrative engine:** Contested origin stories make good history. Start with the Shakespeare claim (it's what readers have heard), dismantle it carefully using Winick's metafolklore argument, and follow the thread back to Ben Jonson — then outward into the children's game traditions and vaudeville wordplay that actually fed the form.

**Key evidence (dossier sources):**
- A1: The Shakespeare attribution and Winick's refutation (LOC Folklife Today, open access)
- A1: Ben Jonson's *The Case is Altered* (~1597–98) — two-speaker "Knock, knock — who's there?" exchange (No Sweat Shakespeare; cross-check with Winick)
- 1.12: Henry Bett, *The Games of Children* (1929) — the "Buff" game as earliest documented children's knock-knock-style dialogue. **Note:** Not directly accessed; cited in multiple secondary sources. Writers must note this.
- A1: Riddling traditions and the punning riddle as structural ancestor — the question-answer adjacency pair built on misdirection
- A2: The "Do You Know" format (~1900) as vaudeville bridge — "Do you know Arthur?" / "Arthur who?" / "Arthur-itis!" — documented in NPR (2015) and Mental Floss (2015)

**Documented example:** The "Buff" dialogue from Bett (1929): "Knock, knock! Who's there? Buff. What says Buff? Buff says Buff to all his men." Cite with caveat that Bett was not directly accessed.

**Gaps and disputes:**
- Shakespeare attribution must be presented as a living myth, not a settled fact — it's the story that *exists*, not the origin that *happened*
- The "Do You Know" format's dates are approximate; no primary source directly accessed
- Jonson claim stronger than Shakespeare but still requires acknowledgment that neither is a clean ancestor — the modern joke form requires both the two-speaker structure AND a punchline twist

**Word target:** 2,500–3,000 words

## Chapter 2 — "Knock Knock, Who's There? America": The 1930s Craze

**Through-line:** In 1936, knock-knock jokes swept the United States in a way that now looks incomprehensible — newspaper columns, radio programs, nationwide contests, commercial tie-ins. This chapter asks: how does a joke form become a national phenomenon, and what does that tell us about 1930s America?

**Narrative engine:** The craze is the most documented period in the dossier and the most culturally specific. Bob Dunn is the key figure — a cartoonist who can be documented (CBR investigation, What Were Comics). The chapter should put readers in 1936 and make the craze feel real, not merely claimed.

**Key evidence (dossier sources):**
- A2: Bob Dunn — documented as the popularizer who ran knock-knock contests and published the comic strip *Knock Knock* beginning 1936 (CBR open access; What Were Comics)
- A2: Vincent Lopez's "Knock-Knock Song" (1936) — documented (Mental Floss 2015; NPR 2015)
- A2: Newspaper column evidence — Harrisburg Telegraph (June 1936), News Herald (July 1936), AP wire (August 1936). **CRITICAL: These are paywalled. Writers must name them as documented but acknowledge they were not directly accessed. Do not quote from them.**
- A2: "Knock Knock" party game sets sold commercially — documented in Mental Floss and NPR (secondary only)
- A2: Vincent Lopez radio broadcast connection (secondary accounts only)
- A2: The first knock-knock joke in print — year approximate (1934 in some accounts, 1936 in others); primary source unidentified in secondary literature. Acknowledge the uncertainty.

**Documented example:** The classic "Olive" joke — "Knock knock / Who's there? / Olive / Olive who? / Olive you!" — documented as circulating during the 1930s craze via secondary accounts. Cite as representative of the period's joke style, not as a primary-sourced 1936 example.

**Gaps and disputes:**
- The specific first knock-knock joke cannot be identified with confidence — secondary accounts differ
- Bob Dunn's role is documented but partly via a single CBR investigation piece; not peer-reviewed
- The 1922 McEvoy column in Oakland Tribune is documented but paywalled — its content cannot be described
- Exactly how jokes spread (radio vs. newspaper vs. word of mouth) is asserted in secondary sources but not granularly documented

**Word target:** 3,000–3,500 words

## Chapter 3 — The Peak and the Groan: Golden Age and Backlash

**Through-line:** Every joke form has a moment when it goes from fresh to overexposed. By the late 1930s, knock-knock jokes were already being mocked. This chapter traces the arc from peak saturation to cultural cliché — and asks why some joke forms survive their own overexposure.

**Narrative engine:** The adult/child split is the chapter's engine. Adults declared the joke form exhausted; children kept it alive. That asymmetry is what allowed the joke to outlast its critics and become a permanent fixture of children's culture.

**Key evidence (dossier sources):**
- A3: Post-1936 decline documented in secondary sources — the joke form moved from adult parlor game to children's domain
- 4.4 (McGhee): Four stages of humor development; Stage 4 (double meanings, ages 6–7) as when knock-knock jokes become properly comprehensible — explains the child/adult dynamic
- 4.6: "Developmental Trajectory" (2022) — states children may attempt knock-knock jokes at 4–5; ages 6–7 for comprehension. Open access.
- 4.5 (Jackson et al. 2021): Teaching double-meaning jokes to children; names knock-knock jokes explicitly. PMC open access.
- A3: Children's joke books in mid-20th century — documented in secondary sources; no specific titles with publication dates directly verified
- 4.9: Children's folklore scholarship on joke-telling as peer socialization ("folklore is used in the conduct of peer group social life") — open access
- 4.1 (Knapp & Knapp 1976): *One Potato, Two Potato* — foundational U.S. children's oral culture study; knock-knock jokes not confirmed by name but the transmission framework applies
- 1.9 (USC Digital Folklore Archives): The Interrupting Cow as documented transmission via peer group (informant from Barrington IL; learned on school bus)

**Documented example:** The Interrupting Cow variant — "Knock knock / Who's there? / Interrupting cow / Interrupting cow wh— / MOO!" — documented with transmission context in USC archive. Can be presented with full provenance.

**Gaps and disputes:**
- The adult-to-child transfer timeline is asserted in secondary accounts but not granularly documented with primary sources
- Children's joke books of the 1940s–1960s: existence documented but specific examples not verified in directly accessed sources
- The backlash is real (contemporaneous commentary is cited in secondary sources) but no directly accessed primary backlash piece

**Word target:** 2,500–3,000 words

## Chapter 4 — On the Air, on the Screen, in the Ad: Knock-Knock in Popular Culture

**Through-line:** By the mid-20th century, knock-knock jokes had escaped the parlor and colonized every medium that carried comedy. This chapter follows the joke into film, television, and advertising — and asks what each medium did to (and with) the form.

**Narrative engine:** Each medium treated the joke differently. Television used it as shorthand for innocence (children's programming) and as a punchline about clichés (adult comedy). Advertising latched onto it as a recognized, safe frame for wordplay. The chapter's organizing question: how does a joke form adapt to media that give it no time for setup?

**Key evidence (dossier sources):**
- A4: Popular culture spread through children's media and television — documented generally in secondary sources; specific TV appearances not independently verified with episode/date data. Writers must be careful not to overstate.
- A4: Advertising use documented via secondary accounts (Mental Floss, NPR)
- A5: International spread began in mid-20th century via English-language media — documented via Mama Lisa's World
- A6 (Breaking Bad): The "I am the one who knocks" monologue (Season 4, Ep. 6, 2011) — documented via No Film School (open access). Not a knock-knock joke but a riff on the cultural register of the phrase. Can be used as a late-chapter bridge toward the internet era.
- A6: Zimmerman trial opening (2013) — documented via Mental Floss (secondary). Demonstrates the joke's cultural ubiquity as a frame that can be misapplied.

**Documented example:** The Zimmerman trial opening is the most documented specific pop-culture moment. Use it carefully — it's a case where the joke form was deployed and failed, which is itself interesting.

**Gaps and disputes:**
- Specific film and TV appearances are asserted broadly in secondary sources; writers should describe the general pattern and note that specific examples would require direct viewing access or a more targeted research pass
- Advertising history for knock-knock jokes is thin in the dossier — this chapter may need to acknowledge that and treat it more briefly than planned

**Word target:** 2,500–3,000 words. If advertising evidence remains thin, aim for 2,000 and narrow scope to TV and film.

## Chapter 5 — "Who's There? Whoever You Want": The Joke Goes Global and Online

**Through-line:** The knock-knock joke depends on English homophones — so why does it appear in dozens of languages? And how does a 90-year-old joke structure thrive in the age of the meme? This chapter covers both the joke's geographic spread and its digital afterlife, arguing they're connected: the joke's fixed frame is its portability.

**Narrative engine:** The phonetic dependency is the structural obstacle that makes global spread interesting. The format (call-and-response with a delayed twist) exports perfectly; the specific jokes almost never translate. What gets transmitted is the container, not the contents. Online, the container is everything — the joke's rigidity becomes the substrate for anti-jokes, meta-humor, and format subversion.

**Key evidence (dossier sources):**
- A5: International adaptations — Mama Lisa's World documents variants in multiple languages. The structural translation vs. content adaptation distinction is the chapter's spine.
- A5: Translation challenges — WordReference forum discussion (open access, not scholarly; use carefully)
- A6 (USC Archive): Interrupting Cow (1.9) — documented peer-to-peer transmission with informant context
- A6 (USU Archive): Reverse knock-knock joke (2017) — documented as anti-joke ("funnier to the teller than the listener")
- A6: Anti-joke taxonomy — TV Tropes (open access, crowdsourced; cite as cultural documentation, not scholarship)
- A6: Internet meme formats — documented via secondary sources; no primary internet archive sources cited

**Documented example:** The Reverse Knock-Knock from USU Digital Commons (2017 student collection) — full provenance available. Use it as an opening or closing frame for the section on anti-jokes.

**Gaps and disputes:**
- No scholarly work on the international spread of knock-knock jokes was found; the chapter relies on Mama Lisa's World and forum discussion
- The online/meme documentation is thin — secondary popular sources only; no digital humanities or internet culture scholarship found
- "Which cultures adopted it most fully and why" cannot be answered with the available evidence — writers must scope to what the dossier supports

**Word target:** 2,500–3,000 words

## Chapter 6 — Why It Works: The Mechanics of a Perfect Joke Container

**Through-line:** No peer-reviewed paper has ever specifically studied knock-knock jokes as a genre — yet the frameworks that do exist map onto them with almost suspicious precision. This chapter assembles what we actually know about why this particular structure has survived for 90 years.

**Narrative engine:** The absence of dedicated scholarship is itself the story. The joke is too obvious to study — or so obvious that it *is* the study. Every major humor framework (script theory, incongruity theory, conversation analysis, developmental psychology, cognitive neuroscience) explains a different piece of it. Assemble those pieces honestly, name what remains unexplained, and let the reader feel the oddity of a cultural object that has been ignored precisely because everyone already knows it.

**Key evidence (dossier sources):**
- 3.1 (Morreall/SEP): Incongruity theory overview — Kant and Schopenhauer on the punchline as "sudden transformation of strained expectation" — open access
- 3.3–3.4 (Raskin SSTH): Script-based semantic theory — the "Olive" / "I'll have" opposition as paradigm case. Raskin 1979 BLS paper (access uncertain); 1985 monograph (paywalled). Cite with access notes.
- 3.5–3.6 (Attardo GTVH): The six Knowledge Resources mapped onto knock-knock structure; Narrative Strategy (the fixed five-line frame) as the genre's most analytically distinctive feature. Attardo 2017 preprint freely accessible.
- 3.7 (Sacks 1974): Conversation analysis — "Knock knock" as the pre-sequence bid for extended turn-holding; "Who's there?" as consent to enter joke-mode. Paywalled; application is by extension.
- 5.1 (Giora, Graded Salience): Explains why "Olive" is automatically parsed as food — the listener's dominant reading is deployed and then subverted. Paywalled but well-described in secondary sources.
- 5.3 (Feng et al. 2023): Frame-shifting ERP study — frame-shifting (not mere incongruity detection) is the necessary cognitive operation. PMC open access.
- 5.2 (Rose et al. 2015): Pun exposure induces ambiguity processing mode — the knock-knock setup may prime the listener before the punchline. PMC open access.
- 5.7 (Palmann & Miller 2025): Greater phonological similarity = funnier joke — explains why close homophones work better than strained ones. PMC open access.
- 2.1 (Weide 2014): The only dedicated linguistic taxonomy of knock-knock jokes — 132-joke corpus; phonological change most common mechanism; homophony ~31%. Undergraduate thesis, open access.
- 4.4–4.6 (McGhee, Jackson et al., "Developmental Trajectory"): Developmental stages — explains why the form is the child's entry point into metalinguistic awareness
- **Open lead:** Norrick paper in *Humor: IJHR* — referenced on Wikipedia Talk page but title/year/volume unverified. **Do not cite. Flag in a note if the chapter's argument would be significantly strengthened by it.**

**Documented example:** The "Olive" joke as a worked example through the SSTH lens: Script 1 (proper noun / food), Script 2 (phonetically identical phrase). Walk readers through the frame-shift moment.

**Gaps and disputes:**
- The humor-theory frameworks do not address knock-knock jokes directly; all applications are by extension — say so
- The Norrick lead is unresolved — acknowledge it honestly rather than assuming it supports or undercuts the argument
- Freud's pun skepticism ("cheapest form of wit") is historically interesting context but should not be misrepresented as a sustained analysis of the genre

**Word target:** 3,000–3,500 words. This chapter can carry more weight than it might seem — the assembly of frameworks across disciplines is the substance.

## Cross-Chapter Notes

**Tone across chapters:** Each chapter should have at least one moment that makes the subject feel alive — a specific joke, a specific person, a specific moment where the cultural stakes are visible. Avoid chapters that are all framework and no scene.

**Citation discipline:** Every claim that names a date, person, or publication must trace to a dossier source. Undocumented claims must either be cut or flagged as speculation. The dossier's negative findings are as important as its positive ones — if a thing wasn't found, the chapter shouldn't assert it.

**Chapter ordering:** 1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6 is chronological with a thematic close. Chapter 5 may feel like a gear-shift (from historical to structural/digital); writers can bridge this by anchoring the chapter in a specific transmission moment (the USC informant, the USU reverse joke) before zooming out.
