# Chapter 1: Before Anyone Was Home — Origins and Precursors

There is a story told about knock-knock jokes that goes like this: William Shakespeare invented them in 1606, in the middle of *Macbeth*, when a drunken porter mutters "Knock, knock, knock! Who's there, i' th' name of Belzebub?" while imagining himself as the gatekeeper of hell. It is a good story. It is also, almost certainly, wrong — or at least, it is the kind of wrong that matters more than ordinary wrongness, because it tells us something important about how we think about where jokes come from.

We want jokes to have origins. We want a moment when someone, somewhere, first said a thing in a particular way and the room fell quiet with laughter. But joke structures don't work like that. They accumulate. They borrow. They arrive, when they arrive at all, through a long process of drift and recombination that doesn't leave much of a paper trail. The knock-knock joke is no exception. What exists before the 1930s — when the modern form crystallized into a national craze — is not a clean origin but a scattered family of ingredients: theatrical door-knocking routines, children's games built around ritualized exchanges, and vaudeville wordplay traditions that had been sliding toward the pun-based joke for decades. None of these is the knock-knock joke. All of them are in it.

---

## The Shakespeare Problem

Start with Shakespeare, because that's where everyone starts. The porter's scene in *Macbeth* (Act II, Scene 3, c. 1606) is the most frequently cited precursor to the modern knock-knock joke, and the Royal Shakespeare Company itself has credited Shakespeare with "the first use of the phrase 'knock knock, who's there?'" The scene is famously odd — a brief comic interlude between Duncan's murder and its discovery — in which a porter, drunk, imagines himself admitting the damned to hell:

> Knock, knock, knock! Who's there, i' th' name of Belzebub? Here's a farmer that hanged himself on th' expectation of plenty.

The porter does this three times, imagining different categories of sinner. The language is there. The rhythm is there. The problem is everything else.

Stephen Winick, a folklorist at the American Folklife Center of the Library of Congress, wrote about this in 2024 in a blog post that is more analytically precise than its format suggests. His argument is that the Shakespeare attribution is what folklorists call *metafolklore* — a traditional story told about a tradition, more revealing about what we want to believe than about what actually happened. His objections are specific. First: the porter's scene is a dramatic monologue, not a two-speaker interactive exchange. The porter imagines knocking; there is no second voice saying "who's there?" and no punchline. Second: the scene's targets — a price-gouging farmer, an equivocating Jesuit, an overreaching tailor — are politically specific to 1606 England. The Jesuit reference almost certainly alludes to Henry Garnet, executed that year for his role in the Gunpowder Plot; the whole scene is topical satire, not joke-telling. Third, and most damingly: there is no documented line of descent from this scene to any joke tradition. "There doesn't seem to be any clear line of descent from the drunken porter's irritated mutterings," notes one Shakespeare resource — and that assessment is, as far as available evidence shows, correct.

This doesn't mean Shakespeare is irrelevant. The porter's scene is evidence that the dramatic convention of door-knocker wordplay was available in English theatrical culture in 1606 — that an audience would have found the rhythm of "knock, knock — who's there" legible as a comic register. But cultural availability is not origin. Water was available before the kettle was invented.

## The Jonson Claim

If not Shakespeare, then — perhaps — Ben Jonson. Winick points out that Jonson's earlier play *The Case is Altered* (c. 1597–98) contains a scene that maps more precisely onto the knock-knock structure. Two characters exchange the lines: "Knock, knock, knock — who's there?" and a response. It is brief, it involves two speakers, and it predates the *Macbeth* passage by nearly a decade.

Why does the two-speaker structure matter so much? Because the knock-knock joke is not a soliloquy — it is a duet. The porter in *Macbeth* imagines an exchange; Jonson's scene stages one. The difference is not minor. The entire mechanism of the knock-knock joke depends on the audience being in the exchange, saying lines, consenting to the ritual. A one-speaker version of the knock-knock format is not a reduced version of the joke; it is not the joke at all. Jonson's scene, in having two speakers, has the correct architecture. Shakespeare's porter, alone on stage muttering at an imaginary gate, has the words but not the form.

This is a stronger structural claim, but it doesn't solve the origin problem — it just pushes it back. Jonson was working with a conventional comic idiom available to Elizabethan dramatists, not inventing a new joke form. The exchange has the scaffolding but not the mechanism: it doesn't build toward a phonetic pun. The door-and-response pattern was cultural raw material, not a joke. What the early modern theater examples demonstrate, taken together, is that the call-and-response pattern around a door was established enough in English popular culture to serve as a joke frame. The joke form that would eventually crystallize around it — with its compulsory five-beat exchange and its phonetic punchline — was still centuries away.

The frequency of door-based comedy in early modern English theater is itself worth pausing over. The theatrical door was a reliable comic register for structural reasons that hold up on examination: it created a natural divide between characters, one with knowledge and one without, with the resolution of that information asymmetry as the scene's engine. The comic potential in "who's there?" was not incidental — it was the point. Who is pretending to be who, who knows what the others don't, who has the power to open or refuse? These are the same structural tensions that the knock-knock joke, three centuries later, would encode in five lines of compulsory exchange. The door was always a comedy machine. The joke form that crystallized in the 1930s just made the machine pocket-sized.

## Children's Games and the Door Ritual

While playwrights were using door-knocking as a comic register, children were playing games built on almost identical structures — and this is, arguably, a more direct ancestor of what came later.

Henry Bett's 1929 book *The Games of Children: Their Origin and History* (cited in multiple secondary sources; the book itself was not directly accessed during research) contains an early documented description of a children's game called "Buff," played in Britain for centuries. The game's accompanying dialogue goes: "Knock, knock! Who's there? Buff. What says Buff? Buff says Buff to all his men." The exchange is formulaic and ritualized, built on a question-and-answer structure where a specific kind of verbal game is the point. It is not a joke in the modern sense — there is no punchline twist — but it is a call-and-response game anchored to a door, with a fixed frame and a participatory structure that requires more than one voice.

The Bett citation appears in several secondary accounts and is widely treated as the earliest documented children's knock-knock-style game. It's worth being careful here: Bett was not directly accessed during research, and the secondary descriptions are consistent but unverifiable without access to the 1929 volume. What can be said with confidence is that children's oral culture in Britain, by the early twentieth century at the latest, included door-based call-and-response games with fixed ritualized frames. The academic study of this tradition, undertaken most exhaustively by Iona and Peter Opie in the 1950s (*The Lore and Language of Schoolchildren*, 1959), documented the extraordinary stability of children's oral culture — jokes, rhymes, and game formulas that had survived centuries of transmission entirely through peer-to-peer exchange, without adult mediation. The knock-knock game tradition, if Bett's account is accurate, fits this pattern exactly.

## The Vaudeville Bridge: "Do You Know Arthur?"

Somewhere between the children's game and the 1930s joke craze, there is a missing link — or rather, a family of missing links. The most clearly documented of these is the "Do You Know" game, a vaudeville-era wordplay format that circulated in the United States around the turn of the twentieth century. Reported in secondary sources including NPR and Mental Floss (primary sources were not directly accessed), the format worked like this:

> "Do you know Arthur?"
> "Arthur who?"
> "Arthur-itis!"

The structure is almost the knock-knock joke. You have a setup question; a "who?" response that establishes the expectation of a name; and a punchline that replaces the name with a phonetically similar word or phrase. What's missing is the ritual frame — the knock-knock / who's there exchange that makes the modern form so instantly recognizable. But the phonetic mechanism is already there: the punchline works by making the listener hear two overlapping readings of the same sounds, and then snapping between them.

The "Do You Know" game is interesting precisely because it is almost but not quite the thing. It demonstrates that the wordplay mechanism — the phonetic pun, the two overlapping readings — was in circulation in American popular culture long before the 1930s. The knock-knock joke didn't invent the pun; it gave the pun a fixed ritual container.

## Riddling Traditions and the Punning Riddle

To understand why that container was so effective, it helps to look at the deeper tradition the knock-knock joke was tapping into: the punning riddle.

Riddles are among the oldest documented forms of verbal play in any language. The structural core of the riddle — a question that misleads, followed by an answer that reframes — is directly ancestral to the knock-knock joke. Alan Dundes, one of the foundational figures in American folklore scholarship, worked on structural definitions of the riddle in the 1960s (with Robert Georges, published in the *Journal of American Folklore*, 1963; behind a paywall and not directly accessed during research). Their approach treated riddles as question-answer adjacency pairs built on misdirection and resolution — and this description maps cleanly onto the knock-knock joke's mechanism, where the name ("Olive") establishes one interpretive frame and the punchline ("I love you!") snaps the listener into another.

The punning riddle — "What has four legs in the morning, two at noon, and three in the evening?" — builds misdirection by activating the wrong schema and then correcting it. The knock-knock joke does the same thing with phonetic material instead of imagery: it activates the "name" schema and then reveals a phrase hiding inside the name's sounds. The cognitive event is structurally identical. The knock-knock joke is, in the most rigorous sense, a punning riddle with a mandatory ritual frame — a riddle that tells you it is coming and requires you to participate in your own misdirection.

The English riddling tradition runs deep enough that this ancestry is not merely theoretical. Old English literature includes elaborate verse riddles — the *Exeter Book*, compiled around 975 CE, contains nearly a hundred of them — built on misdirection and resolution, presenting an object from an unexpected angle and inviting the reader to name what it actually is. These riddles rely on the same cognitive event the knock-knock joke would formalize a thousand years later: an incorrect interpretive frame established through misdirection, then corrected by a revelation that reorganizes everything you thought you understood. The tradition ran through Middle English into Renaissance wordplay games, into the pun-heavy Elizabethan wit that produced Jonson and Shakespeare's door scenes, and eventually into the vaudeville phonetic games that were the knock-knock joke's immediate ancestors. This is not a direct genealogical descent — the tradition doesn't produce each other through careful inheritance. They are variations on a structural theme that human cognition, for whatever reason, finds persistently pleasurable: the setup that deceives, and the correction that satisfies.

What the knock-knock joke added to the punning riddle tradition was not the pun itself — it was that frame. Consider what is structurally unusual about a joke that requires you to participate: you cannot simply receive a knock-knock joke. You have to perform part of it. Think about what normally happens when someone tells a joke: there is an implicit social negotiation — "I'd like to take up some of your attention for a moment" — that usually happens informally, through tone and context. The knock-knock joke makes that negotiation explicit and mandatory. "Knock knock / who's there?" is, in conversation-analytic terms, a pre-sequence: the teller's bid for permission to tell a joke, formalized as a brief performance in itself — but unlike the casual "did you hear the one about," this pre-sequence assigns the listener a scripted line. The audience, by saying "who's there?", consents to enter joke-mode and accepts the obligation of staying in the exchange through to the punchline. Harvey Sacks, one of the founding figures of conversation analysis, described in a 1974 paper (in *Explorations in the Ethnography of Speaking*; behind a paywall; the application here is by extension) how joke-telling requires an audience to cede normal turn-taking rights and hold their response until the punch. The knock-knock format builds that consent into the structure of the joke itself. You cannot hear "knock knock" without knowing you are about to be asked to participate.

## What Was There Before There Was a Knock-Knock Joke

Gather these strands together, and what do you have? You have a theatrical tradition that treated the door as a comic register. You have a children's game tradition that treated call-and-response around a door as a form of play. You have a vaudeville tradition of phonetic wordplay that had already identified the "who?" question as a setup mechanism. And you have a deep tradition of punning riddles that provided the basic mechanism the modern joke form would crystallize around.

None of these is the knock-knock joke. The knock-knock joke, in its modern form — the rigid five-line exchange, the specific "who's there / [name] / [name] who?" scaffold — did not exist before the 1930s in any documented form. What existed was a set of cultural ingredients that, in the right conditions, could combine into something new.

Those conditions arrived in 1936. The ingredients had been accumulating for centuries.
