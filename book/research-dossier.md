### A1. Origins: Literary Precursors and Pre-Craze Forms

#### The Shakespeare Attribution — and Why It's Contested

The most widely repeated origin story holds that Shakespeare invented the knock-knock joke via the porter's speech in *Macbeth*, Act II, Scene 3 (c. 1606). The porter, drunk, imagines answering a gate while he mutters "Knock, knock, knock! Who's there, i' th' name of Belzebub?" The Royal Shakespeare Company has credited Shakespeare with "the first use of the phrase 'knock knock, who's there?'"

- **Source:** [No Sweat Shakespeare, "Shakespeare and Knock Knock Jokes"](https://nosweatshakespeare.com/shakespeare-knock-knock/) — open access. Notes explicitly: "There doesn't seem to be any clear line of descent from the drunken porter's irritated mutterings."
- **Source:** [Library of Congress Folklife Today blog, Stephen Winick, "Knock Knock! Who's There? Metafolklore, Jokes, and Shakespeare," April 20, 2024](https://blogs.loc.gov/folklife/2024/04/knock-knock-whos-there-metafolklore-jokes-and-shakespeare/) — open access. Winick argues the passage is "metafolklore" — a traditional narrative about origins rather than a genuine origin. His objections: the porter is a single speaker (no interactive dialogue), references Henry Garnet's 1606 execution (making it politically topical, not joke-like), and has no punchline.

#### Ben Jonson's Earlier Claim (c. 1597–98)

Winick (LOC, 2024) identified a passage in Ben Jonson's *The Case is Altered* (composed c. 1597–98, predating *Macbeth* by 8–9 years) as structurally closer to a modern knock-knock joke. In Act IV, Scene 7, servant Onion and cobbler Juniper approach a door:

> **ONION:** Knock, knock!
> **RACHEL:** Who's there? Father?
> **JUNIPER:** Father? No, and yet a father, if you please to be a mother.

This has genuine interactive dialogue and a punchline (a sexual proposition wrapped in wordplay). Winick found this through Early English Books Online database research.

- **Source:** LOC Folklife Today blog (Winick, 2024) — open access (see URL above).
- **Caveat:** No documented line of descent from Jonson's scene to the 20th-century joke format. Winick suggests both playwrights "drew on a deep well of folk culture," implying the door-knock exchange was already a folk trope before either wrote it down.

#### The "Do You Know" Precursor Format (c. 1900–1920s)

Before knock-knock jokes solidified as a format, a structurally similar joke circulated using "Do you know?" as the opener:

> "Do you know Arthur?"
> "Arthur who?"
> "Arthurmometer!"

Journalist Merely McEvoy documented this type in a column in the *Oakland Tribune* (May 26, 1922), observing that such jokes returned cyclically every 20 years as "nifties."

- **Source:** [NPR History Dept., "The Secret History of Knock-Knock Jokes," March 3, 2015](https://www.npr.org/sections/npr-history-dept/2015/03/03/389865887/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes) — open access. Cites the McEvoy column; the original *Oakland Tribune* text would require a newspaper archive subscription.
- **Source:** [Best Life Online, "Knock-Knock Joke Origins"](https://bestlifeonline.com/knock-knock-joke-origins/) — open access. Repeats NPR's account without additional primary sourcing.

#### Henry Bett's *The Games of Children* (1929) — **[UNCERTAIN]**

Multiple secondary sources cite Henry Bett's *The Games of Children: Their Origin and History* (1929) as containing the first printed description of the knock-knock structure, embedded in a children's game called "Buff" in which one child bangs a stick and says "Knock knock."

- **Sources:** [Mental Floss, "8 Moments in Knock Knock Joke History"](https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/55962/8-moments-knock-knock-joke-history) — open access. [Grokipedia, "Knock-knock joke"](https://grokipedia.com/page/Knock-knock_joke) — open access.
- **Caveat — [UNCERTAIN]:** No researcher in this study could access the Bett text directly from a freely available source. The claim travels through secondary sources only. The book appears to be out of print and not digitized openly. Treat as plausible but unverified at the primary level.

### A2. The 1930s US Craze

#### The First Published Knock-Knock Joke (1934) — **[UNVERIFIED at primary level]**

Multiple secondary sources state that the first knock-knock joke published in a newspaper column appeared in 1934:

> Knock knock. Who's there? Rufus. Rufus who? Rufus the most important part of your house.

- **Sources:** Mental Floss (see above); CBR, ["The Knock-Knock Joke Didn't Start With Bob Dunn"](https://www.cbr.com/knock-knock-joke-comic-bob-dunn/) — open access.
- **Caveat — [UNVERIFIED]:** None of the freely accessible sources identifies which newspaper or gives an exact date for this 1934 joke. The claim is repeated consistently but the underlying primary source is not cited in any open-access article found. Newspapers.com or ProQuest would be required for primary verification.

#### The 1936 Peak: Radio, Press, Politics, and Music

The craze exploded in 1936, reaching saturation across radio, newspapers, advertising, and music. Several mutually reinforcing factors are documented:

**Political catalyst:** Col. Frank Knox was selected as the Republican vice-presidential running mate for Alf Landon in 1936. Knox's name rhyming with "knock" made him an irresistible peg for the joke format. The *Harrisburg Telegraph* (June 17, 1936) specifically attributed "Knock-Knock Mania" to this development; WKBO radio in Harrisburg broadcast knock-knock jokes throughout the day.

- **Source:** NPR History Dept. (2015) — open access. The *Harrisburg Telegraph* original is **paywalled** (newspaper archive required).

**Radio saturation:** Jean Mackenzie wrote in the *News Herald* (Franklin, PA, July 25, 1936): "You can't turn the radio on anymore without getting one of the Knock-Knock gags."

- **Source:** NPR History Dept. (2015) — open access. The *News Herald* original is **paywalled**.

**Press documentation:** The Associated Press ran a wire story, "'Knock Knock' Latest Nutsy Game for Parlor Amusement," published in the *Titusville Herald* on August 3, 1936.

- **Source:** Mental Floss (2015) — open access. The AP wire / *Titusville Herald* original is **paywalled**.

**Music:** Swing orchestras incorporated knock-knock jokes into audience-participation novelty numbers. Both Fletcher Henderson's and Vincent Lopez's orchestras recorded versions of "The Knock-Knock Song."

- **Source:** NPR History Dept. (2015) — open access.
- **Date discrepancy — [FLAG]:** One source (Grokipedia) places "Vincent Lopez's 'The Knock-Knock Song'" in 1934; NPR groups it with the 1936 craze without specifying a year. The discrepancy is unresolved in the sources consulted. Do not assert a single date.

**Advertising:** Pennsylvania retailers led commercial adoption. Edgmont Cash & Carry grocery (Chester, PA) ran display ads in knock-knock format and later sponsored a "Knock! Knock! Contest" with prizes (August 1936).

- **Source:** NPR History Dept. (2015) — open access. Original newspaper ads are **paywalled**.

#### Bob Dunn's Comic Book (1936) — Popularizer, Not Inventor

Cartoonist Bob Dunn published *Knock Knock: Featuring Enoch Knox* (Western Publishing / Whitman, 1936), a collection of more than fifty single-page black-and-white comic strips, each built on a knock-knock joke. Maurice Horn's comics encyclopedia credits Dunn as "the inventor of the knock-knock joke"; this claim is directly refuted by CBR's investigation.

- **Sources:** [CBR, "The Knock-Knock Joke Didn't Start With Bob Dunn"](https://www.cbr.com/knock-knock-joke-comic-bob-dunn/) — open access. Rating: **FALSE** — knock-knock jokes appeared in print as early as 1934, two years before Dunn's book. [What Were Comics, "Knock Knock (1936)"](https://www.whatwerecomics.com/blog/2018/8/8/0009-knock-knock-1936) — open access.

#### The Fictional "Inventor" and Satirical Backlash

Radio comedian Fred Allen, in his December 30, 1936 year-end broadcast, conducted a mock interview with a fictional character named "Ramrod Dank," billed as "The first man to coin a knock knock." The satirical conceit shows that even in 1936, the question of origin was seen as absurd.

- **Source:** Mental Floss (2015) — open access.

Psychologist D.A. Laird, director of the Rivercrest Psychological Laboratory at Colgate University, published articles in late 1936 condemning knock-knock jokes as symptoms of "mass manias" and comparing the impulse to *Witzelsucht* (pathological punning, a term associated with frontal-lobe disorders). Sigmund Freud's earlier critique of puns in *Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious* (1905) was also invoked.

- **Sources:** NPR History Dept. (2015) — open access. Grokipedia — open access.

#### No Credible Inventor

The preponderance of evidence is that no single person invented the knock-knock joke. The format evolved gradually from oral "Do You Know" riddling traditions and children's door-knocking games, crystallized into its five-part call-and-response structure sometime between 1929 and 1934, and exploded in popularity in 1936 through a confluence of radio, press, and music rather than any single originating act.

- **Source:** Mental Floss (2015) — open access. No episode-level citations are provided in that source; specific episode documentation would require access to NBC archives or a television history database.

- **Source:** ["How to Say 'Knock Knock' in 35 Languages," Foreign Languages](https://foreignlanguages.com) — open access (URL retrieved via search results, exact page URL not captured; verify before citing).

#### Cultural Equivalents — Different Structure, Similar Function

Many cultures have a functionally equivalent pun-riddle joke tradition that does *not* use the knock-knock frame:

- **French:** *Blague de Toto* — jokes centered on a mischievous schoolboy "Toto"; rely on wordplay and situational puns.
- **German:** *Fritzchen* jokes — same role as Toto jokes.
- **British English:** "Little Johnny" jokes.
- **Japanese:** Punning humor that plays on sound-alike words (e.g., "Hawai ni hashain ga inai" — no dentists in Hawaii — because "ha wa ii" = "teeth are good").

- **Source:** [Mama Lisa's World, "Knock-Knock Jokes Around the World"](https://www.mamalisa.com/blog/knock-knock-jokes-around-the-world/) — open access.

#### Translation Challenges

The knock-knock joke's punchline depends on English homophones and near-homophones. In languages without those specific sound collisions, the format either fails to translate or must be rebuilt with entirely different word material. The structure (call-and-response with delayed punchline) translates; the specific jokes almost never do.

- **Source:** Mama Lisa's World (above); [WordReference Forums thread on knock-knock jokes in other languages](https://forum.wordreference.com/threads/knock-knock-joke.2305831/) — open access (forum discussion, not scholarly).

### A6. Internet Era: Subversions, Meta-Humor, and Pop Culture

#### The "Interrupting Cow" Subversion

The Interrupting Cow variant subverts the format by having the teller interrupt the straight man mid-question:

> Knock knock. / Who's there? / Interrupting cow. / Interrupting cow wh— / **MOO!**

This is documented as genuine folk transmission: an 18-year-old informant from Illinois (Barrington) reported learning it on the school bus in elementary school and spreading it during recess and lunch.

- **Source:** [USC Digital Folklore Archives, "Interrupting Cow Knock-Knock Joke"](http://folklore.usc.edu/interrupting-cow-knock-knock-joke/) — open access. This is an undergraduate folklore fieldwork submission, not a peer-reviewed study, but it constitutes a documented collection record.

#### Anti-Jokes and Structural Inversion

The "Reverse Knock-Knock Joke" documented at Utah State University's folklore archive (2017): the format is inverted so the *listener* initiates with "knock knock," leaving the teller to say "Who's there?" — which disrupts the expected exchange and generates confusion rather than a punchline. The collector notes it functions as "an anti-joke — funnier to the teller than the listener."

- **Source:** [Tanner Perkes, "Reverse Knock-Knock Joke," USU Digital Commons Student Folklore Collection, Winter 2017](https://digitalcommons.usu.edu/student_folklore_all/86/) — open access.

TV Tropes documents the full taxonomy of knock-knock joke subversions in popular media, including anti-jokes, meta-jokes, and format violations.

- **Source:** [TV Tropes, "Knock-Knock Joke"](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/KnockKnockJoke) — open access (crowdsourced wiki, not scholarly).

#### Pop Culture Moments

**George Zimmerman trial (2013):** Defense attorney Don West opened his closing argument with a knock-knock joke: "Knock knock. Who's there? George Zimmerman. George Zimmerman who? Alright good, you're on the jury." The joke drew widespread criticism and a reported *New York Times* correction of some kind.

- **Source:** Mental Floss (2015) — open access. The NYT correction is mentioned in one source (Agent C tool result) but not elaborated; original NYT text is **paywalled / unverified**.

**Breaking Bad — "I Am the One Who Knocks" (2011):** Walter White's monologue in Season 4, Episode 6 ("Cornered") — "I am the one who knocks" — became a widely shared internet clip. This is not a knock-knock joke but a cultural riff on the phrase's authority-asserting register.

- **Source:** [No Film School, "'I Am the One Who Knocks': The Exact Moment Heisenberg Was Born"](https://nofilmschool.com/breaking-bad-i-am-the-one-who-knocks) — open access.

#### Children's Language Development — Popular Claims, No Academic Citations

Popular parenting and education sites claim knock-knock jokes develop five skills in children: language/vocabulary, social connection, listening/auditory processing, confidence, and creativity. **No academic citations, named researchers, empirical studies, or developmental frameworks** were found to support these claims in any source accessed.

- **Status — [UNVERIFIED / NO ACADEMIC BASIS FOUND]:** The claims exist in popular sources only. If this angle is pursued for the book, a search of developmental psychology or speech-language pathology journals (via PsycINFO or similar) would be required.

### 1. Academic Folklorists: Opie, Dundes, and the Major Journals

#### Iona and Peter Opie

**Source 1.1**
Opie, Iona and Peter. *The Lore and Language of Schoolchildren.* Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1959. Reissued: New York Review Books Classics, 2001.
- URL: https://archive.org/details/lorelanguageof00opie (Internet Archive; borrowable with free account)
- Paywalled: No
- Type: Primary (original ethnographic fieldwork; ~5,000 child informants at 70 British schools)

The foundational academic study of children's oral culture in Britain. The Opies surveyed children aged 6–14 across England, Scotland, and Wales over eight years, collecting jokes, riddles, epithets, wordplay, sayings, and superstitions. Their core theoretical contribution: children's oral culture is a self-sustaining peer-to-peer transmission system, stable across generations and geographically distributed without adult mediation. The book contains chapters on riddles and on "Wit and Repartee" covering stock jokes and retorts.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** Knock-knock jokes are not confirmed to appear by name in this work. No snippet, review, or secondary commentary found quotes the Opies discussing knock-knock jokes. The 1950s survey predates the wide spread of the knock-knock format in Britain.

#### Elliott Oring

#### Library of Congress — Metafolklore Framing

#### Historical Precursor

**Source 1.12**
Bett, Henry. *The Games of Children: Their Origin and History.* London: Methuen, 1929.
- Paywalled: Out of copyright; no freely accessible scan confirmed
- Type: Primary

### 2. Peer-Reviewed Work Specifically on Knock-Knock Jokes

#### The Scholarly Gap — Summary Assessment

The knock-knock joke is one of the most structurally distinctive and culturally ubiquitous joke forms in English and one of the least studied as a genre in peer-reviewed scholarship. What exists falls into two patterns: (a) methodological-convenience studies that use knock-knock jokes as a controlled stimulus because of their rigid structure; (b) undergraduate theses and lower-prestige venues treating the genre as a primary subject.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 2.1 — Most significant gap:** No article specifically treating knock-knock jokes as a genre appears in *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research*, the field's leading specialist journal. Multiple searches confirmed this absence (subject to the JSTOR/De Gruyter access limitations noted).

**NEGATIVE FINDING 2.2 — Critical open lead:** A Wikipedia Talk page references a Neal Norrick paper specifically on knock-knock jokes in *Humor: IJHR*, but title, year, and volume number could not be located. This is the single most important unresolved lead for peer-reviewed genre analysis. Researchers with De Gruyter access should search "Norrick" in the IJHR archive.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 2.3:** No article in *Language*, *Journal of Pragmatics*, *Journal of Linguistics*, or *American Speech* specifically on knock-knock jokes was found.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 2.4:** No peer-reviewed historical or cultural history paper on the 1936 knock-knock fad exists. The fad is described in popular sources only.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 2.5:** No conversation-analysis or interactional sociolinguistics paper on knock-knock jokes was found, despite the genre being textbook-quality material for such analysis.

#### The Only Dedicated Academic Analysis

**Source 2.1**
Weide, Jared. "The Linguistic Mechanisms of Knock-Knock Jokes." Honors Capstone Project, University of Texas at Arlington, Fall 2014. CC BY 4.0.
- URL: https://mavmatrix.uta.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1007&context=honors_fall2014
- Paywalled: No (open institutional repository)

### 3. Humor Theory Frameworks

#### Incongruity Theory

**Source 3.1**
Morreall, John. "Philosophy of Humor." *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.* First published 2012; revised 2024.
- URL: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/humor/
- Paywalled: No
- Type: Secondary (authoritative philosophical survey)

Best free-access overview of the foundational incongruity theorists. **Kant** (*Critique of Judgment*, 1790): laughter from "the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing." **Schopenhauer** (*The World as Will and Representation*, 1818/1844): incongruity between abstract concept and concrete perception — maps directly onto the knock-knock punchline substituting a phonetically similar but categorically wrong object for the expected name. The SEP entry contains no discussion of knock-knock jokes or call-and-response formats.

---

#### Freud — Condensation, Double Meaning, and the Triadic Structure

**Source 3.2**
Freud, Sigmund. *Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious.* Vienna: Franz Deuticke, 1905. Trans. James Strachey. W. W. Norton, 1955.
- URL: https://ia802907.us.archive.org/17/items/SigmundFreud/Sigmund%20Freud%20%5B1905%5D%20Jokes%20and%20their%20Relation%20to%20the%20Unconscious%20(James%20Strachey%20translation,%201955).pdf
- Paywalled: No (Internet Archive)
- Type: Primary

Condensation and double meaning — both central to the knock-knock phonetic pun mechanism. Establishes the triadic social structure of the joke (teller / audience / butt), mapping onto knock-knock's mandatory two-party exchange. Distinguishes "innocent" jokes (pure technique, no aggression) — knock-knock jokes are paradigm innocent jokes in Freud's taxonomy. Freud was explicitly skeptical of puns as the "cheapest" form of wit. Application is by extension; knock-knock jokes were not culturally established in 1905.

#### Raskin's Script-Based Semantic Theory of Humor (SSTH)

**Source 3.3**
Raskin, Victor. "Semantic Mechanisms of Humor." *Proceedings of the Annual Meeting of the Berkeley Linguistics Society* 5 (1979): 325–335.
- Paywalled: Unknown (BLS proceedings; not freely located)
- Type: Primary (foundational paper; original SSTH formulation)

Original formulation of SSTH. Establishes that jokes require two overlapping but opposing scripts. Applied to knock-knock jokes: Script 1 = proper-noun reading ("Olive"); Script 2 = phrase reading ("I'll have"). Logically opposed but phonetically identical — the paradigm SSTH phonetic-opposition case.

---

**Source 3.4**
Raskin, Victor. *Semantic Mechanisms of Humor.* Dordrecht: D. Reidel Publishing, 1985. (Studies in Linguistics and Philosophy, vol. 24.)
- Paywalled: Yes (book; no free text found)
- Type: Primary (foundational monograph)

Book-length SSTH treatment. Key concepts: Script, Script Opposition, Trigger (punchline element forcing the script switch), Isotopy (coherent semantic line built during setup).

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** Whether Raskin's 1985 book contains a worked knock-knock example cannot be verified without library access.

#### Attardo's General Theory of Verbal Humor (GTVH)

**Source 3.5**
Attardo, Salvatore, and Victor Raskin. "Script Theory Revis(it)ed: Joke Similarity and Joke Representation Model." *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research* 4, no. 3–4 (1991): 293–347.
- URL: https://www.degruyter.com/document/doi/10.1515/humr.1991.4.3-4.293/html
- Paywalled: Yes (De Gruyter)
- Type: Primary (foundational GTVH paper)

Introduces GTVH's six Knowledge Resources (KRs): Script Opposition (SO), Logical Mechanism (LM), Situation (SI), Target (TA), Narrative Strategy (NS), Language (LA). Applied to knock-knock jokes: SO = phonetic homophone opposition; LM = sound-substitution false analogy; **NS = the fixed five-line dialogue frame** (the most analytically distinctive KR — what separates knock-knock from riddles structurally); LA = the pun.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** No freely accessible paper by Attardo or Raskin applying GTVH to knock-knock jokes was found.

- Paywalled: No for preprint

#### Conversation Analysis — The Ritual Frame

**Source 3.7**
Sacks, Harvey. "An Analysis of the Course of a Joke's Telling in Conversation." In Bauman, Richard, and Joel Sherzer (eds.), *Explorations in the Ethnography of Speaking*, pp. 337–353. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1974.
- URL: https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/explorations-in-the-ethnography-of-speaking/an-analysis-of-the-course-of-a-jokes-telling-in-conversation/4A8610D1D725BEDF789DC6A9B4993AFD
- Paywalled: Yes
- Type: Primary (foundational conversation analysis)

Establishes that joke-telling requires a pre-sequence bid for extended turn-holding, with the audience consenting to suspend normal turn-taking rights. ### 5. Linguistics and Cognitive Science on Puns and Homophones

#### The Psycholinguistic Mechanism — Graded Salience

**Source 5.1**
Giora, Rachel. "Understanding Figurative and Literal Language: The Graded Salience Hypothesis." *Cognitive Linguistics* 7 (1997): 183–206.
- URL: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Understanding-figurative-and-literal-language:-The-Giora/a6fc9f4c8d7cb1ba69d6765f094b039ee76e39e9
- Paywalled: Yes
- Type: Primary (foundational paper)

Establishes that more salient word meanings are accessed faster regardless of context. **Directly predicts the knock-knock mechanism:** the listener's lexicon activates "Olive" as a food item automatically; the less salient reading ("I'll have") is only retrieved when the punchline forces reanalysis.

---

**Source 5.2**
Rose, Spalek, and Abdel Rahman. "Pun Exposure Induces Co-activation of Phonologically Related Word Meanings in Language Production." *PLoS ONE* 10, no. 7 (2015): e0130853. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0130853
- URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4482729/
- Paywalled: No (PMC open access)
- Type: Primary (experimental; 88 participants; picture-word interference paradigm)

Demonstrated experimentally that hearing puns induces an "ambiguity processing mode" that enhances co-activation of phonologically related alternate meanings during subsequent word production. Suggests the knock-knock setup may prime listeners into this co-activation mode before the punchline lands.

#### Pun Processing — Neural Evidence

**Source 5.3**
Feng, Y. et al. "Frame-Shifting Instead of Incongruity Is Necessary for Pun Comprehension: Evidence from an ERP Study on Chinese Homophone Puns." *Language, Cognition and Neuroscience* 38, no. 8 (2023). DOI: 10.1080/23273798.2023.2192509
- URL: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23273798.2023.2192509
- Paywalled: Partial (Taylor & Francis)
- Type: Primary (ERP study; most directly relevant neurolinguistic study to the knock-knock mechanism)

Found that **frame-shifting** — switching between two interpretive frames activated by the homophone — is the necessary cognitive operation, not simple incongruity detection. Maps precisely onto the knock-knock mechanism: the name activates one frame (proper noun / food); the punchline triggers a frame-shift to the phonetically identical phrase.

---

**Source 5.4**
Li, X. et al. "Contextual Support for Less Salient Homophones and Pun Humor Appreciation: Evidence from Eye Movements in Reading Chinese Homophone Puns." *Frontiers in Psychology* 13 (2022): 875479. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2022.875479
- URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9131095/
- Paywalled: No (PMC open access)
- Type: Primary (eye-tracking experiment)

Presenting the less salient homophone meaning in context led to greater humor experience, consistent with Graded Salience. Confirms that both meanings must be simultaneously active for pun appreciation. Directly models the knock-knock mechanism.

**Source 5.5**
Mayerhofer, B., and A. Schacht. "From Incoherence to Mirth: Neuro-Cognitive Processing of Garden-Path Jokes." *Frontiers in Psychology* 6 (2015): 550. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2015.00550
- URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4429229/
- Paywalled: No (PMC open access)

Garden-path joke endings produce enhanced N400 (semantic integration difficulty) followed by late positivities correlated with humor appreciation. Three-stage model: (1) violation detection, (2) semantic revision, (3) mirth. Maps onto the knock-knock mechanism structurally.

**Source 5.6**
Zhao, J. et al. "The Resolution Stage, Not the Incongruity Detection Stage, Is Related to the Subjective Feeling of Humor: An ERP Study Using Japanese Nazokake Puns." *Brain and Cognition* (2022). DOI: 10.1016/j.bandc.2022.105790

#### Phonological Distance and Funniness

**Source 5.7**
Palmann, A., and T. Miller. "What's in a Pun? Assessing the Relationship Between Phonological Distance and Perceived Funniness of Punning Jokes." *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research* 38, no. 4 (2025): 643–658. DOI: 10.1515/humor-2024-0060
- URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12487772/
- Paywalled: No (PMC open access)
- Type: Primary (quantitative; 2,718 annotated puns with crowdsourced funniness ratings)

Greater phonological similarity between pun and target = funnier joke (significant negative correlation). Homographic puns rated significantly funnier than heterographic puns. **Directly supports the construction principle of knock-knock jokes:** maximum phonological similarity between the name and the intended phrase is what makes the joke funnier, not merely a stylistic convention.

**Caveat:** Study did not examine knock-knock jokes specifically; all stimuli were self-contained written jokes, not call-and-response format. Findings are analogically applicable.

#### Relevance Theory

**Source 5.8**
Yus, Francisco. "Humor and the Search for Relevance." *Journal of Pragmatics* 35, no. 9 (2003): 1295–1331. DOI: 10.1016/S0378-2166(02)00179-0
- Paywalled: Yes (403 on fetch)
- Type: Primary (central paper applying Relevance Theory to humor)

Humor arises when a hearer follows a path toward relevance that is subverted by an unexpected interpretation. For puns: the hearer pursues the most salient interpretation; the punchline reveals a second interpretation also achieving relevance. **Directly applicable to knock-knock jokes:** the name is initially parsed as a proper noun; the punchline reveals the phonetically identical phrase as the "real" relevant interpretation.

The mechanism is fully describable using existing frameworks (GTVH, Graded Salience, frame-shifting, paronomasia target recoverability) but has not been given a named, widely adopted term in the linguistics literature.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 5.B:** No fMRI or EEG study specifically on knock-knock jokes was found. Neuroimaging work addresses puns and homophone puns in general.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 5.C:** Sperber and Wilson (1986) do not specifically treat knock-knock jokes or puns in depth in the original *Relevance* monograph. The application to puns was developed by Yus (2003) and subsequent scholars.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 5.D:** The garden-path joke literature addresses semantic/pragmatic ambiguity — related to but distinct from the phonetic ambiguity mechanism in knock-knock jokes. No garden-path paper specifically analyzed phonetically ambiguous names.

**NEGATIVE FINDING 5.E:** No *Journal of Psycholinguistic Research* article on pun processing or homophone ambiguity in humor was found. The relevant research is distributed across *Frontiers in Psychology*, *Brain and Cognition*, *Journal of Neurolinguistics*, *PLoS ONE*, and *Language, Cognition and Neuroscience*.

### Cross-Cutting Patterns and How to Leverage This Research

**Pattern 1: Methodological Convenience as Accidental Genre Documentation**
The richest peer-reviewed material on knock-knock jokes treats them as a convenient controlled stimulus rather than a cultural object (Schmidt et al. 2014 on bodily synchronization; Summerfelt et al. 2010 on memory). Writers can cite these studies for structural properties of the genre — the 8-beat timing, the rigid turn sequence, the phonetic ambiguity mechanism — while noting the studies themselves were not about the cultural phenomenon.

**Pattern 2: The Genre Is Undertheorized Relative to Its Cultural Prevalence**
Across all five research areas, the same gap appears: the knock-knock joke's rigid ritual frame, its phonetic-substitution mechanism, and its specific place in children's developmental acquisition have received far less academic attention than genres of comparable cultural significance. This gap is itself a meaningful observation about how folklore scholarship has distributed its attention.

**Pattern 3: The Developmental Window Is Well-Documented Even Without Knock-Knock-Specific Studies**
McGhee's Stage 4 humor development (ages 6–7), the Jackson et al. (2021) explicit mention, and the WJARR 2022 review converge on a picture: children can attempt knock-knock jokes as early as age 4–5 but cannot reliably produce or appreciate them correctly until ages 6–7 when metalinguistic awareness of double meanings is established.

**Pattern 4: The Ritual Frame Is the Genre's Defining Feature**
The GTVH's Narrative Strategy knowledge resource (Attardo & Raskin 1991) isolates the fixed five-line dialogue frame as what distinguishes knock-knock jokes from riddles and one-liners structurally. Sacks' pre-sequence bid analysis explains its interactional function. Schmidt et al.'s bodily synchronization data shows it produces measurable social entrainment. Three frameworks from different disciplines converge on the same structural fact.
