## Preface

*The Practitioner's Handbook of Extradimensional Navigation, Third Edition*
*The Institute for Manifold Studies*

---

The discipline of extradimensional navigation is, by the reckoning of most historians of science, no older than seventy years — though it will be observed that the theoretical foundations were laid considerably earlier, in the brane-world formalisms of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries. It was not until 2019 that the first Casimir resonance lattice of sufficient stability was produced under laboratory conditions, and not until 2031 that the Pellerin-Okafor team achieved what is now regarded as the inaugural transit: a controlled passage through a compactified Calabi-Yau manifold and a successful return, the latter being, in those early years, by no means guaranteed.

The chaotic period that followed — colloquially, and the Institute does not endorse the term, referred to as the "Wild Decades" — produced advances of extraordinary value alongside losses that the Institute commemorates annually. It has been our experience that enthusiasm, when insufficiently tempered by rigor, is among the more reliable predictors of adverse outcomes. The casualty figures for practitioners operating without formal certification have improved markedly since the first edition of this Handbook (2058), a development we attribute in roughly equal measure to better methodology and, candidly, to a smaller number of persons willing to attempt transit without certification.

This third edition has been necessitated by several developments since the second (2074): the incorporation of revised brane-stabilization protocols following the Kessler group's findings on dimensional shear gradients; the correction of three non-trivial errors in Chapter Seven (the Institute thanks the anonymous correspondent who survived long enough to submit the errata); the addition of two new chapters addressing exotic matter scarcity under degraded Casimir lattice conditions; and the general updating of safety appendices to reflect current Institute standards.

The reader is directed to the updated Table of Manifold Tolerances in Appendix C, which supersedes all prior versions and should be treated as definitive.

We commend this volume to practitioners at all levels of experience, with the observation that the dimensions do not make allowances for overconfidence.
