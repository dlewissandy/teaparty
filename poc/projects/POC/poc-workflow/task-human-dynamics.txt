DOCUMENT TITLE: # Human Dynamics: What Organizational Psychology Teaches Agent Alignment

CORE ARGUMENT: The deepest lessons for AI agent alignment come not from engineering but from organizational psychology. Effective human teams do not just share information -- they build shared mental models. The entire intent engineering system is an attempt to engineer shared mental models in agent-human collaboration.

SECTIONS (use these exact headers):

## Shared Mental Models as the Foundation of Team Alignment
Open with the concept precisely:
- Shared mental models (SMMs): a shared understanding of goals, constraints, roles, and decision authority -- not just shared information. Two people can have exactly the same information but very different mental models of what it means and what to do with it. Information transfer is necessary but insufficient for alignment.
- How intent engineering constructs a shared mental model: INTENT.md is the artifact of SMM-building, not just a task specification. The 15-minute conversation is the SMM-building process. The document is what survives it.
- The quality of the SMM, not the quality of the task specification, determines alignment quality. An agent that executes a perfectly specified task with a poor mental model of why the task matters will fail at exactly the decision points the specification did not anticipate -- which is most decision points.
- This reframes what intent gathering is for: not to produce a document, but to build a shared model of what success looks like that persists through all the moments the document does not explicitly cover.

## Trust as Asymmetric and Incremental
Make the organizational psychology connection explicit:
- Trust is built through "competence credentialing": demonstrating reliable judgment in small, low-stakes actions before being trusted with high-stakes ones. This is how trust works in human teams and how it should work in agent-human collaboration.
- Trust is lost asymmetrically: one serious misalignment can undo many demonstrated successes. The loss function is steeper than the gain function. This is exactly what the detailed-design encodes: negative signal -0.10, positive +0.05. The math is right because the psychology is right.
- The cold-start to warm-start progression is correct for exactly this reason -- but the expansion of autonomy should be explicitly tied to demonstrated alignment in specific domains, not to time elapsed or session count. An agent that has demonstrated reliable judgment on writing tasks should earn higher autonomy for writing tasks, not for coding tasks. Domain specificity is essential.
- This means the agent should be able to articulate to itself and to the human: "I am operating with higher autonomy on this decision because we have established consistent alignment on similar decisions in this domain." Making the trust model visible makes it legible and improvable.

## Communication Load as a Tax on Flow
Argue for the optimal check-in cadence:
- Too many check-ins destroy flow, create dependency, and signal that the system cannot be trusted. Users start to disengage from the oversight role they are being asked to play -- precisely the opposite of the intended effect. An agent that checks in constantly trains the human to stop reading check-ins carefully.
- Too few check-ins produce silent drift until a large, expensive mismatch surfaces at delivery. The human who was not consulted at the moment of a key decision cannot retroactively provide the input that would have changed the outcome.
- The optimal check-in cadence is calibrated to task risk, not to agent uncertainty. An anxious agent that checks in when it feels uncertain imposes a hidden cost on users. The check-in decision should be driven by objective risk characteristics (reversibility, organizational impact) not by the agent's internal confidence state.
- Reference the "bring solutions not questions" principle: check-ins must always include the agent's current working model and proposed path. A check-in that says "I encountered X, what should I do?" is a failure. A check-in that says "I encountered X, I recommend Y for these reasons, I will proceed unless you object within Z" respects the human's time and demonstrates competence.

## Psychological Safety for Uncertainty and Escalation
Draw the parallel to team dynamics:
- In high-performing human teams, escalation culture matters enormously. If raising concerns is penalized -- explicitly through criticism or implicitly through disappointment -- people stop raising concerns. The concerns do not go away; they go underground.
- For agent systems: escalation must be framed as good judgment, not as failure. "I am flagging this because the cost of being wrong is high" is competence. Agents that project false confidence to avoid appearing to ask questions are more dangerous than agents that escalate appropriately.
- The complement to "bring solutions not questions" is: "acknowledge genuine uncertainty explicitly." These two principles are not in tension -- they define different situations. When the agent has enough information to form a recommendation, bring the recommendation. When the agent genuinely cannot form a defensible recommendation without human input, say so -- but say it with the specific information the human needs to decide.
- The spec's escalation model is structurally correct. The psychological framing in agent prompts matters as much as the model. An agent prompted to treat escalation as failure will suppress escalations that should surface.

## The Dunning-Kruger Trap
Make the argument that agents have this failure mode:
- Agents, like humans, are potentially most confident when operating in unfamiliar territory -- pattern-matching on superficially similar past experience. The most dangerous execution state is high confidence combined with low actual alignment.
- What makes this particularly dangerous in the POC architecture: the uber lead never sees raw file content, only JSON summaries. A liaison that confidently reports "chapter complete, high quality" based on a superficially plausible but actually misaligned output creates a false signal that propagates up the hierarchy.
- Systems need to calibrate confidence explicitly: not just "I am doing X" but "I am doing X with high confidence in the approach but I want to note that this is my first dispatch of this type in this domain -- the estimate carries more uncertainty than usual."
- This is the missing link between the escalation model and the memory system: confidence should be reported alongside recommendations, and surprises -- outcomes that contradict confident predictions -- should trigger immediate calibration updates, not just standard -0.10 signal recording. The magnitude of the surprise should influence the magnitude of the calibration.

## RACI and Decision Ownership
Connect to the POC multi-team architecture:
- RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) is the human-team equivalent of decision boundary mapping. In human organizations, RACI breakdowns -- unclear ownership, missing consultation, forgotten informing -- are the primary source of coordination failures in multi-team work.
- INTENT.md decision boundaries are an implicit RACI. Making it explicit reduces ambiguity for agents operating in parallel: "the writing liaison is Responsible for chapter production; the editorial liaison is Consulted before any structural changes; the uber lead is Accountable for cross-team coherence; the human is Informed of all deliverables."
- In the POC's two-level hierarchy, RACI breakdowns surface as: a subteam makes a decision that should have escalated to the uber lead; two liaisons make conflicting decisions because neither knew the other was deciding the same thing; a dispatch completes work that another dispatch had already done because the informing step was skipped.
- Making RACI explicit in the planning phase is not overhead -- it is the mechanism that prevents the coordination failures that are far more expensive to diagnose and repair.

## How Effective Human Teams Handle Ambiguity
Close with practical lessons:
- Effective teams clarify at the lowest-cost moment: before starting, not after investing effort. This is the planning-phase argument applied to human dynamics.
- They distinguish "I will figure it out" ambiguity (local, low-stakes, does not affect others) from "we need to decide" ambiguity (shared, high-stakes, affects multiple work streams). Agents should make this distinction explicit rather than treating all ambiguity as equally worthy of escalation.
- They use working agreements that cover edge cases, not just the happy path. INTENT.md does this with decision boundaries -- but the boundaries should include explicit edge case handling: "if you encounter X, here is how to handle it without escalating."
- They maintain a shared understanding of what "good enough" looks like, so individuals can make local quality judgments without constant calibration. The INTENT.md success criteria section serves this purpose -- but only if it is specific enough that an agent can apply it without further input.
