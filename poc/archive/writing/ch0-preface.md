## Chapter 0: Preface

The universe, it turns out, has more rooms than anyone suspected.

This is the Fourth Edition of the *Extradimensional Traveller's Handbook*, published by the Consortium for Manifold Navigation and Interchronal Safety (CMNIS) under authority SBY-0. It supersedes all previous editions and incorporates revisions mandated by the Novikov Accords (Revised), the UFP 9.2 update cycle, and no fewer than forty-seven incident reports that the Consortium would prefer had never been filed.

### A Brief History of Transit

The theoretical foundations of extradimensional travel were laid by Dr. Yelena Sovchak of the Meridian Institute in the year SBY-312, when she first proposed that the **Standard Manifold** — the familiar 4-dimensional (3+1) macroscopic spacetime in which practitioners live and work — was underlaid by seven additional spatial dimensions in a compactified state. Sovchak's landmark paper, *On the Navigable Topology of the Compressed Register*, described what we now call **Calabi-Yau fibers**: each of the 7 compactified spatial dimensions, possessing distinct topology that would, in time, prove to have profound consequences for transit behavior.

Practical stabilization of transit media came a century later. In SBY-211, the Harkon-Miele Laboratory achieved the first sustained production of **Casimir substrate** — exotic matter with negative energy density, generated via engineered Casimir cavity arrays — and used it to hold open the first **micropore**: a wormhole with a throat radius smaller than one millimeter. Early micropore transits were unmanned and largely destructive to the test objects involved, a fact that the Consortium notes with retrospective appreciation for the researchers' choice of test objects.

The first crewed micropore transit was completed in SBY-178 by Provisional Navigator Adaeze Okonkwo aboard the *ISV Threshold*. The transit displaced the vessel 14 kilometers and deposited the crew safely, if briefly, in a region of significant geological activity. Navigator Okonkwo's post-transit report remains required reading at the CMNIS Training Annex — particularly her closing observation that the rocks had not, in fact, moved.

The CMNIS itself was chartered in SBY-144, following the Dorchester Incident, in which three independent transit operators simultaneously attempted to stabilize overlapping micropore corridors and produced what physicists now term **manifold bleed**: vacuum energy leakage between adjacent manifold layers indicating an insufficiently sealed transit. The bleed event affected roughly four square kilometers of a populated district. The Consortium was, it is fair to say, formed with some urgency.

Temporal transit — the use of **closed timelike curves (CTCs)**, worldlines that return to their own spacetime origin — became practically achievable in SBY-97, following the theoretical resolution of the paradox suppression mechanism. Physicist Renna Faraji demonstrated that what prevents travelers from creating grandfather-style paradoxes is not technology, custom, or moral fiber, but physics: a phenomenon now called the **Novikov lock**, the quantum-mechanical suppression of paradox-producing CTC configurations. It is not a device one can bypass. This point has been tested. The Consortium has the paperwork.

The **Novikov Accords** were signed in SBY-12 by 34 member states and seven non-terrestrial signatory bodies. The Accords govern all civilian CTC operations: mandating chronal frame registration, prohibiting unescorted **temporal anchor** approaches, and establishing the incident-reporting framework that feeds directly into the edition you are now reading. The Accords have been revised four times; each revision correlates with an event that the original drafters believed could not happen.

### Audience

This handbook is written for certified practitioners holding at minimum a CMNIS Class II Transit Certification. It is not a research text, an engineering reference, or an introduction for the curious. If you are reading this without the appropriate certification, please close the handbook and contact a licensed training facility. If you are reading this *after* a transit that you were not qualified to conduct, the Consortium's Emergency Coordination desk is staffed continuously across all registered temporal frames.

### Scope and Limitations

This handbook covers operational procedures for micropore transit, **fold transit** (travel through a Calabi-Yau dimension by locally inflating its compactification radius), warp-metric vessel operation using the **Alcubierre shell**, CTC navigation, and **brane transit** between adjacent Dirichlet branes. It provides safety protocols, instrument guidance, and regulatory reference under the **Unified Field Protocol (UFP)**, current revision 9.2.

This handbook does not contain engineering schematics for transit hardware, advanced research theory, or classified **Manifold Index (Mₓ)** coordinates. Requests for such materials should be directed to the appropriate CMNIS divisional office, accompanied by valid authorization credentials and, if applicable, a reasonable explanation.

⚠️ **WARNING:** Operating outside your certified transit parameters — including unauthorized fold depths, unregistered CTC departures, and micropore stabilization without confirmed Casimir substrate reserves — constitutes a violation of UFP 9.2 and automatically voids any CMNIS indemnity coverage. The Consortium does not retroactively extend coverage to incidents that began with the phrase "it should be fine."

### A Note on Temporal Sequence

The Consortium is aware that some practitioners will read this preface after having already completed one or more transits described in later chapters — perhaps following a retrograde CTC return, perhaps simply because they read handbooks from the back. We have done our best to make each section useful regardless of the order in which it is encountered. If you find a procedure that appears to contradict your recent experience, please consult Chapter 9 (Anomalous Recall and Chronal Frame Discrepancy) before concluding that the handbook is wrong.

We are glad you are here. We are glad you are here in one piece. Please read the safety chapters before you need them.

*— CMNIS Editorial Board, SBY-0*
