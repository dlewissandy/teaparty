## Chapter 8: Safety Protocols & Hazards

### 8.1 Hazard Classification System

All transit hazards recognized under UFP 9.2 are assigned a class from 1 to 5 based on two criteria: the severity of harm to personnel and equipment, and whether the condition is reversible if addressed promptly. Operators must be able to identify a hazard's class immediately upon instrument alert. Response protocols are class-specific and non-negotiable.

**Table 8.1 — Hazard Classification**
| Class | Hazard | Severity | Reversible? | Response |
|-------|--------|----------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Minor chronal shear | Minor injury | Yes | Medical monitoring |
| 2 | Manifold bleed (early) | Moderate | Yes (if caught early) | Abort + report to CMNIS |
| 3 | Spatial lock failure | Serious | Yes (if aborted before transit) | Abort transit immediately |
| 4 | Major chronal shear | Serious | Partial | Evacuate + full tensor shielding |
| 4 | Micropore collapse | Fatal | No | Prevention only |
| 5 | Decoherence cascade | Fatal | No | Prevention only |
| 5 | Warp bubble integrity failure | Fatal | No | Prevention only |

Class 4 and Class 5 hazards share the defining characteristic that once they have fully materialized, no intervention recovers the situation. The distinction between Classes 4 and 5 is that Class 4 fatalities are equipment-dependent — adequate shielding or timely evacuation may avert them — while Class 5 events are inherently unrecoverable regardless of available resources. Prevention is the only acceptable response to Class 5 hazards.

### 8.2 Manifold Bleed: Causes, Symptoms, Containment

**Manifold bleed** is the leakage of vacuum energy between adjacent manifold layers caused by incomplete portal sealing or degradation of the **Casimir substrate** — the exotic matter with negative energy density required to stabilize wormhole apertures — lining the throat of a micropore. It is classified as a Class 2 hazard when detected early and a Class 4 hazard if allowed to propagate.

**Causes.** Manifold bleed arises from three primary sources: insufficient Casimir substrate concentration in the portal ring, mechanical damage to the portal frame distorting the throat geometry, and residual energy from a transit that was not cleanly closed. Repeated rapid cycling of a micropore without allowing full vacuum equilibration between transits is a common operational cause.

**Symptoms.** The primary instrument indicator is an elevated vacuum energy reading at the portal perimeter, typically above 0.03 anomalous units on a calibrated vacuum spectrometer. Secondary indicators include a faint luminescent fringe at the portal edge, thermal anomalies in the surrounding transit bay, and — in advanced cases — audible distortion consistent with resonance between adjacent manifold layers. Personnel in the transit bay may report mild spatial disorientation or anomalous peripheral vision, symptoms that must be taken seriously and not dismissed.

**Containment.** Containment is time-sensitive. A bleed that is caught within the first three minutes can be closed with a Casimir substrate purge; one that has progressed to full-layer contact requires facility evacuation.

📋 **PROCEDURE 8-B: Manifold Bleed Containment Protocol**
1. Confirm vacuum energy reading above 0.03 anomalous units.
2. Halt all active transits in facility.
3. Isolate affected portal with field containment ring.
4. Deploy Casimir substrate purge at bleed point.
5. Monitor vacuum energy reading every 30 seconds.
6. If reading does not decrease within 5 minutes, evacuate and contact CMNIS Hazard Control.
7. Do not re-enter facility until CMNIS inspection clearance received.

**Reporting.** All manifold bleed events — including those resolved successfully without evacuation — must be reported to CMNIS within 24 hours under UFP 9.2 Section 14. See Section 8.9 for reporting requirements.

### 8.3 Chronal Shear: Exposure Limits, Shielding, Treatment

**Chronal shear** is the tidal force experienced by any object that spans a temporal gradient large enough to impose differential chronal displacement across its physical extent. At sufficient magnitude, chronal shear is fatal. Exposure classes are determined by the temporal displacement gradient encountered, not by the absolute destination date.

**Class 1 (less than 10-year displacement gradient, no shielding required).** Travelers typically experience mild disorientation, transient nausea, and minor joint pain. No lasting physical damage is expected. Medical monitoring for 12 hours post-transit is required. No specialized shielding is mandated, though standard transit suits provide incidental protection.

**Class 2 (10 to 25-year gradient, standard transit suit required).** Without a standard chronal-rated transit suit, exposure at this level produces soft-tissue microhemorrhage and temporary cognitive disruption. With a suit, effects are reduced to Class 1 equivalents. Personnel exposed without a suit at Class 2 levels require immediate medical evaluation and a minimum 48-hour recovery observation period.

**Class 3 (25 to 50-year gradient, full tensor shielding required).** Class 3 exposure without tensor shielding is acutely dangerous. Symptoms include severe internal hemorrhage, acute neurological disruption, and organ stress sufficient to cause fatality in approximately 30% of unshielded cases. Full tensor shielding reduces the effective exposure to Class 1 levels. Medical treatment for Class 3 exposure without shielding requires hospitalization and may require chronal desynchronization therapy.

**Class 4 (greater than 50-year gradient, experimental shielding only).** At this level, chronal shear exceeds the rated protection of standard tensor shields. Experimental and prototype shielding systems exist and are operated under special CMNIS research licenses, but none carry a general safety certification. Unshielded Class 4 exposure is fatal. This class of transit is not authorized for commercial or personal use under current UFP standards. Researchers operating under Class 4 conditions must have individual medical waivers, dual independent shielding systems, and real-time monitoring.

### 8.4 Decoherence Cascade

A **decoherence cascade** is the progressive collapse of quantum superposition states within a traveler's biological and neurological systems, caused by unshielded crossing of a **Cauchy horizon** — the boundary beyond which predictability breaks down in a **closed timelike curve (CTC)** spacetime. It is classified as Class 5. There is no treatment. There is no recovery.

**Causes.** The Cauchy horizon forms at the boundary of the CTC's temporal loop. Properly shielded travelers cross this boundary without incident; the **chronal frame** assignment system is specifically designed to stabilize the quantum state of the traveler during this crossing. Unshielded crossing — whether due to shield failure, unauthorized transit, or improper chronal frame assignment — exposes the traveler's quantum state to the full decoherence environment of the horizon.

**Symptoms.** Decoherence cascade does not present as conventional injury. The traveler initially experiences spatial and temporal disorientation, a sensation of existing at multiple points simultaneously, and progressive inability to form coherent memories. These symptoms escalate. Within minutes of unshielded horizon crossing, the traveler loses temporal synchronization entirely. They are, in the clinical sense, no longer located at any single point in spacetime. This state is irreversible.

**Prevention.** Verify chronal frame assignment before every CTC transit. Confirm shield integrity against Cauchy horizon specifications, not merely standard chronal shear ratings — they are not the same measurement. Never proceed with a CTC transit on a degraded shield system. The Novikov lock prevents paradox; it does not protect the traveler from their own equipment failures.

### 8.5 Micropore Collapse Mid-Transit

A **micropore** is a wormhole with a throat radius under one millimeter, stabilized by Casimir substrate. Micropore collapse during transit — while a living organism occupies the throat — is classified as Class 4/5 depending on whether the organism has entered the throat. A collapse with the organism clear of the throat is Class 4 (equipment loss, potential injury from energy discharge). A collapse with the organism in the throat is Class 5.

**Probability.** Under nominal operating conditions with fully rated Casimir substrate reserves at 100% specification, the probability of spontaneous micropore collapse is approximately 1 in 10⁷ transits. This number rises sharply with degraded substrate: at 80% reserve, the failure probability increases by a factor of approximately 40. At 60% reserve, it increases by a factor of over 400. Substrate degradation is not linear. It is not safe to operate at reduced reserves and assume proportionally reduced risk.

**Casimir substrate reserve requirements.** UFP 9.2 mandates a minimum of 120% of calculated transit requirement in Casimir substrate before initiating any micropore transit. The 20% margin is not a comfort buffer. It accounts for real-time substrate consumption variability during transit, thermal fluctuation in the throat geometry, and the reserve needed to execute an emergency closure if a transit must be aborted mid-sequence. Operating below 120% reserve is a UFP violation. Operating below 100% reserve is prohibited under any circumstances.

⚠️ **WARNING:** A micropore collapse with an organism in the throat has zero recovery options. There is no procedure, no intervention, and no second chance. Inspect your Casimir substrate reserves before every transit without exception.

The transit record system logs substrate levels at initiation. If an investigation following an incident reveals that a transit was initiated below minimum reserve levels, the operator bears full liability for the outcome. Post-incident review cannot undo a collapse.

### 8.6 Spatial Lock Failure

**Spatial lock** is the quantum-entanglement targeting procedure that fixes exit coordinates for micropore transit. Without a confirmed spatial lock, the micropore exit point is indeterminate. A failed spatial lock before transit begins is a Class 3 hazard and is recoverable by aborting. A spatial lock failure after transit initiation has begun is a different and more dangerous situation.

**Detection before transit.** The spatial lock confirmation indicator must display a positive lock status before any transit is initiated. An indeterminate or failed lock reading is an immediate abort condition. Do not proceed on a partial lock. Do not proceed on a degraded lock signal that "seems close enough." The exit coordinates will not be close enough.

**Abort criteria.** If the lock status has not achieved full confirmation within the standard targeting window — typically 90 seconds from initial targeting — abort the transit and diagnose the targeting system. Common causes include interference from local gravitational anomalies, Casimir substrate contamination in the targeting array, or miscalibrated quantum entanglement between the entrance and exit portal rings.

**Recovery if transit has already begun.** If a spatial lock failure is detected after the transit sequence has initiated but before the traveler has entered the throat, the sequence must be aborted immediately using the Universal Abort Sequence (Procedure 8-A). If the traveler has partially entered the throat and lock failure is detected, the situation is critical. The abort sequence must still be executed. The traveler's position within the throat determines whether closure is possible without harm; the automated safety systems will calculate this in real time. Follow the automated system's guidance without delay.

### 8.7 Warp Bubble Integrity Failure

The **Alcubierre shell** — the exotic-matter bubble wall of a warp-metric vessel — contains the warp field that permits FTL transit. The shell is maintained by continuous Casimir substrate injection into the bubble boundary. If shell integrity fails during active warp transit, the warp field collapses instantaneously. The contents of the bubble, including the vessel and all occupants, experience the full energy differential of the collapse simultaneously. This is Class 5. There is no recovery.

The physics of the failure mode make intervention impossible. The collapse timescale is below any response threshold achievable by either automated systems or human operators. By the time an integrity failure is measurable, it has already occurred.

**Prevention is the only acceptable framework for this hazard.** Warp bubble integrity monitoring must be continuous throughout transit, with real-time telemetry logged and independently verified by at least two separate sensor systems. Pre-transit integrity verification must confirm shell uniformity to within 0.01% variation across the entire bubble boundary; asymmetric shell geometry is a precursor condition for integrity failure under field stress. Casimir substrate flow rates must be confirmed stable before transit initiation and monitored continuously during transit.

Any anomalous reading in shell integrity sensors during transit requires immediate assessment. There is no safe threshold for anomalous readings. If the integrity monitoring systems cannot be verified, the transit must not proceed.

### 8.8 Emergency Procedures

When a hazard condition is detected, the Universal Abort Sequence is the first response regardless of the specific hazard type. Hazard-specific procedures follow after the abort sequence is complete. Do not attempt to diagnose the hazard before executing the abort. Diagnose after.

📋 **PROCEDURE 8-A: Universal Abort Sequence**
1. Immediately halt transit initiation or signal abort to automated systems.
2. Secure Casimir substrate reserves (close injection valves).
3. Initiate micropore closure sequence if portal is open.
4. Activate emergency beacon.
5. Record current chronal frame and Manifold Index.
6. Assess hazard class of triggering condition.
7. Evacuate transit zone per CMNIS evacuation protocol.
8. File incident report within 4 hours.

**Beacon activation.** The emergency beacon transmits the facility's **Manifold Index (Mₓ)** — the coordinate triplet identifying the specific brane-separated manifold of the facility — along with the current chronal frame and the hazard class code. CMNIS Hazard Control monitors beacon frequencies across registered manifolds. Response time varies by manifold accessibility and hazard class; Class 5 events trigger automatic priority dispatch.

**Manifold anchor deployment.** In the event of a transit that has entered an uncontrolled state, deploying a manifold anchor fixes the chronal coordinates of the transit endpoint to prevent further drift. Anchor deployment is a secondary procedure, executed after the abort sequence is complete and only if personnel are confirmed clear of the transit zone.

### 8.9 Incident Reporting Requirements

All incidents involving hazard Class 2 and above must be reported to CMNIS under UFP 9.2 Section 14. The reporting requirement applies regardless of outcome — a contained Class 2 manifold bleed that was successfully purged must still be reported. The purpose of mandatory reporting is not punitive; it is to maintain the CMNIS hazard database that informs safety standards for all operators.

**What must be reported.** The incident report must include: the facility Manifold Index, the date and chronal frame of the incident, the hazard class and type, the sequence of events leading to the hazard condition, the response actions taken, the outcome, the identity of all personnel involved, and the current status of any affected equipment. For Class 4 and Class 5 events, a full equipment telemetry record must be attached.

**Reporting timeline.** Initial reports must be filed within 4 hours of incident containment or resolution. If the incident is ongoing, a preliminary report must be filed within 4 hours of detection. A full investigative report is due within 30 days. For Class 5 events, CMNIS will assign an independent investigator; the operator is required to cooperate fully and preserve all physical evidence and telemetry records without alteration.

**Consequences of failure to report.** Failure to file a required incident report within the mandated timeline is a UFP violation subject to fines and license suspension. Failure to report a Class 4 or Class 5 incident — or reporting with material omissions — carries criminal liability under the Novikov Accords framework, regardless of whether the incident caused harm to third parties. Operators who discover that a previous incident was unreported or incorrectly reported are required to file a corrective report; voluntary correction carries substantially reduced penalties compared to discovery by CMNIS investigation.

## Chapter 9: Regulations & Ethics

### 9.1 The Novikov Accords

The Novikov Accords constitute the foundational international treaty framework governing all **closed timelike curve (CTC)** operations, warp-metric transit, and manifold transit. The Accords were signed in SBY-0 − 15 by 47 nations and have since been ratified by all recognized interchronal jurisdictions.

📖 **HISTORICAL NOTE:** The Novikov Accords (SBY-0 − 15) were signed by 47 nations following the Threnody Incident, in which an unlicensed retrograde traveler attempted — and physically could not, due to Novikov lock — alter the outcome of a regional conflict, but caused significant Casimir substrate contamination across a 40-square-kilometer area. The incident established the legal precedent that Novikov-consistent harm is still harm, and that the practitioner bears liability regardless of whether the intended outcome was achieved.

The Threnody Incident resolved a question that had been debated since the theoretical foundations of CTC transit were established: if a traveler physically cannot produce a paradox because **Novikov lock** — the quantum-mechanical suppression of paradox-producing CTC configurations — prevents it, does the attempt carry legal weight? The Accords answered definitively: yes. The law treats the attempt and the harm caused in its execution as the practitioner's responsibility. The fact that the intended outcome was physically impossible does not eliminate liability for the damage caused in the attempt.

**Key provisions.** The Accords establish four primary regulatory structures. First, mandatory licensing for all temporal and manifold transit operations, with license classes scaled to transit type (see Section 9.2). Second, a framework of prohibited temporal zones — **Cauchy horizon**-adjacent epochs, bootstrap protection zones, and designated historical preservation periods — within which transit is criminally prohibited. Third, a paradox liability framework that assigns legal responsibility for all Novikov-consistent harms to the initiating practitioner (see Section 9.3). Fourth, the establishment of the Chronal Patrol as the interchronal enforcement authority operating across temporal and manifold boundaries (see Section 9.6).

The Accords are a living document under continuous revision through the CMNIS treaty process. The current operative revision is paired with UFP 9.2.

### 9.2 Licensing Requirements by Transit Class

Licensing under the Novikov Accords is tiered by transit complexity and risk profile. Each level requires the prerequisites of all levels below it.

**Level 1 — Spatial Transposition (Short Range).** Covers micropore transit for spatial displacement without temporal component, within the same manifold, over distances up to 10,000 km. Prerequisites: completion of CMNIS Basic Transit Safety certification, documented training in spatial lock procedures and Casimir substrate handling, and facility registration with CMNIS. No minimum age requirement above the age of legal majority in the issuing jurisdiction.

**Level 2 — Warp-Metric Transit.** Covers Alcubierre-class warp bubble transit for superluminal spatial displacement. Prerequisites: Level 1 license in good standing for a minimum of 12 months, completion of the CMNIS Warp Operations certification, documented practical hours on a rated simulator, and completion of at least 10 supervised transits with a licensed Level 2 or higher operator. Warp-metric vessels require separate CMNIS vessel registration.

**Level 3 — Temporal Transit, Forward Only.** Covers transit along a CTC worldline in the forward temporal direction. Prerequisites: Level 2 license in good standing for a minimum of 24 months, completion of the CMNIS Temporal Operations — Forward certification, documented completion of chronal frame assignment training, and a psychological fitness evaluation by a CMNIS-approved assessor. Forward temporal transit carries Novikov Accords liability provisions from point of licensing.

**Level 4 — Temporal Transit, Retrograde.** Covers transit along a CTC worldline in the retrograde temporal direction — travel into the past relative to the traveler's origin epoch. Prerequisites: Level 3 license in good standing for a minimum of 36 months, completion of the CMNIS Temporal Operations — Retrograde certification, a mandatory review of the Novikov lock's scope and limitations, and a CMNIS board review of the applicant's stated operational purpose. Retrograde licenses are issued on a per-purpose basis and must be renewed for each operational scope change. The Chronal Patrol maintains independent notification rights over all retrograde license issuances.

**Level 5 — Manifold and Brane Transit.** Covers **brane transit** — passage between adjacent Dirichlet branes — and all transit that crosses manifold boundaries. Prerequisites: Level 4 license, completion of the CMNIS Manifold Operations certification, documented training in Manifold Index navigation, and a formal ethical review under CMNIS Ethics Standard 7 (see Section 9.4). Level 5 licenses require annual renewal with a review of all transits conducted in the prior year.

### 9.3 Paradox Liability

Novikov lock is an absolute physical law. A CTC traveler cannot produce a causal paradox; any retrograde action they take will be Novikov-consistent with the history that produced their departure. This is not a legal protection. It is not a defense. The Novikov Accords are unambiguous: the practitioner who initiates a CTC transit bears full liability for all harms arising from the execution of that transit, whether or not the intended outcome was achievable.

The logic is straightforward. If a retrograde traveler attempts an action — regardless of whether Novikov lock prevents its completion — and that attempt causes physical damage, personal injury, or manifold contamination, the practitioner has caused harm. The impossibility of the intended outcome does not reduce the reality of the actual harm. The Threnody Incident was the definitive case establishing this principle.

**The CMNIS liability framework.** Harms arising from CTC transit are adjudicated under the Novikov Accords liability framework, which assigns primary responsibility to the license holder who initiated the transit. Responsibility does not transfer to the Novikov lock, to the equipment manufacturer, or to the destination-epoch entities whose actions were the target of the transit. The initiating practitioner is the responsible party.

**Insurance requirements.** All Level 3 and above license holders are required to carry Novikov-consistent harm liability insurance at minimum coverage levels set by the Accords and reviewed at each UFP revision. Coverage must include third-party property damage, personal injury liability, environmental contamination (including Casimir substrate dispersal), and interchronal claims. Facilities offering temporal transit services to the public are required to carry facility-level coverage in addition to the coverage carried by individual operators.

### 9.4 Ethics of Branching Manifold Transit

CTC transit and manifold transit are not the same activity. They share infrastructure and some operational procedures, but they are categorically different from an ethical standpoint, and the Novikov Accords and CMNIS Ethics Standard 7 treat them accordingly.

**CTC transit** takes place within a single manifold. The **Standard Manifold** — the 4-dimensional macroscopic spacetime — is not branched by CTC transit. The traveler departs, moves along a closed timelike curve, arrives at an earlier point in their own timeline, and exists in a Novikov-consistent loop. No new manifold is created. The traveler who departed and the traveler who arrives are the same person in the same spacetime. The past-epoch people they encounter are real. Their own past self is real. The ethical obligations of behavior in the past epoch are identical to those in any other period of one's own timeline.

**Manifold transit** is different in kind. Per R3, a branching transit produces genuinely separate manifolds. The origin timeline — the **Standard Manifold** from which the traveler departed — is not affected by anything done in the destination manifold. But the destination manifold is real. Its inhabitants are real. Their futures are genuinely shaped by the traveler's presence and actions.

💡 **NOTE:** You cannot change your own past. Novikov lock is an absolute physical law, not a regulatory one. What you CAN do is travel to an adjacent manifold and make choices there — but those choices affect only that manifold (R3). This distinction is the basis of CMNIS Ethics Standard 7, which treats CTC transit and manifold transit as categorically different activities with different ethical frameworks.

**Obligations to origin-manifold inhabitants.** When a traveler departs for an adjacent manifold M₁, their origin manifold M₀ continues to exist with them absent. This is not meaningfully different from any long journey; the ethical obligations of the traveler to dependents, communities, and commitments in M₀ are not suspended by the physics of branching transit.

**Obligations to destination-manifold inhabitants.** The traveler arrives in M₁ as an entity whose presence was not part of the manifold's prior causal history. CMNIS Ethics Standard 7 establishes that this status does not grant license. The traveler carries full moral responsibility for actions taken in M₁. The fact that M₁ is "not your timeline" is not an ethical exemption. Rational Ethics Standard 7 frames the obligation simply: treat the inhabitants of every manifold as you would treat the inhabitants of your own.

### 9.5 Restricted Epochs

Certain chronal periods are designated as restricted or prohibited under the Novikov Accords. Transit into these periods — whether by CTC retrograde travel or, where technically possible, by other means — is a criminal offense regardless of license class.

**Bootstrap protection zones.** The period encompassing the theoretical development and first practical demonstrations of temporal and manifold transit is designated a bootstrap protection zone. Transit into this period is prohibited because the period is causally upstream of the technology itself. Any interference — Novikov-consistent or otherwise — during this period carries the risk of altering the causal chain that produced the technology. The Novikov lock will prevent direct paradox, but the indirect effects of a traveler's presence in a bootstrap zone can still affect the course of events in ways that harm the timeline without producing a clean paradox. The bootstrap zone boundaries are classified; operators may request a consultation with CMNIS to determine whether a target epoch falls within a restricted zone.

**Historical preservation zones.** Designated periods of significant historical or cultural importance may be placed under temporal preservation orders by petition to the CMNIS treaty body. Transit into these zones for observational, research, or commercial purposes requires a specific permit issued by the CMNIS Historical Access Committee. Interference with events in a historical preservation zone carries enhanced penalties.

**Cauchy horizon-adjacent periods.** The temporal neighborhoods of known Cauchy horizons in the manifold record are restricted due to the elevated risk of decoherence cascade for travelers and the potential for manifold bleed between horizon layers. These restrictions are maintained regardless of operator shielding capability.

**Enforcement.** The Chronal Patrol maintains operational awareness of restricted zone boundaries and has standing authority to intercept and return transits that approach restricted epoch boundaries.

### 9.6 Interchronal Border Enforcement

The Chronal Patrol is the enforcement body established by the Novikov Accords with jurisdiction across all temporal and manifold boundaries recognized by treaty signatories. It operates under a mandate that explicitly transcends the territorial jurisdiction of any single nation or epoch, a structural necessity for a body whose operational scope spans both time and manifold space.

**Detection.** The Chronal Patrol maintains a network of temporal signature detectors distributed across the treaty-member chronal landscape. All licensed transit operations register a recognizable transit signature that identifies the operator's license class, the Manifold Index of origin, and the declared destination. Unlicensed transit produces a signature that lacks these identifiers. The absence of a license signature in a monitored zone triggers an automatic alert.

Detection of transits into restricted epochs is a standing priority. Patrol response times vary by zone proximity and available resources, but the enforcement framework does not require real-time interception to proceed with prosecution; temporal forensics allow for reconstruction of transit events after the fact, and the Patrol has statutory authority to initiate proceedings against operators identified through post-event investigation.

**Jurisdictional framework.** Jurisdictional questions in interchronal law are among the most complex in the legal field, and the Novikov Accords represent an ongoing negotiation among member jurisdictions rather than a fully resolved framework. For the operator's practical purposes: if you are licensed by a treaty signatory, the Accords apply to your operations regardless of where or when you conduct them. The destination epoch's legal framework may apply simultaneously. Consult legal counsel with interchronal jurisdiction expertise before conducting operations in epochs or manifolds with uncertain legal status.

### 9.7 Penalties for Unlicensed Transit

Operating temporal or manifold transit without the appropriate license class constitutes a violation of the Novikov Accords and, in all treaty-signatory jurisdictions, a criminal offense under domestic law.

**Fines.** Base fines for unlicensed transit are scaled by transit class: Level 1 equivalent operations attract base fines in the range of 10,000 to 50,000 Standard Units. Level 4 and Level 5 equivalent operations without license can attract fines exceeding 1,000,000 Standard Units per transit event. Fines are assessed per transit, not per episode; multiple transits in a single unlicensed operation multiply accordingly.

**License suspension and revocation.** Licensed operators who conduct transits beyond their license class, or who assist unlicensed operators, face suspension for a first offense and revocation for a second. A revoked license may not be reapplied for within 10 years, and the revocation record is permanent in the CMNIS operator registry, which is accessible to all treaty signatories.

**Criminal liability for harm.** Where unlicensed transit results in harm to persons, property, or manifold integrity, the operator faces criminal liability under both the Novikov Accords framework and applicable domestic law. The Accords impose enhanced penalties when the harm arises from an unlicensed operation, recognizing that the absence of proper training and certification is itself a contributing factor to harm caused.

**Casimir substrate contamination.** Environmental remediation requirements for Casimir substrate contamination are strict and expensive. An operator responsible for contamination — whether licensed or unlicensed — is liable for the full cost of CMNIS-supervised remediation, which in cases of significant dispersal can be substantial. Unlicensed operators who cause contamination face the additional criminal penalties described above. The environmental liability does not discharge through criminal sentencing; remediation costs remain the operator's obligation regardless of the outcome of criminal proceedings.

The Novikov Accords do not distinguish between operators who intended harm and operators who caused harm through ignorance or negligence. The regulatory framework is premised on the availability of training and certification. An operator who chose not to obtain that training bears the consequences of the choice.
