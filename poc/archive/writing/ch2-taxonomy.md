## Chapter 2: Dimensional Taxonomy

This chapter provides the authoritative dimensional reference for all transit operations governed by the Unified Field Protocol (UFP), CMNIS revision 9.2. The taxonomy presented here classifies all eleven known dimensions by type, natural scale, navigability, and energy requirements. Practitioners should consult this chapter before planning any non-Class N transit.

---

### 2.1 The Standard Manifold (Dimensions 1–4)

The **Standard Manifold** is the 4-dimensional (3+1) macroscopic spacetime inhabited by the practitioner. It consists of three unbounded spatial dimensions (x, y, z) and one temporal dimension (t), and represents the default operational environment for all registered travelers. All other dimensional structures described in this handbook exist either embedded within, compactified beneath, or adjacent to the Standard Manifold.

#### Spatial Dimensions (1–3)

Dimensions x, y, and z are unbounded in extent and symmetric under rotation. Traversal in any spatial direction requires no specialized equipment beyond conventional means of locomotion. These dimensions are assigned **Navigability Class N** (Natural Transit). Energy costs are purely mechanical — kinetic and gravitational potential energy — and no CMNIS certification specific to dimensional transit is required. Standard spaceflight certification covers all Class N operations.

Coordinate conventions in the Standard Manifold follow the CMNIS Unified Metric Framework: all spatial coordinates are expressed in meters relative to a mission-specified origin, with epoch synchronized to CMNIS Standard Time before departure. When transiting through any other dimensional class and returning to the Standard Manifold, chronal frame synchronization must be verified before resuming Class N navigation.

#### Temporal Dimension (4)

Dimension t occupies a unique position in the taxonomy. It is macroscopic and unbounded — like x, y, and z — but it is asymmetric. The thermodynamic arrow of time enforces unidirectional forward motion under normal conditions: all objects advance through dimension t at exactly 1 second per second in their rest frame, without exception and without energy expenditure.

Forward transit in dimension t is therefore passive and automatic. No practitioner action is required, and no CMNIS certification governs forward temporal motion beyond ordinary spaceflight operations.

Retrograde transit in dimension t requires an entirely different mechanism and is addressed in detail in Section 2.3.

**Instrument Note (R5):** All instruments, including clocks, continue operating normally during any dimensional transit. Clocks are not suspended, reset, or altered by transit events. Any apparent clock discrepancy on return is a genuine elapsed-time difference, not instrument error, and must be reconciled by the chronal frame record (see Section 2.3).

### 2.2 The Compressed Register (Dimensions 5–11)

The **Compressed Register** comprises the seven compactified spatial dimensions occupying indices 5 through 11. Unlike the macroscopic spatial dimensions, these dimensions are not absent from our universe — they are compactified: curled into loops at a characteristic radius of approximately 10⁻³⁵ meters (near the Planck length), far below the threshold of macroscopic detectability by any conventional instrument.

Each dimension in the Compressed Register corresponds to a **Calabi-Yau fiber**: one of the seven compactified spatial dimensions with its own distinct topological character. The seven fibers are designated α through η. Each fiber possesses a unique Calabi-Yau geometry with distinct curvature, holonomy group, and permitted particle modes. These topological differences have direct consequences for transit behavior, transit energy cost, and the types of physical interactions available within each fiber during transit.

#### Ambient vs. Inflated State

In their ambient state, the Calabi-Yau fibers cannot accommodate macroscopic objects. A fiber at compactification radius 10⁻³⁵ m presents no accessible volume for an object of any biologically relevant scale. Transit into a Calabi-Yau fiber therefore requires local inflation: the compactification radius must be temporarily expanded to macroscopic scale in the immediate vicinity of the traveler and their vessel.

This inflation is accomplished by injecting **Casimir substrate** — exotic matter with negative energy density produced via engineered Casimir cavity arrays — into the fiber junction. The Casimir substrate generates the negative energy density required to locally deform the compactification geometry, expanding the fiber to traversable radius for the duration of transit. This process is called a **fold transit**: travel through a Calabi-Yau dimension accomplished by locally inflating its compactification radius.

The number of Calabi-Yau dimensions through which a fold transit passes is the **fold depth**. Fold depth determines energy cost directly, and the scaling is exponential in accordance with R4:

- Fold depth 1 (α-fiber): 2.1 × 10⁴⁴ J/kg
- Fold depth 2 (β-fiber): 4.4 × 10⁸⁸ J/kg

Each additional fold depth multiplies the energy requirement by a factor of order 10⁴⁴, placing fold depths above 2 outside the range of any currently certified civilian vessel. The α- and β-fibers are the only fibers assigned **Navigability Class F** (Fold-Transit Accessible). CMNIS Level 3 certification is the minimum required for Class F operations.

All instruments continue operating normally during fold transit (R5). Transit duration within folded space is measurable, and departure and arrival times are logged in the mission chronal record.

#### Fibers 5–8 (α through δ)

The α- and β-fibers have been confirmed traversable and are assigned Class F. The γ- and δ-fibers require Casimir injection above Class F thresholds — energy costs exceed 10¹¹⁰ J/kg — placing them in **Navigability Class R** (Restricted Transit). No civilian-rated equipment currently achieves Class R fold transit for γ or δ. These fibers are included in the taxonomy for theoretical planning and completeness.

#### Fibers 9–11 (ε through η)

Dimensions ε, ζ, and η are assigned **Navigability Class X** (Theoretically Inaccessible). No confirmed transit has been recorded as of Fourth Edition (SBY-0). Theoretical models suggest that ζ-fiber transit may require brane-level energy densities. The η-fiber occupies a special position in the taxonomy: it is theorized to be the boundary surface of our Dirichlet brane, and transit into η is believed to constitute entry into the brane boundary structure itself, making it conceptually distinct from fiber transit in the usual sense.

All Class X research is restricted to CMNIS Division 7 (theoretical operations).

### 2.3 Dimension 4 (Time): Special Status

As noted in Section 2.1, dimension t is macroscopic and unbounded but asymmetric. The thermodynamic arrow of time produces a fundamental directional bias: entropy increases in the forward direction, and ordinary physics does not spontaneously permit retrograde displacement.

#### Closed Timelike Curves

The sole confirmed mechanism for retrograde temporal transit is the **closed timelike curve (CTC)**: a worldline that returns to its own spacetime origin. A CTC is not a device; it is a geometric feature of sufficiently curved spacetime, producible (at large energy cost) by controlled mass-energy distribution. CTC formation is the exclusive province of Class R temporal operations and requires CMNIS Level 5 certification.

Retrograde displacement via CTC costs approximately 10¹⁸ J per year of retrograde displacement. Forward temporal transit — the normal, passive advancement of any object through time — requires no energy expenditure beyond ordinary locomotion.

#### Temporal Anchor and Chronal Frame

Every CTC transit establishes a **temporal anchor**: the chronal coordinate fixed by the CTC departure event. The temporal anchor is the earliest moment reachable by a traveler on a given CTC worldline. It cannot be circumvented; no transit on the same CTC can reach a time prior to the departure event that created the loop.

Throughout a CTC transit, the traveler's position in time is tracked using a **chronal frame**: the temporal reference frame assigned to and maintained by CMNIS-certified equipment for the duration of the transit. The chronal frame record is mandatory for all Class R temporal operations and must be reconciled with Standard Manifold time on return.

#### Novikov Lock

The **Novikov lock** is the quantum-mechanical suppression of paradox-producing CTC configurations. It is not a device, a protocol, or an engineering constraint — it is a law of physics. The Novikov lock operates by suppressing the probability amplitude of any CTC worldline configuration that would prevent the events enabling the traveler's departure. Practitioners cannot alter the past events that made their CTC departure possible (R2). Equipment rated for Class R temporal operations is designed with the assumption that the Novikov lock holds; no certified CMNIS procedure requires or anticipates paradox resolution.

#### Chronal Shear

**Chronal shear** is the tidal force experienced by an object that spans a large temporal gradient — that is, an object large enough, or poorly shielded enough, that different parts of it experience significantly different temporal coordinates simultaneously. Chronal shear is a primary hazard of improperly executed CTCs. It is rated by class: Class 1 and 2 shear are tolerable for short durations with standard temporal shielding; Class 3 and above is fatal without dedicated shielding equipment. All CMNIS Level 5 certified vessels include Class 4 temporal shielding as a minimum requirement.

#### Cauchy Horizon and Decoherence Cascade

The **Cauchy horizon** is the boundary in a CTC spacetime beyond which predictability breaks down — the surface at which the self-consistency conditions of the Novikov lock become active. Crossing an unshielded Cauchy horizon can induce a **decoherence cascade**: a quantum superposition collapse event in which the traveler's quantum state entangles destructively with the branching structure of the CTC. Decoherence cascades are not survivable without Cauchy shielding. All CMNIS transit vessels are equipped with Cauchy shielding as a standard fitout requirement.

⚠️ **WARNING:** Any attempt to breach the temporal anchor — to travel to a time earlier than the CTC departure point — is physically impossible under Novikov lock, but equipment rated below Class 4 temporal shielding may sustain decoherence cascade while attempting to approach the anchor boundary. Do not approach within 72 hours of the anchor.

### 2.4 Navigability Classes

The following classes appear in dimension tables throughout this handbook and in all CMNIS transit permits and vessel certifications. Each class specifies the equipment, certification, and energy requirements for transit.

---

**Class N — Natural Transit**

No special equipment is required beyond standard means of locomotion. Class N applies to dimensions x, y, and z. Energy costs are negligible — conventional kinetic and gravitational potential energy only. No CMNIS certification specific to dimensional transit is required for Class N; standard spaceflight certification applies.

---

**Class F — Fold-Transit Accessible**

Class F requires local inflation of a Calabi-Yau fiber via Casimir substrate injection. It applies to the α-fiber (fold depth 1) and the β-fiber (fold depth 2). Energy costs are (R4):

- Depth 1: 2.1 × 10⁴⁴ J/kg
- Depth 2: 4.4 × 10⁸⁸ J/kg

CMNIS Level 3 certification is the minimum for Class F operations. Transit duration in folded space is measurable and logged; clocks continue normally (R5).

**Manifold bleed** is the leakage of vacuum energy between adjacent manifold layers, typically indicating a poorly sealed fold transit. Manifold bleed is caused by degradation of the Casimir substrate during transit and appears as anomalous energy flux readings at the vessel boundary.

💡 **NOTE:** Manifold bleed is not immediately dangerous at low levels, but sustained bleed above 10⁻⁸ W/m² indicates imminent substrate collapse. Abort fold transit immediately if sensors detect this threshold.

---

**Class R — Restricted Transit**

Class R requires exotic matter above a certified threshold, or dedicated CTC formation equipment. It applies to:

- Dimension t (time): retrograde transit via CTC only; subject to Novikov Accords treaty obligations
- γ-fiber and δ-fiber: Casimir injection above Class F threshold; energy cost >10¹¹⁰ J/kg; no civilian-rated equipment currently achieves this

CMNIS Level 5 certification is the minimum for any Class R operation. Temporal operations under Class R are additionally subject to the Novikov Accords and require per-mission authorization from CMNIS Temporal Operations.

---

**Class X — Theoretically Inaccessible**

No confirmed transit as of Fourth Edition (SBY-0). Class X applies to the ε-fiber, ζ-fiber, and η-fiber. Energy costs are unknown; theoretical models suggest ζ-fiber may require brane-level energy densities. The η-fiber is theorized to be the boundary surface of the Dirichlet brane; transit into η would constitute brane transit (see Section 2.5).

All Class X research is restricted to CMNIS Division 7 (theoretical).

📖 **HISTORICAL NOTE:** Three attempts to probe the ε-fiber were made between SBY-44 and SBY-12. None returned confirmable data. CMNIS classifies all three incidents as "instrument anomaly pending review."

### 2.5 Adjacent Manifolds (Brane Separation)

The eleven dimensions described in Sections 2.1 through 2.4 constitute the full dimensional structure of our Standard Manifold and its Compressed Register. However, M-theory predicts that our Standard Manifold is itself one of many: each Dirichlet brane in the M-theory landscape is a separate physical universe, occupying its own 4-dimensional macroscopic spacetime and its own Compressed Register, separated from our brane by the brane boundary structure associated with the η-fiber.

**Brane transit** is passage between adjacent Dirichlet branes — that is, transit from our Standard Manifold into a genuinely separate physical universe. It is the highest-energy transit class described in this handbook and has not been achieved by any certified vehicle as of Fourth Edition (SBY-0).

Each brane-separated manifold is identified by a **Manifold Index (Mₓ)**: a coordinate triplet (Mₓ, My, Mz) specifying the manifold's position in the M-theory brane landscape relative to our home manifold. Our Standard Manifold is designated M₀·₀·₀ in all CMNIS catalogues. Adjacent manifolds confirmed through vacuum energy signature analysis are assigned sequential index values in each coordinate direction.

Manifold identification at transit approach is accomplished via vacuum energy signature differentials: each brane has a characteristic vacuum energy density distinguishable from neighboring branes at approach distances accessible to current sensor technology. CMNIS maintains a restricted catalogue of confirmed adjacent manifolds, available to Level 5 certified practitioners under research authorization.

The minimum energy estimate for brane transit is 10⁵⁶ J — no certified transit vehicle currently approaches this threshold. Brane transit entries in this handbook are included for taxonomic completeness and long-range theoretical planning only.

Brane transit must be distinguished from CTC-induced manifold branching. Intentional brane transit is a deliberate crossing of the η-fiber boundary, navigating from one Dirichlet brane to another. CTC-induced branching, if it occurs, is an unintended consequence of quantum decoherence at the Cauchy horizon: a separate manifold may be produced by the decoherence event, but the traveler does not navigate to it and cannot target it. In either case, R3 applies: branching transits produce genuinely separate manifolds, and the original timeline or brane is not affected by events occurring in the destination manifold.

💡 **NOTE:** CMNIS does not currently certify brane transit for any operational purpose. Manifold Index coordinates in this handbook refer to navigational reference only. Contact CMNIS Division 7 for research access protocols.

⚠️ **WARNING:** Any transit that produces manifold bleed signatures consistent with brane boundary proximity must be aborted immediately. Uncontrolled approach to the η-fiber has caused permanent instrument loss in four documented incidents.
