## Foreword

The Interstitial Transit Authority has, for the better part of three decades, served as the preeminent regulatory and operational body for extradimensional transit across all signatory jurisdictions. In that time, it has been the privilege — and occasional burden — of this office to oversee the safe, compliant, and orderly movement of personnel, equipment, and authorized cargo through wormholes, branes, and associated exotic-matter infrastructure.

The Third Edition of this handbook served the community admirably for eleven years. That it contained certain inaccuracies is not a reflection of any failure on the part of the drafting committee, but rather a testament to the difficulty of writing technical guidance for phenomena that are, at the time of writing, only partially understood, and in some cases only partially legal. The temporal excursion mortality figures cited in Appendix C of the Third Edition were, as has since been established, based on a data set that included several incidents that are now believed to have not occurred, in the causal sense. These have been corrected. We do not dwell on this further.

The Fourth Edition represents a significant milestone: it is the first edition to be partially declassified. Sections previously available only to ITA-credentialed researchers and approved governmental bodies are now available to the general professional public, subject to the understanding that this office accepts no liability for misuse, misinterpretation, or misapplication of the information contained herein.

It should be noted that the ITA does not accept liability for paradox-related incidents of any kind. This is not merely a legal position. The Novikov self-consistency principle, as discussed in Chapter 3, holds that any sequence of events giving rise to a paradox has, by definition, zero probability. Accordingly, any incident reported as "paradox-related" was not, in the technical sense, a paradox. The ITA's position is that such incidents are therefore outside the scope of the present handbook, and travelers are encouraged to seek clarification from the relevant annex, several of which remain classified.

This edition was prepared by the Office of Operational Safety and Exotic Matter Compliance, with contributions from multiple review committees whose membership is, in several cases, difficult to confirm due to ongoing temporal audit procedures.

We trust it will be useful.

## Chapter 1: Foundations of Extradimensional Infrastructure
*What the Universe Allows, and What We've Learned to Ask For*

### §1.1 The Structure of the Bulk

The universe accessible to ordinary human observation — three spatial dimensions and one temporal dimension — is not, in the framework used by this authority, the whole of what exists. Current consensus in theoretical physics, and more practically in ITA operational doctrine, holds that our observable universe constitutes a 3-brane: a three-dimensional membrane embedded within a higher-dimensional spacetime known as the bulk. The bulk has, in the M-theory formulation adopted as ITA's working cosmological framework, eleven dimensions in total — ten spatial and one temporal.[^1]

The remaining seven spatial dimensions not accessible to ordinary observation are not absent. They are compactified: curled into structures at approximately the Planck scale, roughly 10^-35 meters. At this scale, they are not directly observable by any instrument operating at human-relevant energy levels, which has historically led to their being dismissed as theoretical curiosities. ITA operations have conclusively demonstrated that they are not curiosities. They are infrastructure.

The practical consequence of this geometry that most directly affects ITA operations is the behavior of the fundamental forces. Electromagnetic, weak nuclear, and strong nuclear interactions are, in current models, confined to the brane. They do not propagate into the bulk. Gravity, however, is not so confined. Gravitons propagate through the full eleven-dimensional bulk, which dilutes their effective strength across the additional dimensions. This is the accepted explanation for the apparent weakness of gravity relative to the other fundamental forces — a disparity of approximately 10^38 in coupling strength that has long been known as the hierarchy problem. The ITA does not consider this problem solved. We consider it operationally relevant, and proceed accordingly.

### §1.2 The Four Transit Modalities

ITA operations are organized around four recognized transit modalities. Each modality has distinct physical requirements, operational constraints, regulatory classifications, and exotic matter budgets. Personnel should be familiar with all four categories, if only to correctly identify which type of incident they are reporting.

**Spatial Wormhole Transit (SWT)** is the most operationally common modality and the primary subject of this handbook. SWT involves the traversal of a Morris-Thorne wormhole connecting two spatially separated points. The wormhole is maintained in a traversable state through the continuous application of exotic matter (see §1.4). Transit is physical: the traveler enters one mouth and exits the other. This is what ITA personnel mean by "teleportation," and what ITA personnel will gently correct others to mean as well (see callout box, this section).

**Split-mouth Displacement Wormhole — Temporal (SDW-T)** is a specialized variant of SWT in which the two wormhole mouths have been time-displaced relative to one another. This is achieved by accelerating one mouth to relativistic velocities for a calculated duration, inducing time dilation, and then returning it. The result is a wormhole in which the two mouths exist at different points in coordinate time, enabling temporal excursion. SDW-T operations are subject to Chapter 3 and the relevant classified annexes.

**Brane Excursion and Dimensional Entry (BEDE)** involves the decompactification of Kaluza-Klein dimensions sufficient to allow physical entry into the bulk. BEDE operations are the least common modality and carry the highest operational complexity and exotic matter expenditure. Entry into the bulk theoretically permits traversal to other branes — other universes, in colloquial usage — though the ITA notes that no authorized return from such excursions has been confirmed to date.[^2] BEDE procedures are addressed in Chapter 6.

**Quantum Coherent Data Relay (QCDR)** is included in this classification framework for completeness and for the purpose of preventing confusion. QCDR is the quantum teleportation of information and photon states via entanglement. It is not a transit modality for physical matter. It is not possible to use QCDR to move a person, an object, or a biological sample from one location to another. It is mentioned here because this handbook has, in previous editions, received queries suggesting otherwise. The answer remains no. See §1.3.

> **A Note on Nomenclature**
>
> Within ITA operational contexts, the term "teleportation" refers exclusively to Spatial Wormhole Transit (SWT): physical traversal through a wormhole. It does not refer to Quantum Coherent Data Relay (QCDR), which transfers only quantum states — not matter, not persons, not cargo.
>
> Travelers who use "teleportation" to mean QCDR will be gently corrected by ITA staff, and less gently corrected by physics.
>
> This distinction is not a matter of preference. It reflects a categorical difference in the underlying mechanisms. Wormhole transit moves you through space. Quantum state transfer does not move you anywhere. The ITA appreciates that popular usage has not always respected this distinction. The ITA also notes that popular usage has not always survived contact with the relevant phenomena.

### §1.3 Why Macroscopic Quantum Teleportation Is a Physical Impossibility

The question of why quantum teleportation cannot be used to transport physical objects — and specifically persons — is sufficiently common that it merits a dedicated section.

Quantum teleportation, as implemented in QCDR systems, requires the preservation of a coherent quantum state in the object being transferred. This coherence is fragile. Interaction with the surrounding environment causes decoherence: the quantum state collapses into a classical mixture, destroying the information required for faithful reconstruction.

The timescale over which this decoherence occurs scales inversely with the mass of the object and the thermal noise of the environment. For a 70-kilogram human being at any operationally realistic temperature, the decoherence timescale is on the order of 10^-21 seconds. This is not a policy constraint. This is not a technological limitation that improved engineering might eventually overcome. This is a consequence of the relationship between thermal fluctuations and quantum state preservation, and it is not negotiable.[^3]

The ITA does not prohibit macroscopic quantum teleportation of persons. The ITA notes that physics has already done so, and declines to duplicate the effort.

### §1.4 Exotic Matter and the WTSU System

All wormhole-based transit modalities — SWT and SDW-T — require the continuous presence of exotic matter at the wormhole throat to maintain traversability. Exotic matter, designated EM-negative in ITA accounting, is matter with negative energy density. It locally violates the weak energy condition, which is a requirement for keeping a Morris-Thorne wormhole open rather than allowing it to collapse.

Exotic matter is the fundamental bottleneck resource for all ITA operations. Its production is energy-intensive, its storage is technically demanding, its transport is regulated under the Exotic Matter Handling Protocol (ITA-OPS-2.7), and its expenditure is tracked in Wormhole Throat Stabilization Units (WTSU). The WTSU is the ITA's unit of account for exotic matter budgeting and serves as the basis for operational cost estimation, regulatory reporting, and liability assessment.

The relationship between wormhole throat geometry, traversal parameters, and WTSU expenditure is addressed in Chapter 2. For present purposes, it is sufficient to note that insufficient exotic matter supply is the most common cause of unplanned throat collapse, and that unplanned throat collapse is the most common cause of the type of incident that results in the revision of mortality rate tables. The Third Edition tables have been revised. The Fourth Edition tables are current as of their compilation date, with the caveat noted in the foreword.
