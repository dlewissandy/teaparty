## Chapter 2: Spatial Wormhole Transit (SWT)

*Getting From A to B Without Travelling In Between*

### 2.1 Wormhole Geometry

A Morris-Thorne traversable wormhole consists of two mouths — discrete regions of curved spacetime — connected by a throat: the minimum-radius cross-section of the wormhole's interior geometry. Each mouth opens onto ordinary spacetime and can, in principle, be positioned at any two locations in the universe, subject to practical engineering constraints discussed in Section 2.5. The mouths are not holes in the conventional sense; they are topological features of the metric, and a traveler passing through one encounters no wall, membrane, or surface — only a smooth transition into the wormhole interior.

The distinguishing property of SWT is that the spatial distance measured along the wormhole interior — through the throat — is far shorter than the coordinate distance between the mouths as measured through ordinary space. The two mouths may be separated by several light-years; the traversal distance through the throat may be a few hundred meters. The geometry is non-Euclidean, non-trivial, and entirely intentional.

[FIGURE 2.1 — Wormhole Embedding Diagram: placeholder]

The metric of a Morris-Thorne wormhole in its simplest form describes a geometry in which radial distance from either mouth decreases to a minimum (the throat radius, *b₀*) and then increases again on the far side. Both mouths exist simultaneously in ordinary spacetime. They are not the same location; they are connected locations.[^2-1]

### 2.2 Negative Energy Manifold Systems and Exotic Matter Stabilization

A traversable wormhole requires the presence of matter with negative energy density — what the ITA designates EM-negative — distributed along the throat to hold it open. EM-negative locally violates the weak energy condition, a property that general relativity requires in order to permit traversable geometries. Without it, any perturbation of the throat — gravitational, mechanical, or electromagnetic — causes the throat to pinch off within microseconds. This process is not recoverable. It is designated Emergency Throat Collapse (ETC) in ITA operational documentation and is discussed further in Chapter 5.

The Negative Energy Manifold System (NEMS) is the ITA's engineering solution to continuous EM-negative production and delivery at the throat. Current ITA production-grade NEMS hardware relies on Casimir arrays: nanoscale parallel conducting plates in vacuum, in which quantum vacuum fluctuations produce a net negative energy density between the plates. These arrays are distributed circumferentially at the throat and monitored continuously by the WTSU (Wormhole Throat Stabilization Unit), which adjusts array geometry in real time to maintain throat radius within tolerance.[^2-2]

The energy cost of stabilization scales as the inverse square of the throat radius — that is, smaller throats require proportionally more EM-negative per unit radius, not less. A 0.5-meter throat requires substantially more stabilization energy per unit volume than a 10-meter throat. This is counterintuitive to most travelers and is the source of several recurring procurement disputes that the ITA declines to adjudicate here.

### 2.3 ITA Standard Operating Procedure 2.1: Transit Procedure

All SWT transits must be conducted in accordance with ITA SOP 2.1. Deviations require prior written authorization from a Class-III Transit Coordinator or above.

**Approach Phase.** The traveler approaches the designated mouth at sub-relativistic velocity. At a distance of no less than 50 meters from the mouth boundary, the traveler's transit beacon initiates a registry ping to the ITA Transit Registry. Confirmation of mouth-pair identity is required before proceeding. Do not proceed if the registry ping returns an unregistered or mismatched gravitational signature hash.

**Coupling Phase.** NEMS telemetry is queried via the WTSU uplink. The throat must be confirmed stable — all parameters green — before the traveler enters the coupling zone. If NEMS status is amber or red, halt approach and contact the nearest Transit Operations Center. If no Transit Operations Center is reachable, consult Chapter 5.

**Traversal.** Transit duration depends on throat length, which is a geometric property of the wormhole's interior and is distinct from the coordinate separation of the mouths in ordinary space. For ITA-standard Class-II throats, transit duration is typically 2 to 15 seconds. Travelers should not be alarmed by the visual distortion of the external universe observed during traversal; this is a consequence of gravitational lensing at the throat boundary and does not indicate a fault condition.

**Exit Phase.** The exit mouth incorporates a deceleration zone — a region of modified metric curvature designed to reduce the traveler's effective velocity relative to the exit frame. The traveler clears the exit mouth, and NEMS status at both mouths is automatically logged to the ITA Transit Registry. The transit record is timestamped, hashed, and archived. Travelers are advised to confirm their registry log entry before departing the exit area.

### 2.4 Tidal Force Thresholds by Throat Radius

Tidal forces at the throat scale inversely with throat radius. The following table summarizes ITA throat classifications and rated uses.

| Throat Radius | Tidal Gradient | Rated Use |
|---|---|---|
| 0.5 m | Severe | Traveler extraction only; non-living cargo; emergency recovery operations. Not rated for unprotected biological transit. |
| 2 m | Moderate | Human-rated with standard NEMS transit suit and tidal dampeners installed. |
| 10 m+ | Low | Vehicle-rated; large cargo; standard commercial and freight transit. |

Travelers transiting a 2-meter throat without the required NEMS suit and dampeners do so outside ITA-rated conditions. The ITA notes that this does not prevent such transits from occurring; it merely affects liability classification.

### 2.5 Mass, Throughput Limits, and NEMS Capacity

Throughput through a wormhole is not limited by geometry alone. Forcing large mass through a small throat at high transit rate requires a proportionally higher EM-negative output from the NEMS array. As mass throughput increases, the NEMS system must work harder to maintain throat geometry against the additional stress-energy. At the limits of NEMS capacity, the throat radius begins to contract. Continued operation beyond rated capacity initiates ETC conditions.

Throughput tables indexed by throat radius and NEMS class are published in Appendix B. Operators are responsible for verifying that planned transit schedules do not exceed NEMS rated capacity.[^2-3]

### 2.6 Paired-Mouth Registration

Before any SWT transit is conducted, both mouths of the wormhole pair must be registered with the ITA Transit Registry. Registration requires submission of the gravitational signature hash of each mouth — a unique identifier derived from the local spacetime metric at the mouth boundary — along with the operator's credentials and a completed Form ITA-7 (Wormhole Mouth Pair Declaration).

Operating an unregistered wormhole mouth is a Class-II violation under the ITA Transit Code. Operating both mouths of an unregistered pair simultaneously constitutes two separate violations. The ITA is aware that this arithmetic is disputed by several legal practices; the relevant appeals remain pending.

Mouth-pair registration is also required for insurance purposes. Unregistered mouths are not covered under any ITA-affiliated transit liability policy.

### 2.7 Note on Temporal Displacement

The preceding sections describe SWT in the purely spatial case — two mouths existing at the same coordinate time. When one mouth of a registered pair has been subjected to relativistic transport, the mouth-pair may exhibit temporal displacement, connecting two points in spacetime separated in both space and time. This is the domain of Split-mouth Displacement Wormhole (Temporal) operations and is addressed in Chapter 3.

## Chapter 3: Temporal Displacement Operations (SDW-T)

*When You're Going, and When You're Going*

### 3.1 The Split-Mouth Technique

A standard Morris-Thorne wormhole, as described in Chapter 2, connects two spatially separated mouths existing at the same coordinate time — the same epoch. The wormhole interior is purely spatial: entering mouth A and exiting mouth B deposits a traveler at a different location but no different moment.

Temporal displacement is achieved by a straightforward application of special relativistic time dilation, which the ITA designates the split-mouth technique.

Begin with a registered mouth pair, both mouths at rest in the same reference frame at the same coordinate time. Designate the fixed mouth as Mouth A and the mobile mouth as Mouth B. Mouth B is then transported along a relativistic trajectory — accelerated to a significant fraction of the speed of light, held at that velocity for a measured interval, and returned to its original or designated spatial position. Due to time dilation, Mouth B ages less than Mouth A during this process. Upon return, Mouth B is temporally displaced: it now exists at an earlier coordinate time than Mouth A, despite being spatially adjacent.

[FIGURE 3.1 — Split-Mouth Technique (Spacetime Diagram): placeholder]

The wormhole interior now connects two time-separated events in ordinary spacetime. A traveler entering Mouth A — the later mouth — exits through Mouth B into the past. A traveler entering Mouth B — the earlier mouth — exits through Mouth A into the future. The degree of temporal displacement is determined by the velocity profile and duration of Mouth B's relativistic journey, and is calculated in advance by the displacement contractor. Displacement calculations are submitted as part of Form ITA-23 (SDW-T Mouth Trajectory Declaration) and are subject to ITA technical review.[^3-1]

The wormhole remains spatially traversable throughout this process. The NEMS requirements are identical to standard SWT operations. Temporal displacement adds no additional tidal stress to the throat geometry.

### 3.2 The Novikov Self-Consistency Principle, Operationalized

The universe does not permit inconsistent histories. More precisely: the quantum mechanical amplitude for any sequence of events that would produce a logical paradox — a causal loop that negates its own precondition — is identically zero. Such events do not occur. This is the Novikov self-consistency principle, and it is not ITA policy. It is a feature of the physical universe.

In practical terms: a traveler cannot, regardless of intent, kill their own grandfather before their parent's conception. A traveler cannot prevent their own transit from occurring. A traveler cannot deliver information to the past that causes the conditions for their departure to fail to materialize. These actions are not merely prohibited; they are physically impossible. The quantum mechanical pathways leading to paradox have zero amplitude and are, in a meaningful sense, not available to the traveler as options.

> **Operational Note:** PECA clearance (Section 3.4) assesses the Novikov consistency of a proposed excursion prior to departure. A proposed action that would constitute a paradox will not receive PECA clearance and will not be authorized. This is not the ITA declining to permit paradox. This is the ITA declining to authorize excursions for which PECA cannot confirm physical realizability. The distinction has been explained to several travelers who found it unsatisfying. The physics remains unchanged.

The ITA has chosen to align its temporal operations policy with the Novikov self-consistency principle on pragmatic grounds: a policy that prohibited physically impossible actions and permitted only physically possible ones was considered the most operationally coherent framework available. Several ITA legal advisors initially objected to this framing on the grounds that it left insufficient room for legal discretion. They were eventually persuaded by the physics.[^3-2]

### 3.3 Temporal Coordinate Notation

ITA documentation distinguishes two coordinate systems for SDW-T operations.

**T-coordinates** (coordinate time) refer to the calendar date and time at the destination epoch — the position in history to which the traveler is transiting. T-coordinate is objective and independent of the traveler's personal history. It is the year on the destination newspaper.

**C-coordinates** (causal-order time, or 'clock time') refer to the time experienced along the traveler's worldline — their biological age, their wristwatch, the sequence of events as they personally lived them. A traveler who departs at C-coordinate age 34 and transits to a T-coordinate twenty years in the past arrives in the past still aged 34. Their C-coordinate continues to advance normally from that point.

[FIGURE 3.3 — T-Coordinate vs. C-Coordinate Reference Grid: placeholder]

The distinction matters. A traveler who makes repeated temporal excursions may have a C-coordinate age substantially different from what the T-coordinate history of their location would suggest. ITA logs record both coordinates for every SDW-T transit. Travelers are required to maintain a personal C-coordinate log (Form ITA-31, Personal Worldline Record) for any excursion series involving more than three SDW-T transits.

A common source of confusion: C-coordinates advance monotonically and cannot be reversed. T-coordinates are a property of the destination, not the traveler. A traveler cannot 'undo' C-coordinate aging by transiting to an earlier T-coordinate epoch. The ITA notes that this is frequently not what travelers hoped to hear.

### 3.4 Pre-Excursion Consistency Audit (PECA)

No SDW-T transit may be authorized without a completed and cleared Pre-Excursion Consistency Audit. PECA is a formal mathematical and logical review of the proposed excursion timeline — the sequence of events, interactions, and intended actions proposed by the traveler — conducted to verify Novikov consistency before departure is approved.

The PECA process involves submission of the traveler's Excursion Intent Declaration (Form ITA-28), computational consistency modeling against the registered historical record of the destination epoch, and review by a certified PECA officer. The full protocol is described in Chapter 6.

A failed PECA does not indicate that the traveler has proposed something immoral or dangerous in the ordinary sense. It indicates that the proposed excursion, as described, cannot physically occur as described. The traveler is invited to revise their Excursion Intent Declaration and resubmit.

PECA clearance confirms that the excursion is Novikov-consistent and physically realizable. It does not guarantee that the excursion will be comfortable, pleasant, or productive. Several travelers have been surprised to discover that a PECA-cleared excursion was exactly as difficult as the historical record implied it would be. The ITA considers this an accurate outcome, not a defect.

### 3.5 Closed Timelike Loops and Bootstrap Object Management

Some SDW-T excursions produce closed timelike loops (CTLs) — causal structures in which an object, piece of information, or sequence of events forms a loop with no external origin point. A document that exists in the destination epoch because a traveler brought it from the future, where it existed because it was found in the past — this is a bootstrap object. It has no discernible point of original creation. It simply is, and has always been, and will always be, by virtue of the loop.

Bootstrap objects are physically legal. They are Novikov-consistent by construction — if they were not, they would not exist, and the loop would not have formed. They require no special handling beyond accurate documentation and the submission of Form ITA-44 (Bootstrap Object or Self-Originating Information Declaration) to the ITA Registry within 30 days of excursion completion.[^3-3]

Travelers who find bootstrap objects philosophically troubling are advised to consult the Glossary, where the relevant terminology is defined, and Chapter 6, where the paperwork requirements are laid out in full. The ITA does not adjudicate the ontological status of self-originating information. It registers it.
