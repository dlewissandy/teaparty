## SECTION 1: INCONGRUITY THEORY — Foundational Thinkers

### Source 1.1
**Citation:** Morreall, John. "Philosophy of Humor." *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*. First published 2012-11-20; substantive revision 2024-09-19. Edward N. Zalta & Uri Nodelman (eds.).
**URL:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/humor/
**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible.
**Primary/Secondary:** Secondary (encyclopedia entry synthesizing primary sources).
**Relevance:** The single most authoritative free-access scholarly overview of the three major humor theories. Covers Kant, Schopenhauer, and Kierkegaard on incongruity in depth.

- **Kant:** Explains humor through violated expectations. *Critique of Judgment* (1790) — laughter arises from "the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing." The mind enjoys the jostling of failed prediction.
- **Schopenhauer:** *The World as Will and Representation* (1818/1844). Locates incongruity between abstract concepts and concrete perceptions. Humor arises from "the sudden perception of the incongruity between a concept and the real objects which have been thought through it." This maps directly onto the knock-knock punchline mechanism: the listener has formed one conceptual expectation (a name or entity) and the punchline substitutes a phonetically similar but semantically incongruous object.
- **Kierkegaard:** Uses the term "contradiction" rather than incongruity. Positions humor at the boundary between ethical and religious existence. Less directly applicable to knock-knock structure.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** The SEP entry contains no discussion of knock-knock jokes, formulaic joke structures, or call-and-response formats.

## SECTION 2: FREUD AND RELIEF THEORY

### Source 2.1
**Citation:** Freud, Sigmund. *Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious* (*Der Witz und seine Beziehung zum Unbewussten*). Vienna: Franz Deuticke, 1905. English translation by James Strachey, 1955, W. W. Norton.
**URL:** https://ia802907.us.archive.org/17/items/SigmundFreud/Sigmund%20Freud%20%5B1905%5D%20Jokes%20and%20their%20Relation%20to%20the%20Unconscious%20(James%20Strachey%20translation,%201955).pdf
**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible via Internet Archive.
**Primary/Secondary:** Primary.
**Relevance:** Foundational psychoanalytic treatment of joke technique and social function. Key concepts:
- **Condensation** (multiple meanings compressed into one word) and **double meaning** — both central to the knock-knock phonetic pun mechanism.
- The joke requires a minimum triadic social structure: joke-teller, audience, and butt. This maps directly onto knock-knock's required two-party exchange (teller + willing responder).
- Freud distinguishes "tendentious" jokes (with aggressive/sexual content) from "innocent" jokes (pure technique). Knock-knock jokes are paradigmatically innocent — their pleasure comes purely from the technical pleasure of wordplay, not from aggression.
- Freud was explicitly skeptical of puns as the lowest form of wit ("cheapest" technique), which is a relevant negative data point for the knock-knock form.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** Freud does not discuss knock-knock jokes specifically (they were not yet culturally established in 1905). The application is by extension only.

## SECTION 3: RASKIN'S SEMANTIC SCRIPT THEORY OF HUMOR (SSTH)

### Source 3.1
**Citation:** Raskin, Victor. *Semantic Mechanisms of Humor*. Dordrecht: D. Reidel Publishing, 1985. (Studies in Linguistics and Philosophy, vol. 24.)
**URL:** No freely accessible full text found during this research session. ResearchGate listing returned 403.
**Paywalled:** Yes — book, not openly available. Excerpts circulate in course packets.
**Primary/Secondary:** Primary (the foundational text of SSTH).
**Relevance:** This is the central theoretical text for analyzing knock-knock jokes linguistically.

Key SSTH concepts relevant to knock-knock jokes:
- **Script:** A cognitive chunk of structured semantic information about how the world works (e.g., "visitor at the door," "a person named Arthur").
- **Script opposition:** A joke is funny when a single text is compatible with two different scripts that are in opposition. The two canonical oppositions are: real/unreal, expected/unexpected, actual/non-actual.
- **Trigger:** The punchline element that forces the switch from the initially activated script to the second, incongruous script.
- **Isotopy:** The coherent semantic line through the joke's setup that the listener constructs before the trigger fires.

**Application to knock-knock jokes:** The setup phrase (e.g., "Atch") activates a proper-name script (Script 1: this is a person's name). The punchline ("Bless you!") activates a phonetically triggered second script (Script 2: a sneeze sound). The joke's mechanism is the forced retroactive reanalysis of the trigger word as a homophone rather than a name. This is a textbook SSTH phonetic-opposition case.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** No Google Scholar result or freely accessible secondary source confirmed that Raskin's 1985 book contains a worked example specifically of a knock-knock joke. The application above is the standard extension made by subsequent commentators. Whether Raskin himself used knock-knock jokes as examples in the 1985 book could not be verified without full-text access.

## SECTION 4: ATTARDO'S GENERAL THEORY OF VERBAL HUMOR (GTVH)

### Source 4.1
**Citation:** Attardo, Salvatore, and Victor Raskin. "Script Theory Revis(it)ed: Joke Similarity and Joke Representation Model." *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research* 4(3–4), 1991, pp. 293–347.
**URL:** https://www.degruyter.com/document/doi/10.1515/humr.1991.4.3-4.293/html
**Paywalled:** Yes (De Gruyter paywall).
**Primary/Secondary:** Primary (the founding GTVH paper).
**Relevance:** Introduces the GTVH's six Knowledge Resources (KRs): Script Opposition (SO), Logical Mechanism (LM), Situation (SI), Target (TA), Narrative Strategy (NS), and Language (LA). For knock-knock jokes:
- SO: the phonetic homophone opposition (name vs. sound/object)
- LM: false analogy or sound-substitution
- NS: the fixed five-line dialogue frame — this KR is especially significant as it would classify knock-knock as a distinct narrative strategy type (formulaic dialogue) separable from riddles or narrative jokes
- LA: the pun

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** No freely accessible paper by Attardo or Raskin applying GTVH specifically to knock-knock jokes was found. Search for "Attardo GTVH knock-knock" returned no direct hits.

## SECTION 5: SOCIAL CONTRACT AND RITUAL STRUCTURE OF JOKE-TELLING

### Source 5.1
**Citation:** Sacks, Harvey. "An Analysis of the Course of a Joke's Telling in Conversation." In Richard Bauman and Joel Sherzer (eds.), *Explorations in the Ethnography of Speaking*, pp. 337–353. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1974.
**URL:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/explorations-in-the-ethnography-of-speaking/an-analysis-of-the-course-of-a-jokes-telling-in-conversation/4A8610D1D725BEDF789DC6A9B4993AFD
**Paywalled:** Yes (Cambridge paywall; full text not extracted — 504 error during fetch).
**Primary/Secondary:** Primary.
**Relevance:** The foundational conversation-analysis treatment of how jokes are initiated, sustained, and completed in interaction. Sacks establishes that joke-telling requires a pre-sequence that bids for extended turn-holding — the audience must consent to suspend normal conversational turn-taking rights. This is directly relevant to knock-knock jokes: the opening "Knock knock" IS the pre-sequence bid; "Who's there?" is the audience's consent to enter joke-mode. Sacks also analyzes "the dirty joke as a technical object," with attention to recipient guidance and punchlines.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** Sacks does not analyze knock-knock jokes specifically. Full text not retrieved.

## SECTION 6: FORMULAIC AND RITUAL JOKE STRUCTURE

### Source 6.1
**Citation:** Biria, Reza, and Zohreh Kassaian. "Formulaic Jokes in Interaction: The Prosody of Riddle Openings." *Pragmatics & Cognition* 19(2), 2011, pp. 272–295. DOI: 10.1075/pc.19.2.05bir
**URL:** https://www.jbe-platform.com/content/journals/10.1075/pc.19.2.05bir
**Also:** https://benjamins.com/catalog/pc.19.2.05bir
**Paywalled:** Yes (John Benjamins paywall).
**Primary/Secondary:** Primary (original research article).
**Relevance:** Directly relevant. Examines formulaic jokes (defined as relatively fixed in textual form — riddles, narrative jokes — as opposed to spontaneous quips). Key finding: riddle questions use constrained pitch variation compared to genuine conversational wh-questions, because the formulaic context removes the need for contextual disambiguation. This prosodic signaling argument extends to knock-knock jokes: "Knock knock / Who's there?" is prosodically marked as joke-mode, not genuine inquiry. Closest available scholarly comparison between riddle-type formulaic jokes and conversational interaction.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** The paper focuses on riddles, not knock-knock jokes specifically. Whether knock-knock jokes are treated as a subtype could not be confirmed without full-text access.

## SECTION 9: ELLIOTT ORING — APPROPRIATE INCONGRUITY

### Source 9.1
**Citation:** Oring, Elliott. *Jokes and Their Relations*. Lexington: University Press of Kentucky, 1992. Reprinted: New Brunswick: Transaction Publishers, 2008 (ISBN 9781412814393).
**URL:** https://books.google.com/books/about/Jokes_and_Their_Relations.html?id=UOuLZcw0Y1gC (Google Books preview only)
**Paywalled:** Yes (book; no free full text found).
**Primary/Secondary:** Primary.
**Relevance:** Oring's "appropriate incongruity" theory — humor depends on perceiving a conflation of incongruous elements that nevertheless seem appropriately related. This is a refinement of standard incongruity theory that requires not just surprise but a felt rightness to the resolution. Directly applicable to knock-knock jokes: the phonetic substitution must be both surprising (incongruous name/object swap) and feel apt (the sound genuinely resembles the setup). If the pun is too forced, the joke fails even though incongruity is present.

**NEGATIVE FINDING:** No confirmed analysis of knock-knock jokes specifically in Oring's work found during this session.
