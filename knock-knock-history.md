## Research findings with source attribution

## 1. Shakespeare's Macbeth, Act 2 Scene 3 — The Porter's Speech

### What the scene actually contains

The Porter scene occurs after the murder of King Duncan. The porter, drunk, imagines himself gatekeeper of Hell and mutters to himself as he hears knocking at the gate. The text includes the phrase "Knock, knock, knock! Who's there, i' th' name of Belzebub?" but is a dramatic **monologue** — one speaker, not an interactive exchange.

### The scholarly verdict: the connection is loose

The most substantive scholarly treatment found via open sources is a 2024 blog post by **Stephen Winick**, a folklorist at the American Folklife Center of the Library of Congress. His piece "Knock Knock! Who's There? Metafolklore, Jokes, and Shakespeare" (April 20, 2024) argues the Shakespeare attribution is a "creation myth" (in the sense used by Stephen Jay Gould) rather than a documented line of descent.

Winick's specific objections:
- The porter is a single speaker performing a dramatic monologue, not two parties doing call-and-response.
- The knocking patterns are inconsistent ("knock knock knock" vs. "knock knock").
- The speech references Henry Garnet's 1605 trial and 1606 execution — contemporary political satire, not comedy.
- There is no punchline structure.
- There is no documented causal connection between the 1606 scene and modern knock-knock jokes that emerged ~300 years later.

The No Sweat Shakespeare site, in a popular-facing article, acknowledges: "There doesn't seem to be any clear line of descent from the drunken porter's irritated mutterings."

**Source (open):** https://blogs.loc.gov/folklife/2024/04/knock-knock-whos-there-metafolklore-jokes-and-shakespeare/
**Source (open):** https://nosweatshakespeare.com/shakespeare-knock-knock/

### Ben Jonson's prior claim — a stronger structural match

Winick identifies a passage in Ben Jonson's *The Case is Altered* (dated 1597–1598, roughly 8–9 years before *Macbeth*) that better matches the modern format. In Act IV, Scene 7, two servants approach a house:

> ONION: Knock, knock!
> RACHEL: Who's there? Father?
> JUNIPER: Father? No, and yet a father, if you please to be a mother.

This is an interactive exchange with: two parties, a "who's there?" response, and a wordplay punchline (a sexual proposition). Winick found this via the Early English Books Online database.

**Status:** Jonson's passage is a stronger structural precursor than Shakespeare's — but the same caveat applies: no documented line of descent to modern jokes.

**Source (open):** https://blogs.loc.gov/folklife/2024/04/knock-knock-whos-there-metafolklore-jokes-and-shakespeare/

### Winick's broader framing

Both Jonson and Shakespeare were drawing on pre-existing oral folk traditions. Winick argues the "knock knock, who's there" opening likely existed in folk routines before either playwright wrote it down. The attribution to Shakespeare specifically follows a pattern Winick documented separately — Shakespeare is routinely (and often inaccurately) credited with inventing English-language usages.

## 2. Vaudeville and English Folk Riddling Traditions

### The "Do You Know" joke as immediate precursor

The clearest documented evolutionary ancestor of the knock-knock joke is the "Do You Know" joke format, circulating around 1900. The journalist **Merely McEvoy**, writing in a 1922 *Oakland Tribune* column, described this form: a jokester approaches someone and asks "Do you know Arthur?" — the listener replies "Arthur who?" — and the punchline is "Arthurmometer!" The wordplay mechanism (name-as-pun) is structurally identical to knock-knock jokes.

McEvoy noted that such jokes returned in cycles roughly every 20 years.

**Source (open):** https://www.npr.org/sections/npr-history-dept/2015/03/03/389865887/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes

### The "Buff" children's game — 1929 documentation

Henry Bett's *The Games of Children: Their Origin and History* (1929) documents a children's game called "Buff" in which one child bangs a stick on the ground and the following exchange occurs:

> Knock, knock!
> Who's there?
> Buff.
> What says Buff?
> Buff says Buff to all his men, and I say Buff to you again.

This is the earliest documented description (found in open sources) of a structured "knock knock / who's there" call-and-response game in children's folk tradition.

**Source (open, via multiple secondary references):** https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/55962/8-moments-knock-knock-joke-history

### Vaudeville connections — UNCERTAIN

Multiple sources assert that knock-knock jokes "appeared sporadically in vaudeville performances and early 20th-century magazines," but no open source consulted names a specific vaudeville act, performer, or documented routine that used the knock-knock format. The call-and-response structure of vaudeville comedy (cross-talk acts, two-handers) is a plausible stylistic ancestor, but the documented link is generic, not specific.

**Status: UNVERIFIED / no specific vaudeville act documented in open sources**

## 3. Children's Joke Books, 1940s–1970s

### 1936: The earliest documented joke book in this format

**Bob Dunn's *Knock Knock* (1936)** — Published by Western Publishing. A collection of more than fifty single-panel or short-strip gag comics, each structured as a knock-knock joke. All in black-and-white; most five or six panels to accommodate the format's five-part structure. Maurice Horn's comics encyclopedia called it "a million selling book"; another source says it "sold over two million copies." These sales figures are UNCERTAIN — the What Were Comics blog questions their accuracy.

Dunn is sometimes credited with "inventing" the knock-knock joke, but this is false. The format existed in print by at least 1934 (see Section 4 below). The CBR Comics investigation of this claim concluded: **"FALSE — Dunn did not invent the knock-knock joke."** He was a major popularizer during the 1936 craze, not the originator.

**Source (open):** https://www.whatwerecomics.com/blog/2018/8/8/0009-knock-knock-1936
**Source (open):** https://www.cbr.com/knock-knock-joke-comic-bob-dunn/

### 1940s–1970s dedicated children's joke books — limited findings

No open source consulted names a specific children's knock-knock joke book title or publisher from the 1940s–1960s (beyond general statements that knock-knock jokes were common in children's humor during this period). Dover Publications offers a *Knock Knock Jokes* collection but its publication date in the open catalog is not confirmed as being from that era. Modern publishers (Highlights, Rob Elliott/Baker Books) dominate current Amazon search results.

**Status: UNCERTAIN — specific 1940s–1970s children's book titles and publishers not verifiable from open sources found.**

## 4. Early Documented Examples in Print

### 1932: Lake Elsinore Valley Sun-Tribune

On March 31, 1932, the *Lake Elsinore Valley Sun-Tribune* (Lake Elsinore, California) printed a knock-knock joke with Paul Revere as the name. This is the earliest specific newspaper date mentioned in open sources reviewed.

**Source (open, secondary):** https://grokipedia.com/page/Knock-knock_joke

### 1934: "Rufus" joke — first commonly cited modern example

A newspaper columnist in 1934 published what is widely cited as the first knock-knock joke in the modern five-part format:
> Knock knock. / Who's there? / Rufus. / Rufus who? / Rufus the most important part of your house.

Some sources describe this as appearing in an advertisement for a roofing company. The exact newspaper name is not confirmed in open sources reviewed.

**Source (open):** https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/55962/8-moments-knock-knock-joke-history

## 5. The 1936 Craze — Radio, Press, and Music

The modern knock-knock joke fad erupted in 1936, with convergent documentation across multiple contemporary sources:

- **Radio:** WKBO (Harrisburg, PA) broadcast knock-knock jokes throughout the day. The *Harrisburg Telegraph* (June 17, 1936) attributed the "Knock-Knock Mania" surge in part to Col. Frank Knox's selection as the Republican vice-presidential candidate — his name was easy punning material.
- **Orchestras:** Swing bands including those led by Fletcher Henderson and Vincent Lopez incorporated audience-participation knock-knock novelty songs such as "The Knock-Knock Song."
- **Press:** An Associated Press article in the *Titusville Herald* (August 3, 1936) was titled "'Knock Knock' Latest Nutsy Game for Parlor Amusement."
- **Radio comedy:** Fred Allen's year-end broadcast (December 30, 1936) included a segment featuring fictional character "Ramrod Dank," deemed "The first man to coin a knock knock" — satire of the craze's absurdity.
- **Backlash:** D.A. Laird (director, Rivercrest Psychological Laboratory, Colgate University) published articles condemning knock-knock jokes as a symptom of "mass manias."

**Source (open):** https://www.npr.org/sections/npr-history-dept/2015/03/03/389865887/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes

## 6. Television Appearances

### Captain Kangaroo — Mr. Moose and the ping-pong ball gag (1955–1984)

The most extensively documented TV use of knock-knock jokes in this era is the recurring gag on *Captain Kangaroo* (CBS, 1955–1984). The puppet character **Mr. Moose**, created and performed by Cosmo Allegretti, made it his mission to trick Captain Kangaroo (Bob Keeshan) into participating in a knock-knock joke. The punchline always included the phrase "ping-pong balls," which triggered hundreds of ping-pong balls to drop from above onto the Captain. Viewers knew it was coming; the Captain never did. This became one of the most remembered running gags in children's television history.

The show ran from 1955 to 1984. The ping-pong gag was a long-running feature, though the precise season it began is not confirmed in open sources reviewed.

**Source (open):** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mr._Moose (returned 403 during fetch — Wikipedia page confirmed to exist via search results)
**Source (open):** https://www.steamboatpilot.com/news/tom-ross-carrots-ping-pong-balls-and-knock-knock-jokes/

### Rowan & Martin's Laugh-In (NBC, 1968–1973)

The Joke Wall segment of *Laugh-In* featured a character called the **Scandinavian Storyteller**, who told nonsensical knock-knock jokes in gibberish. This character has been noted as a possible stylistic antecedent to the Muppets' Swedish Chef. Knock-knock jokes were part of the show's repertoire across its six-season run.

**Source (open):** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rowan_%26_Martin%27s_Laugh-In (returned 403 — confirmed via search results)

### Woody Woodpecker — "Knock Knock" (1940, theatrical short)

The animated character Woody Woodpecker debuted in a 1940 theatrical short called *Knock Knock* (an Andy Panda short). This is not a joke-format use but is a contemporaneous cultural echo of the name. Not a TV appearance in the relevant period.

**Status: tangentially relevant only**

## 7. Advertising Use of the Format

### Edgmont Cash & Carry grocery (Chester, Pennsylvania, 1936)

This retailer ran newspaper display advertisements incorporating the knock-knock format and later (August 1936) sponsored a "Knock! Knock! Contest" with prizes. This is the most specifically documented advertising use found in open sources.

## 8. Knock-Knock Structure Compared to Other Riddling Traditions

### Structural analysis

The knock-knock joke is a five-part closed sequence:
1. "Knock knock."
2. "Who's there?"
3. [Name or word]
4. "[Name or word] who?"
5. [Punchline — typically a pun or wordplay extending the name]

This contrasts with other formats:

**"Why did the chicken cross the road?" (antecedent: 1847)**
The earliest documented appearance is in *The Knickerbocker* magazine (1847), with the answer "Because it wants to get on the other side!" According to music critic Gary Giddins (Ken Burns documentary *Jazz*), the joke spread nationally via minstrel shows beginning in the 1840s. This format is an **anti-joke**: it sets up an expectation of clever wordplay and delivers a literal or bathetic answer. It is a one-way delivery (no call-and-response required from the audience).

The knock-knock format, by contrast, is inherently **participatory** — it cannot proceed without the listener's cooperation. This makes it structurally more like a ritual or game than a one-way joke.

**"What do you call..." format**
This format is a question-and-answer riddle; structurally simpler (two parts: setup + punchline). No required participation ritual. Often punning.

**The "Do You Know" predecessor**
As noted above, the "Do You Know Arthur? / Arthur who? / Arthurmometer!" format (ca. 1900) has the same underlying mechanism as knock-knock jokes — name-as-pun — but lacks the knocking ritual that gives knock-knock jokes their performative frame.

**Wellerisms and traditional riddles**
No open source reviewed made a direct documented connection between knock-knock jokes and Wellerisms (a specific form of traditional riddle with a quoted speaker). The connection to broader riddling traditions is asserted generally but not rigorously documented in any open source found.

**USC Digital Folklore Archives**
A record at USC's Digital Folklore Archives documents a "Knock-Knock Joke with a twist" as an example of the joke as folklore genre, noting the participatory ritual structure. No detailed structural analysis was available in the open excerpt.

**Source (open):** https://folklore.usc.edu/knock-knock-joke-with-a-twist/
**Source (open):** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Why_did_the_chicken_cross_the_road%3F (Wikipedia page confirmed; returned 403 during fetch)
