## PART 1: IONA AND PETER OPIE

### Source 1.1
**Citation:** Opie, Iona and Peter. *The Lore and Language of Schoolchildren.* Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1959. Reprinted as New York Review Books Classics, 2001.
**URL:** https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/365944.The_Lore_and_Language_of_Schoolchildren
**Internet Archive (free full text):** https://archive.org/details/lorelanguageof00opie
**Paywalled:** No — the Internet Archive scan is freely borrowable. The NYRB Classics print edition is commercially available.
**Primary or secondary:** Primary source (original scholarship).

**What it says about joke transmission and children's humor:**
The book is the foundational academic study of children's oral culture in Britain, based on contributions from approximately 5,000 schoolchildren aged 6–14 across England, Scotland, and Wales, collected over roughly eight years beginning in the early 1950s. Its scope includes jokes, riddles, epithets, nicknames, juvenile slang, superstitions, beliefs, customs, rhymes, chants, word play, and sayings. The Opies treat children's oral culture as a self-sustaining transmission system: children pass material peer-to-peer without adult mediation, and the corpus is remarkably stable across generations and geographically distributed — the same joke texts appearing in widely separated parts of Britain without apparent contact between the children.

The book contains a chapter on riddles and a chapter on "Wit and Repartee" that covers stock jokes and retorts. The Opies demonstrate two classes of children's inherited material: those essential to regulating games and relationships, and those that are "mere expressions of exuberance" repeated because they heard them from someone else.

**Knock-knock jokes specifically:** No direct evidence found that the Opies mention knock-knock jokes by name in this book. The 1950s British schoolchildren survey predates the wide spread of the knock-knock format in Britain. No search result or snippet identified a specific passage addressing knock-knock jokes. **NEGATIVE FINDING** — absence is significant given the book's comprehensive scope.

## PART 2: ALAN DUNDES

### Source 2.1
**Citation:** Dundes, Alan. *The Morphology of North American Indian Folktales.* FF Communications No. 195. Helsinki: Academia Scientiarum Fennica, 1964.
**URL:** Not freely available online. Commercially available via academic libraries.
**Paywalled:** Yes — no free digital edition found.
**Primary or secondary:** Primary source.

**What it says about joke structure:**
Applies Vladimir Propp's structural morphology (originally from *Morphology of the Folktale*, 1928) to North American Indian narratives. Dundes introduced the concept of the "motifeme" — an emic structural unit equivalent to Propp's "function" — and proposed motifeme sequences (lack, lack-liquidated; interdiction, violation, consequence, etc.) as a grammar of narrative. This is the foundational framework that later scholars would attempt to apply to joke structure. The work itself is about folktales, not jokes.

**Knock-knock jokes specifically:** No mention found. **NEGATIVE FINDING.**

---

### Source 2.2
**Citation:** Dundes, Alan, and Robert A. Georges. "Toward a Structural Definition of the Riddle." *Journal of American Folklore* 76, no. 300 (1963): 111–118.
**URL:** Accessible via JSTOR — specific stable URL not retrieved in this session; searchable at https://www.jstor.org/journal/jamerfolk
**Paywalled:** Yes — JSTOR access required; freely available with library access or JSTOR free-reads program.
**Primary or secondary:** Primary source.

**What it says:** Applies structural/morphological thinking to the riddle genre, which is the closest genre formally to the knock-knock joke (both are question-answer adjacency pairs built on misdirection and resolution). Dundes and Georges define the riddle as a structural unit and propose a formal taxonomy. The knock-knock joke is a descendant of the punning riddle tradition, so this framework is directly relevant. No search result confirms this article addresses knock-knock jokes by name.

### Source 2.3
**Citation:** Dundes, Alan. "The J.A.P. and the J.A.M. in American Jokelore." *Journal of American Folklore* 98, no. 390 (1985): 456–475.
**URL:** Searchable via JSTOR at https://www.jstor.org/journal/jamerfolk
**Paywalled:** Yes — JSTOR.
**Primary or secondary:** Primary source.

**What it says:** Demonstrates Dundes' method of analyzing joke cycles as cultural symptom — jokes as expressions of social anxiety, stereotyping, and in-group/out-group dynamics. The article does not address knock-knock jokes but illustrates his broader method: jokes are collected as a cycle, analyzed for underlying motifemic structure and cultural logic.

### Source 2.4
**Citation:** Dundes, Alan. *Cracking Jokes: Studies of Sick Humor Cycles and Stereotypes.* Berkeley: Ten Speed Press, 1987.
**URL (Internet Archive):** https://archive.org/details/crackingjokesstu0000dund
**Paywalled:** No — freely borrowable on Internet Archive.
**Primary or secondary:** Primary source.

**What it says:** Collects 13 essays (most previously published) analyzing joke cycles — dead baby jokes, elephant jokes, Polish Pope jokes, light bulb jokes, Auschwitz jokes, and others. Dundes' thesis is that sick humor cycles emerge as socially sanctioned outlets for anxiety about taboo subjects, and that the timing and content of joke cycles can be read as cultural diagnostics. His method combines morphological identification of recurring structures with psychoanalytic and socio-cultural interpretation.

**Knock-knock jokes specifically:** No evidence found that *Cracking Jokes* addresses knock-knock jokes. The joke cycles analyzed are all transgressive content cycles. Knock-knock jokes are a structurally distinct category (punning format rather than transgressive content). **NEGATIVE FINDING.**

## PART 5: LINGUISTICS SCHOLARSHIP ON KNOCK-KNOCK JOKE STRUCTURE

**Citation:** [Author name not retrieved]. "The Linguistic Mechanisms of Knock-Knock Jokes." Honors thesis, University of Texas at Arlington, Fall 2014.
**URL:** https://mavmatrix.uta.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1007&context=honors_fall2014

## PART 7: ELLIOTT ORING — HUMOR THEORY

### Source 7.1
**Citation:** Oring, Elliott. *Jokes and Their Relations.* Lexington: University Press of Kentucky, 1992. Reprinted by Transaction Publishers / Routledge, 2010.
**URL:** https://www.routledge.com/Jokes-and-Their-Relations/Oring/p/book/9781412814393
**Paywalled:** Yes — commercially published; no free digital edition confirmed.
**Primary or secondary:** Primary source.

**What it says:** Oring's central theoretical contribution is "appropriate incongruity" — jokes work by simultaneously presenting two conceptual domains that seem incongruous but are recognized as appropriately related. He applies this to children's riddles: a riddle question presents a logical contradiction; the answer makes the incongruity appropriate through a pun or ambiguity. This framework applies directly to knock-knock jokes (the name in the setup signifies one thing; the punchline reveals a homophone or pun that makes the incongruity appropriate). No confirmation that Oring addresses knock-knock jokes by name.

## PART 8: HISTORY AND JOURNALISM SOURCES

### Source 8.1
**Citation:** "The Secret History of Knock-Knock Jokes." *NPR History Dept.* March 3, 2015.
**URL:** https://www.npr.org/sections/npr-history-dept/2015/03/03/389865887/the-secret-history-of-knock-knock-jokes
**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible.
**Primary or secondary:** Secondary source (journalism).

**What it says:** Traces the knock-knock joke's emergence in the United States in the 1930s, the 1936 craze, the precursor "Do You Know" joke format (c. 1900), and the Shakespeare/Macbeth claims. Describes the format as entering popular culture through radio, newspaper columns, and contests. No academic citations given.

### Source 8.2
**Citation:** "Knock Knock! Who's There? Metafolklore, Jokes, and Shakespeare." *Folklife Today* (Library of Congress blog). April 2024.
**URL:** https://blogs.loc.gov/folklife/2024/04/knock-knock-whos-there-metafolklore-jokes-and-shakespeare/
**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible.
**Primary or secondary:** Secondary source (written by Library of Congress folklife staff; semi-scholarly).

**What it says:** Addresses the Shakespeare origin claim as an example of "metafolklore" — a traditional story told about a joke's origins. Notes that both Shakespeare and Jonson drew on existing folk dialogue routines. Uses the term metafolklore in its technical sense. This is the most analytically precise freely available source found that treats knock-knock jokes as a folklore phenomenon requiring scholarly framing.

## CONSOLIDATED NEGATIVE FINDINGS

The following specific searches were conducted and returned no relevant scholarly results:

1. **No article specifically about knock-knock jokes** was found in the *Journal of American Folklore* through any web search conducted. JSTOR direct search was blocked (403 error) — absence cannot be fully confirmed without authenticated JSTOR access.

2. **No article specifically about knock-knock jokes** was found in *Western Folklore* through any web search. Same JSTOR access limitation applies.

3. **Dundes never addressed knock-knock jokes** in any source found. His joke scholarship focuses on transgressive content cycles (dead baby jokes, ethnic slur cycles, AIDS jokes) — not structural punning formats like the knock-knock.

4. **The Opies do not appear to have written about knock-knock jokes by name** in any of their major works. Their British survey (1950s–1960s) preceded the widespread adoption of the knock-knock format in the UK. No snippet, review, or secondary commentary found that quotes or mentions knock-knock jokes in the Opie corpus.

5. **"The Singing Game" (Opie 1985) is irrelevant** to knock-knock jokes — its scope is restricted to musical movement games.

6. **No peer-reviewed article specifically analyzing knock-knock jokes as a folklore phenomenon** was found in any academic database accessible during this session. The only knock-knock-specific analytical document found is the 2014 UTA undergraduate honors thesis (Source 5.1), which is linguistics-focused and not peer-reviewed.

7. **Google Scholar searches** for combinations of "knock-knock jokes folklore," "knock-knock jokes oral tradition," and "children joke transmission peer" did not surface any dedicated peer-reviewed folklore journal article on the topic.

8. **No work by Dundes** applies his morphological framework specifically to the knock-knock joke. The closest adjacent work is his 1963 riddle article (Source 2.2), which addresses the structural genre from which knock-knock jokes descend.
