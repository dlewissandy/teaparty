### SOURCE 1

**Citation:** Weide, Jared. "The Linguistic Mechanisms of Knock-Knock Jokes." Honors College Thesis, University of Texas at Arlington, Fall 2014.

**URL:** https://mavmatrix.uta.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1007&context=honors_fall2014
**Repository page:** https://mavmatrix.uta.edu/honors_fall2014/8/

**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible via UTA's MavMatrix institutional repository.

**Primary or Secondary:** Primary (original research).

**Description:** The most directly focused academic work on knock-knock jokes as a genre found in the search. Weide compiled a corpus of 132 knock-knock jokes drawn from seven online sources and built a taxonomy of the linguistic mechanisms that drive their humor. Key finding: the pun or alternate meaning at the heart of these jokes almost always involves more than one type of linguistic mechanism, with phonological change (e.g., phonological substitution, elision, addition) being the most common, frequently overlapping with structural reanalysis of the punchline. The paper opens by noting that humor analysts had not yet accounted for the longstanding popularity of knock-knock jokes — an explicit acknowledgment of the scholarly gap. This is an undergraduate honors thesis, not a peer-reviewed journal article.

### SOURCE 2

**Citation:** Summerfelt, Hannah, Louis Lippman, and Ira E. Hyman Jr. "The Effect of Humor on Memory: Constrained by the Pun." *Journal of General Psychology* 137, no. 4 (2010): 376–394.

**DOI:** 10.1080/00221309.2010.499398
**PubMed PMID:** 21086859
**PubMed URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21086859/
**Publisher page (paywalled):** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00221309.2010.499398

**Paywalled:** Yes — Taylor & Francis journal; abstract visible on PubMed, full text requires institutional access.

**Primary or Secondary:** Primary (empirical psychology experiment).

**Description:** Knock-knock jokes are the primary experimental stimulus throughout this four-experiment study. Participants were exposed to jokes either in original pun-retaining form or in a modified pun-removed form. Results: pun humor improved both recall and recognition memory under incidental encoding conditions; rehearsal was ruled out as the cause; puns constrain the information that can fit in the punchline and thereby make recall easier. The paper treats knock-knock jokes as a structurally clean, ecologically valid vehicle for studying pun humor, not as a cultural or literary object. It is the most rigorous empirical study found that uses them as its central stimulus.

### SOURCE 3

**Citation:** Schmidt, R. C., Lin Nie, Alison Franco, and Michael J. Richardson. "Bodily Synchronization Underlying Joke Telling." *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* 8 (2014): Article 633.

**DOI:** 10.3389/fnhum.2014.00633
**PMC:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4133755/
**PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25177287/

**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible via PubMed Central.

**Primary or Secondary:** Primary (empirical experimental study).

**Description:** Knock-knock jokes are the primary experimental task. Sixteen participant pairs enacted ten knock-knock jokes while body movements were recorded. The paper analyzes interpersonal bodily coordination across nested temporal scales: the whole joke, the setup vs. punchline halves, the two-person exchange, and the individual utterance. Key finding: greater-than-chance entrainment of movements occurred at all time scales; the teller's movements led the responder's at longer scales; visual contact strengthened synchronization; explicit gesturing reduced it at shorter scales. The paper's Figure 1 explicitly diagrams knock-knock jokes as having an inherent 8-beat rhythmic structure (setup = 4 beats, punchline = 4 beats). Knock-knock jokes are used because their rigid call-and-response format provides a controlled, ecologically valid interaction structure.

### SOURCE 4

**Citation:** Rayz, Julia, and Lawrence Mazlack. "Computationally Recognizing Wordplay in Jokes." In *Proceedings of the Annual Meeting of the Cognitive Science Society*, 2006.

**Semantic Scholar:** https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Computationally-Recognizing-Wordplay-in-Jokes-Rayz-Mazlack/618f5ec452da1586f27c8743170e9bff8a0ac772
**ResearchGate:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229000046_Computationally_recognizing_wordplay_in_jokes

**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible via Semantic Scholar and ResearchGate PDF.

**Primary or Secondary:** Primary (computational linguistics / AI research).

**Description:** Knock-knock jokes are treated as the primary restricted domain for testing a computational wordplay-recognition system. The authors chose knock-knock jokes specifically because they have a well-defined formal structure and a predictable location for wordplay (the punchline), making them suitable for algorithmic analysis. The system uses Raskin's Script-Based Semantic Theory of Humor as its theoretical foundation and N-gram statistical models to detect where wordplay occurs. Frequently cited as a foundational reference in the computational humor literature. Treats knock-knock jokes as a genre with identifiable structural and semantic properties.

### SOURCE 5

**Citation:** Lestari et al. "How Do These Knock-Knock Jokes Work? A Pragmatic Analysis of Humor." *Journal of Language and Literature* (Universitas Sanata Dharma, Indonesia), 2024 or 2025.

**Publisher page:** https://e-journal.usd.ac.id/index.php/JOLL/article/view/11860
**ResearchGate:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396914743_How_Do_These_Knock-Knock_Jokes_Work_A_Pragmatic_Analysis_of_Humor

**Paywalled:** Likely open access (Indonesian university journal), but the publisher page was inaccessible during fetching (connection refused). Full text may be freely available.

**Primary or Secondary:** Primary (linguistic analysis).

**Description:** Knock-knock jokes are the explicit and sole subject. Applies two theoretical frameworks: Raskin's Script-Based Semantic Theory of Humor (SSTH) and Grice's Cooperative Principle / maxim theory. Data source appears to be knock-knock joke tweets from an Indonesian English-language social media account (@englishfess_). Key findings: the predominant humor type is incongruity; the most flouted maxim is the maxim of relation; cultural context shapes how the jokes function. A non-Western applied linguistics study treating knock-knock jokes as a recognizable cross-cultural genre.

**Caveat:** Full citation details (exact year, volume, issue, page numbers, author first names) could not be confirmed because the publisher page was inaccessible during fetching.

### SOURCE 6

**Citation:** Winick, Stephen. "Knock Knock! Who's There? Metafolklore, Jokes, and Shakespeare." *Folklife Today* (blog of the American Folklife Center, Library of Congress), April 20, 2024.

**URL:** https://blogs.loc.gov/folklife/2024/04/knock-knock-whos-there-metafolklore-jokes-and-shakespeare/

**Paywalled:** No — freely accessible.

**Primary or Secondary:** Secondary (folklorist essay / institutional blog post; not peer-reviewed).

**Description:** Written by a folklorist at the Library of Congress American Folklife Center. Winick challenges the popular attribution of knock-knock jokes to Shakespeare's porter scene in *Macbeth* (Act II, Scene 3), arguing it lacks the two-speaker interactive structure and punchline essential to the genre. He proposes an earlier and structurally closer analogue in Ben Jonson's *The Case is Altered* (c. 1597–98). Introduces the concept of "metafolklore" to analyze the Shakespeare origin myth as a cultural creation narrative. The most substantive genre-level treatment of knock-knock jokes as a folkloric form found in the search, but it is not peer-reviewed.

## PART 3: OVERALL ASSESSMENT OF SCHOLARLY DENSITY

The aggregate finding is a clear and notable scholarly gap.

Knock-knock jokes as a distinct genre have received:

- **One undergraduate honors thesis** treating them as a primary subject (Weide 2014, UTA).
- **One peer-reviewed empirical psychology paper** using them as a primary experimental stimulus (Summerfelt et al. 2010, *Journal of General Psychology*).
- **One peer-reviewed neuroscience/kinesiology paper** using them as a primary experimental task (Schmidt et al. 2014, *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*).
- **One peer-reviewed computational linguistics paper** using them as the primary test domain (Rayz & Mazlack 2006, Cognitive Science Society proceedings).
- **One applied linguistics paper** in a lower-prestige venue treating them as the explicit subject of pragmatic analysis (Lestari et al., Indonesian university journal, 2024/2025).
- **No peer-reviewed paper** in any linguistics, folklore, cultural history, or humor studies journal that treats knock-knock jokes as a genre worthy of sustained literary, folkloric, or cultural analysis.

The genre has attracted computational and experimental researchers who value its rigid formal structure as a methodological convenience — it provides a controlled, parseable, ecologically valid interaction format. It has attracted almost no interpretive or humanistic scholarly attention. The absence of dedicated treatment in *Humor: International Journal of Humor Research* is the sharpest single indicator of the gap.

The knock-knock joke is simultaneously one of the most structurally distinctive and culturally ubiquitous joke forms in English and one of the least studied as a genre in its own right.
