# Analysis Framework

How to analyze papers in relation to the research topic. Use this framework when filling in the `## Relevance` section of each paper's markdown file and when writing synthesis documents.

---

## The Four Lenses

For each paper, evaluate through four lenses:

### 1. Alignment

How does this paper support or validate the research topic?

- Does it provide evidence for claims the research makes?
- Does it use similar methods or approaches?
- Does it reach conclusions consistent with the research direction?
- Does it solve a subproblem the research depends on?

**Template**: "This paper aligns with [research topic] because [specific connection]. In particular, [finding/method] directly supports [specific aspect of research]."

### 2. Differences

Where does this paper challenge, contradict, or take a fundamentally different approach?

- Does it present evidence against claims the research makes?
- Does it use an opposing methodology or framework?
- Does it define key terms differently?
- Does it solve the same problem but reach different conclusions?

Differences are valuable — they sharpen the research question and identify where the contribution is most needed.

**Template**: "This paper differs from [research topic] in [specific way]. It argues [position], which challenges [specific aspect]. This difference matters because [implication]."

### 3. Extensions

What ideas from this paper could extend the research in new directions?

- Could a method from this paper be applied to the research problem?
- Does it suggest a generalization the research hasn't considered?
- Does it point to an application domain the research could serve?
- Does it offer a theoretical framework that could organize the research differently?

**Template**: "This paper suggests extending [research topic] by [specific direction]. The idea of [concept/method] could be applied to [specific aspect] because [reasoning]."

### 4. Follow-ups

What specific next steps does this paper suggest?

- Which of its references should we read?
- What experiments or analyses would test its claims against the research?
- What open questions does it raise that the research could address?
- Are there authors whose other work might be relevant?

**Template**: "Follow up: [specific action]. Reason: [why this matters for the research]."

## Synthesis Patterns

When combining analyses across papers for the synthesis document:

### Thematic Clustering
Group papers that address the same aspect of the research question. Name the theme, list the papers, and summarize the cluster's collective contribution.

### Consensus vs. Debate
Identify where the literature agrees (consensus) and where it disagrees (active debate). Consensus findings are load-bearing — the research can build on them. Debates are opportunities — the research can contribute to resolving them.

### Evidence Strength
Rate the strength of evidence:
- **Strong**: Multiple peer-reviewed studies with consistent findings, reproduced results
- **Moderate**: Single well-designed study, or multiple studies with some inconsistency
- **Emerging**: Preprints, workshop papers, single studies with small N
- **Anecdotal**: Blog posts, practitioner reports, Reddit discussions

### Gap Identification
Gaps are where the literature is silent. They are the most valuable finding because they define the research contribution. Types of gaps:
- **Empirical gap**: No one has tested this specific combination
- **Methodological gap**: Existing methods don't address this aspect
- **Theoretical gap**: No framework explains this phenomenon
- **Application gap**: Known techniques haven't been applied to this domain

---

## Quality Checklist for Paper Analysis

Before marking a paper as deeply-read:

- [ ] Notes capture the paper's actual contribution, not just its abstract
- [ ] At least one lens (alignment/differences/extensions/follow-ups) has substantive content
- [ ] Specific findings, numbers, or methods are cited — not just vague descriptions
- [ ] The analysis connects back to the research question explicitly
- [ ] If the paper is highly relevant, at least 2-3 follow-up actions are identified
