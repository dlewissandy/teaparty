# Paper Structures

Reference guide for section-level structure by paper type. Load this when choosing or drafting a structure.

---

## IMRAD (Empirical / Experimental Papers)

The standard for original research reporting results of experiments, studies, or data analysis.

### Introduction
- **Job**: Establish the problem, review relevant prior work, state the gap, declare your contribution.
- **Shape**: Funnel — broad context narrows to your specific question.
- **Common pitfalls**: Too much background (this isn't a review paper), burying the research question, not stating the contribution explicitly.
- **Ends with**: A clear statement of what this paper does (objective, hypothesis, or research question).

### Methods
- **Job**: Describe what you did so someone could reproduce it.
- **Includes**: Study design, participants/data sources, procedures, measures/instruments, analysis approach.
- **Tone**: Past tense, precise, concrete. "We recruited 120 participants" not "Participants were recruited."
- **Common pitfalls**: Omitting details that seem obvious to you, mixing methods with results, not specifying software versions or statistical tests.
- **Ethics**: State IRB/ethics approval, consent procedures, data handling.

### Results
- **Job**: Report what you found. Facts, not interpretation.
- **Includes**: Key findings with statistical details, tables, figures.
- **Tone**: Past tense, declarative. "Response times were significantly faster in condition A (M = 340ms, SD = 45) than condition B (M = 410ms, SD = 52), t(58) = 5.2, p < .001."
- **Common pitfalls**: Interpreting results (save for Discussion), cherry-picking, reporting too many decimal places, figures that duplicate tables.
- **Tables/Figures**: Each must be referenced in text and have a self-explanatory caption.

### Discussion
- **Job**: Interpret results, compare with prior work, acknowledge limitations, state implications.
- **Shape**: Mirror of Introduction — starts specific (your findings), widens to broader implications.
- **Includes**: Summary of key findings, comparison with prior work (agreement and disagreement), limitations (be honest), implications (theoretical and practical), future directions.
- **Common pitfalls**: Restating results without interpreting them, overclaiming, ignoring contradictory evidence, burying limitations in the last paragraph.

### Conclusion
- **Job**: Concise synthesis. What should the reader take away?
- **Length**: 1-2 paragraphs. Not a summary of the whole paper — a distillation.
- **Common pitfalls**: Introducing new information, repeating the abstract, vague generalities.

**Writing order**: Methods -> Results -> Introduction -> Discussion -> Conclusion -> Abstract -> Title

## Thematic (Review Articles / Surveys)

For synthesizing a body of literature around themes, not reporting new data.

### Introduction
- **Job**: Define the topic, state why a review is needed now, declare the scope and approach.
- **Includes**: What's covered, what's excluded, how literature was identified (for systematic reviews: full search strategy).

### Thematic Sections (3-6 typically)
- **Job**: Each section covers one theme, concept, or dimension of the topic.
- **Organize by ideas, not by paper.** Don't write "Smith (2020) found X. Jones (2021) found Y." Write "Two approaches dominate: X (Smith, 2020) and Y (Jones, 2021)."
- **Each section should**: Define the theme, synthesize findings across sources, identify consensus and disagreement, note gaps.

### Synthesis / Discussion
- **Job**: Pull themes together. What's the state of knowledge? Where are the gaps? What should researchers do next?
- **Includes**: Cross-cutting patterns, contradictions, methodological concerns, research agenda.

### Conclusion
- **Job**: Key takeaways and practical implications.

**Writing order**: Thematic sections -> Synthesis -> Introduction -> Conclusion -> Abstract -> Title

## Case Study / Case Report (CARE-Compliant)

For reporting individual cases with educational value. Follow the CARE (CAse REport) guidelines.

### Introduction
- **Job**: Why is this case worth reporting? What does it add to the literature?
- **Length**: Brief — 1-2 paragraphs.

### Case Presentation
- **Job**: Describe the case chronologically.
- **Includes**: Patient demographics, presenting complaint, history, examination findings, diagnostic workup, diagnosis, treatment, outcome, follow-up.
- **CARE timeline**: Consider a timeline figure showing key events.
- **Privacy**: De-identify thoroughly. State consent for publication.

### Discussion
- **Job**: Place this case in context. What does it teach?
- **Includes**: Comparison with similar cases in literature, clinical reasoning, what's novel, limitations of single-case inference.

**Writing order**: Case Presentation -> Discussion -> Introduction -> Abstract -> Title

## Position Paper / Commentary

For arguing a thesis, proposing a framework, or responding to published work.

### Introduction
- **Job**: State the issue and your position clearly, up front.
- **Don't bury the thesis.** The reader should know your argument by the end of paragraph one.

### Argument Sections
- **Job**: Build the case with evidence, reasoning, and examples.
- **Structure options**: By claim (claim 1 -> evidence, claim 2 -> evidence) or by lens (theoretical, empirical, practical).
- **Address counterarguments** explicitly. Steelman them, then respond.

### Implications
- **Job**: So what? What should change in practice, policy, or research?

### Conclusion
- **Job**: Restate the thesis and the strongest reason to accept it.

**Writing order**: Argument sections -> Implications -> Introduction -> Conclusion -> Abstract -> Title
