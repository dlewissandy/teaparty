**SOURCE 1**
**Citation:** Opie, Iona and Peter Opie. *The Lore and Language of Schoolchildren.* Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1959. Reissued: New York Review Books Classics, 2001.
**URL:** https://www.nyrb.com/products/the-lore-and-language-of-schoolchildren
**Archive (free):** https://archive.org/details/lorelanguageofsc0000iona
**Paywalled:** No (archive.org copy freely available; print editions purchasable)
**Primary or Secondary:** Primary (original ethnographic fieldwork)
**Description:** The foundational work on children's peer-transmitted folklore in Britain. The Opies surveyed approximately 5,000 children aged 6–14 in 70 schools across England, Scotland, and Wales, collecting directly from children rather than adult recall. Covers jokes, riddles, rhymes, chants, word play, sayings, and customs. The book documents how remarkably stable this "memetic ecosystem" is across generations without adult mediation. No confirmed specific chapter on knock-knock jokes found — that negative finding is noted below.

**URL:** https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED080213

## SECTION 2: DEVELOPMENTAL PSYCHOLOGY — HUMOR STAGES AND METALINGUISTIC AWARENESS

---

**SOURCE 6**
**Citation:** McGhee, Paul E. *Humor: Its Origin and Development.* San Francisco: W. H. Freeman, 1979.
**URL:** https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/646310.Humour
**Paywalled:** Book (no free digital version located)
**Primary or Secondary:** Primary
**Description:** The foundational developmental psychology text on children's humor. McGhee builds on Piagetian theory to propose four stages of humor development: (1) incongruous actions toward objects; (2) incongruous labeling of objects and events; (3) conceptual incongruity; (4) humor in multiple meanings. Stage 4 — comprehension of double meanings — is what makes puns, riddles, and knock-knock jokes work. McGhee places the onset of Stage 4 humor at approximately ages 6–7, coinciding with Piaget's concrete operational stage. He argues children resolve joke incongruity through "fantasy assimilation" distinct from ordinary learning. No direct confirmation found that McGhee writes specifically about knock-knock jokes by name in this volume.

**SOURCE 9**
**Citation:** Jackson, M. L., Nuñez, R. M., Maraach, D., Wilhite, C. J., and Moschella, J. D. "Teaching Comprehension of Double-Meaning Jokes to Young Children." *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis*, vol. 54, no. 3, 2021, pp. 1095–1110. https://doi.org/10.1002/jaba.838

Highly relevant.

## SECTION 3: LINGUISTIC ANALYSIS OF KNOCK-KNOCK JOKES

---

**SOURCE 15**
**Citation:** Weide, Jared. "The Linguistic Mechanisms of Knock-Knock Jokes." Honors Capstone Project, University of Texas at Arlington, Fall 2014.
**URL:** https://mavmatrix.uta.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1007&context=honors_fall2014
**Paywalled:** No (open institutional repository)
**Primary or Secondary:** Primary (original taxonomy, though undergraduate thesis)
**Description:** The only dedicated scholarly treatment of knock-knock joke structure found in this research. Weide compiled 132 knock-knock jokes from seven online sources and created a taxonomy of linguistic mechanisms. Key finding: "the pun or alternate meaning at the heart of these jokes almost always involves more than one type of linguistic mechanism," with phonological change and structural reanalysis being the most common. Argues knock-knock jokes deserve serious linguistic study for applications in education and computational humor. Not a developmental or folkloristic study — does not address children's acquisition or peer transmission. Undergraduate thesis; treat as primary source with appropriate weight.

## NEGATIVE FINDINGS — EXPLICIT RECORD

The following questions were researched and returned no usable positive results:

1. **Does Knapp and Knapp (1976) mention knock-knock jokes specifically?** Not confirmed. Archive.org page returned only catalog metadata; full text was not accessible during this session. Specific knock-knock coverage cannot be verified without library access.

2. **Does Sutton-Smith's major works specifically discuss knock-knock jokes?** Not confirmed. His riddle paper (1973) addresses riddle structure broadly; no secondary source reviewed in this session cited him on knock-knock jokes by name.

3. **Does the Opies' *Lore and Language of Schoolchildren* (1959) mention knock-knock jokes?** Not confirmed. The book was published in 1959; knock-knock jokes were current in British and American popular culture by then (having spread from the U.S. via radio in the 1930s), but specific coverage was not confirmed in any source reviewed.

4. **Does Labov address knock-knock jokes or joke-telling (as distinct from ritual insults)?** No. His chapter concerns sounding/signifying — competitive insult exchange — not joke formats. The connection is structural (peer verbal performance as socialization) not topical.

5. **Does Wolfenstein (1954) cover knock-knock jokes?** Not confirmed. Her book was published as knock-knock jokes were gaining their child-culture association, but no secondary description of the book mentions them specifically.

6. **Is there a dedicated peer-reviewed scholarly study specifically of knock-knock jokes and child development?** Not found. The Jackson et al. (2021) paper in *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis* comes closest, mentioning knock-knock jokes by name in a developmental context, but as an aside rather than the study's focus. Weide (2014) is the only dedicated knock-knock study, but it is a linguistics undergraduate thesis, not developmental psychology.

7. **Is there scholarship linking knock-knock joke transmission specifically to power dynamics or identity formation among peers?** Not found as a specific study. The general humor-socialization literature addresses humor and peer status/social competence without knock-knock-specific analysis.

8. **Was Brian Sutton-Smith's work on verbal humor and jokes accessible in full text?** Partially. His 1973 riddle paper is freely available via ERIC. His books are not freely digitized beyond archive.org borrow.
